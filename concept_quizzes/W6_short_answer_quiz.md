## Questions

### Q1

**Type:** Multiple choice

**Learner-facing question:** Which statement best captures the Week 6 problem with postdiction?

A. It always produces mathematically impossible models  
B. It lets the analyst reshape the explanation after seeing the data, which weakens the credibility of the validation claim  
C. It only happens in biology experiments  
D. It means the experiment should never be repeated

**Expert response:** B is the best answer. Week 6 frames postdiction as structurally too close to curve fitting after the fact. The problem is not that it is impossible to write a plausible explanation, but that the explanation is no longer a committed prediction.

### Q2

**Type:** Multiple choice

**Learner-facing question:** In Tier 2, why does intended use matter before the experiment runs?

A. It tells you what color to make the plots  
B. It defines what counts as a meaningful prediction and what level of agreement matters for the experiment’s purpose  
C. It replaces the need for a model  
D. It guarantees the calibration is current

**Expert response:** B is the best answer. Intended use determines what response features matter, how strict the acceptance criterion should be, and what kind of comparison is actually useful.

### Q3

**Type:** Multiple choice

**Learner-facing question:** Which item belongs in a defensible pre-lab prediction document?

A. A rewritten explanation after seeing the data  
B. A clear acceptance criterion stated before data collection  
C. A promise to adjust the model until it matches the results  
D. A list of everyone’s favorite plotting styles

**Expert response:** B is the best answer. Week 6 emphasizes a prediction package that includes committed response features, theoretical grounding, uncertainty, acceptance criterion, and risk assessment before hardware access.

### Q4

**Type:** Open response

**Learner-facing question:** Explain why “the result should look reasonable” is not an adequate Week 6 prediction.

**Expert response:** A strong response says that the statement is too vague to be falsifiable or analytically useful. It does not specify what quantity, what magnitude, what pattern, or what bound would count as agreement. Week 6 requires committed features and criteria, not general confidence language.

### Q5

**Type:** Open response

**Learner-facing question:** What is the value of naming an anomalous data pattern before the experiment runs?

**Expert response:** A strong response explains that it prepares the group to recognize model-form limitations or setup problems in real time rather than rationalizing them afterward. It turns “something looks odd” into a pre-identified diagnostic signal. That is part of being prepared to run the experiment, not just analyze it later.
