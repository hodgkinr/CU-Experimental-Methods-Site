The Exploratory Example — A Complete Prediction-to-Outcome Walkthrough

The instructor walks through a complete prediction-to-outcome comparison for an experiment from the Tier 1 pool that no student in the room ran as their Phase 1. This is not a polished demonstration — it is a transparent modeling exercise, done in real time, with the instructor narrating every decision. The sequence mirrors exactly what students must do in Phase 2 and Phase 3: read the theory primer and sensor specs → identify the primary output and its expected range → apply partial derivative propagation to estimate uncertainty → sketch the expected time-series or distribution with committed bounds → define an acceptance criterion → then reveal the actual data and compare. The comparison includes explicit narration of what the discrepancy reveals: is the gap within the propagated uncertainty band (consistent with measurement noise), or does the residual show a systematic trend (evidence of a model-form limitation)? The instructor names which category applies and explains the physical interpretation. **Bridge to E2:** This lecture closes E1 by making explicit what E2 will demand. In E1, students ran their own experiments and separately predicted someone else's. In E2, prediction before exposure is not an exercise — it is the required gate before any hardware access. The canned experiments in E2 are more complex, the models have more parameters, and the consequences of a vague pre-commitment are more visible. What students practiced in Tier 1 Phase 2 is the same skill, now applied under higher stakes. The instructor should close this lecture — and E1 — with the question that opens E2: *what would you need to know, before touching any hardware, to make a prediction you could defend?*

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the five elements that make a prediction defensible — system response features, propagated uncertainty, a sketch with committed bounds, a ranked uncertainty source list, and a named acceptance criterion — and produce all five for an unfamiliar experiment — CLO 1, CLO 2, CLO 3
  • Distinguish between a vague prediction ("it will go up") and a committed prediction ("I expect the measured value to reach X ± Y by time T, because of physical reasoning Z") and articulate why the distinction matters for engineering credibility — CLO 3
  • Construct a predictive model with committed system response features and defined acceptance criterion for an experiment they did not run — CLO 1, CLO 3
  • Identify dominant uncertainty sources in an unfamiliar experimental setup and incorporate them into a prediction memo — CLO 2

    slide 3: Step 1 — Extract the Governing Equation and System Response Features (Image: A derivation slide. The governing equation: "I·θ̈ + κ·θ = 0," leading to the natural frequency expression: "ωₙ = √(κ/I)" and the period "T = 2π√(I/κ)." Below: a table showing the values from the theory primer: "I = 0.0248 kg·m² (disk geometry, measured with calipers and a scale)," "κ = 0.0315 N·m/rad (from wire calibration data in the packet)." Calculation: "T = 2π√(0.0248/0.0315) = 2π × 0.887 = 5.57 s." A gold box: "System Response Feature: The torsional pendulum period is predicted to be 5.57 s. The oscillation amplitude should decay due to air damping — but the model assumes undamped oscillation. Flag this assumption explicitly.") Talking Point: Step one: read the theory primer and extract the governing equation. For a torsional pendulum, the equation of motion is I times theta double-dot plus kappa times theta equals zero, giving a natural frequency of the square root of kappa over I. With the values from the primer — moment of inertia 0.0248 kilogram-meter-squared, torsional stiffness 0.0315 Newton-meters per radian — the predicted period is 5.57 seconds. That is the system response feature I am committing to. I also note immediately that the model assumes undamped oscillation — there is no damping term. The actual disk oscillates in air, which has some drag. Air damping will cause amplitude decay but minimally affects the period for light damping (ζ ≪ 1). The period deviation I observe later is a different effect — amplitude-dependent nonlinearity. For now, my undamped period prediction stands, and I flag the amplitude decay as an expected but unmodeled feature.

    slide 4: Step 2 — Propagate Uncertainty from the Spec Sheet (Image: A partial derivative propagation table. Column headers: "Parameter," "Value," "Partial Derivative ∂T/∂(param)," "Uncertainty δ(param)," "Contribution |∂T/∂(param) × δ(param)|." Rows: "I = 0.0248 kg·m²" — partial derivative = π/√(κI) — δI = ±5% from geometry measurement — contribution = ±0.14 s. "κ = 0.0315 N·m/rad" — partial derivative = −π√(I)/κ^(3/2) — δκ = ±8% from wire calibration uncertainty — contribution = ±0.22 s. "Combined uncertainty (quadrature sum): δT = √(0.14² + 0.22²) = ±0.26 s." A gold box: "Predicted period: 5.57 ± 0.26 s (95% CI, partial derivative method). Dominant source: torsional stiffness κ, contributing 72% of total output variance.") Talking Point: Step two: propagate the uncertainty from the spec sheet values. I take the partial derivative of the period with respect to I and with respect to kappa. The stiffness kappa has larger uncertainty than the moment of inertia — the wire calibration in the packet carries 8% uncertainty, while the geometry measurement for I carries about 5%. Evaluating the propagation, the kappa term dominates: it contributes 72% of the total output variance. The combined uncertainty in the predicted period is plus or minus 0.26 seconds at 95% confidence. So my prediction is: the period is 5.57 plus or minus 0.26 seconds. That is a specific, defensible claim derived from the spec sheet — without having seen the angular encoder measure a single oscillation.

Talking Point: Step three: draw the committed sketch. Here is the expected oscillation — sinusoidal, period 5.57 seconds, amplitude starting near the initial displacement and decaying slowly. I have drawn the amplitude envelope with a slight decay because I know air drag will be present even though the model ignores it. The shaded region at the zero crossings represents the uncertainty in the period — if the measured zero crossings are within the shaded band, the period is consistent with the prediction. Notice that I have committed to the shape, the period, the approximate initial amplitude, and the expected slow decay — all before seeing a single data point. This is the sketch that will go into my Phase 2 memo, and it is the first thing I will overlay the data on when it comes in.

    slide 6: Step 4 — Acceptance Criterion and Risk Assessment (Image: Two clearly separated text boxes. Box 1 — "Acceptance Criterion": "The prediction is successful if the measured oscillation period is within 5.57 ± 0.26 s (the propagated uncertainty interval at 95% CI). A measured period outside this interval requires a physical explanation — either an unaccounted-for uncertainty source or a model-form limitation." Box 2 — "Risk Assessment — Two Anomalous Signatures to Watch": "(1) If the period decreases significantly with amplitude (nonlinearity), the small-angle approximation in the model has failed. The linear model is invalid at large angular displacements. (2) If the period is significantly shorter than predicted — less than 5.2 s — the torsional stiffness in the spec may be miscalibrated, or the wire has hardened since the calibration was performed." Both boxes use consistent formatting. Caption: "Commit to what wrong looks like before you see the data.") Talking Point: Step four: state the acceptance criterion and the risk assessment. The acceptance criterion is numerical and pre-committed — if the measured period is within 5.57 plus or minus 0.26 seconds, I will classify the comparison as supporting agreement. If it falls outside, I need a physical explanation. The risk assessment names two specific anomalous patterns I should watch for. First: if the measured period decreases with amplitude — if early oscillations are shorter than later ones as the amplitude decays — the small-angle approximation in the linearized model has failed. The linear model is only valid for small angular displacements, and this failure is predictable in advance. Second: if the measured period is significantly shorter than predicted — say, below 5.2 seconds — the torsional stiffness in the packet may be wrong, or the wire properties have changed since calibration. Both anomalous signatures are physically motivated, and both are commitable before the data is released.

The measured period, averaged over eight complete oscillations using zero-crossing analysis, is 5.83 seconds. My prediction was 5.57 plus or minus 0.26 seconds. Using my pre-committed criterion — agreement if the measured period is within the uncertainty interval — the answer is marginal. 5.83 is exactly at the boundary. I need to apply the validation metric carefully. The relative difference is (5.83 minus 5.57) divided by 5.57 — about 4.7%. The combined uncertainty is 0.26 s, which is 4.7% of the model prediction. So the difference is just barely within the combined uncertainty. I will classify this as marginal agreement — but I should investigate the discrepancy rather than simply accepting agreement.

    slide 8: What Does the Discrepancy Tell Us? (Image: A residual analysis plot. X-axis: "Oscillation Number" from 1 to 8. Y-axis: "Period (s) for each oscillation." The individual measured periods are plotted as dots. A horizontal dashed line marks the predicted value of 5.57 s. All measured periods are above the prediction line, and there is a slight trend: earlier oscillations (when amplitude is large) have slightly longer periods — around 5.90 s — while later oscillations (when amplitude is small) converge toward 5.65 s. A gold annotation: "Systematic trend: period decreases as amplitude decreases — consistent with nonlinear stiffness at large displacement." A second annotation: "Residuals are NOT random. The small-angle approximation fails at large initial displacement — this was the first risk assessment item.") Talking Point: Look at the residuals. The measured period is not just slightly high — it shows a systematic trend. The first few oscillations, when the amplitude is large, have longer periods. The later oscillations, as amplitude decays, have shorter periods approaching the linear model prediction. That is not random scatter. That is the small-angle approximation failing at large displacement — exactly the first risk assessment item I committed to before seeing the data. The linear model predicts a constant period regardless of amplitude. The physical system shows amplitude-dependent period because the linearization I·θ-double-dot plus kappa·theta is only valid for small theta. The linear torsional pendulum model uses the approximation sin(θ) ≈ θ for small angles. At large angular amplitudes, the exact sin(θ) restoring force is weaker than the linear approximation, reducing the effective stiffness and increasing the period. At large theta, the nonlinear sine term in the full restoring force equation matters. The anomalous signature I predicted is the one I see. This is what it looks like when a model-form failure is predicted in advance and then confirmed: you learn something specific about the physics of the system, not just that the model was approximately right.
