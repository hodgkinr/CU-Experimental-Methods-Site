Calibration Revisited — Formal Procedure & Calibration Status Review

Revisits calibration at a depth beyond E1 Week 3's conceptual introduction. Covers calibration as a formal procedure with its own uncertainty budget: input reference uncertainty, fit uncertainty (regression error), and residual uncertainty after correction. Students see that calibration uncertainty propagates into the final measurement outcome and must be documented in any credible experimental report. This lecture also introduces the calibration status review — checking whether instruments are within their calibration interval and what the practical consequence is if they are not. This maps directly to Section 3C of the Tier 2 group report. The calibration-vs-validation distinction from E1 Week 3 is deepened: calibrating an instrument can improve measurement fidelity, but if residuals from the calibration prompt the experimentalist to re-examine model assumptions, the process has crossed from calibration into model refinement — a fundamentally different activity. **Off-the-shelf sensor verification example:** Include a concrete worked example of verifying an off-the-shelf power sensor against first-principles measurement (P=IV using multimeters and Ohm's law). Students see that "verification" of a commercial sensor is distinct from calibrating it — you are checking whether the sensor's output is consistent with first principles, not adjusting it. This example connects to the Lab 1 P=IV work and illustrates the calibration-vs-validation distinction with a real commercial sensor scenario.

Learning Objectives

  • Interpret model-experiment discrepancy as diagnostic information about model-form limitations rather than experimental failure — CLO 3
  • Distinguish between parametric uncertainty (imprecise knowledge of model parameters), model-form error (structural inadequacy of the model), and measurement error — CLO 2, CLO 3
  • Inspect residuals structurally — identifying systematic trends vs. random scatter — and articulate what each pattern implies about model adequacy — CLO 3
  • Perform a calibration status review with reference to instrument documentation and distinguish calibration activities from validation activities — CLO 1, CLO 3

    slide 2: Calibration vs. Validation — The Distinction Deepened (Image: A side-by-side two-column comparison diagram. Left column header: "Calibration." Right column header: "Validation." Row 1 — "What it is": "Calibration: a measurement activity — comparing a sensor's output to a known reference and adjusting the relationship between input and output to reduce systematic bias." | "Validation: a modeling activity — testing whether a mathematical model makes accurate predictions of a physical system's behavior." Row 2 — "What it operates on": "Calibration: the instrument — changes the sensor's calibration coefficients or correction curve." | "Validation: the model — provides evidence about whether the model is adequate for the intended use." Row 3 — "What 'success' looks like": "Calibration: the sensor output agrees with the reference standard within the stated calibration uncertainty." | "Validation: the model prediction agrees with the experimental outcome within the acceptance criterion." Row 4 — "What a 'fail' means": "Calibration: the instrument needs adjustment or replacement." | "Validation: the model is inadequate for the intended use — revision required." The table is clean and readable with alternating row shading. A caption at the bottom: "Conflating these two activities is one of the most common sources of credibility loss in experimental reports.") Talking Point: In Week 3 of E1, I told you that calibration and validation are distinct activities and that conflating them undermines experimental credibility. Now let me explain precisely why. Calibration adjusts a sensor's output to match a known reference — it is an operation on the instrument. When you recalibrate a load cell, you are changing the relationship between force applied and voltage output to reduce systematic bias. Validation tests whether a mathematical model predicts a physical system's behavior accurately — it is an operation on the model. When you compare a structural model's predicted deflection to the measured deflection, you are testing the model. The two activities operate on different objects and serve different purposes. The common error is running an experiment, comparing the data to a model, finding agreement, and concluding the model is validated — when what actually happened is that the sensor was calibrated against the physical system, which is a measurement activity, not a modeling validation. If the same data used to calibrate the sensor is then used to validate the model, you have not performed an independent validation test. You have demonstrated that your sensor is internally consistent, and nothing more.

    slide 3: The Calibration Uncertainty Budget — What Calibration Adds to the Error Stack (Image: A stacked bar chart illustration showing the total measurement uncertainty budget as a stack of three components. Component 1 (bottom, gold): "Random measurement noise — estimated from repeated trials." Component 2 (middle, lighter gold): "Sensor systematic bias — corrected by calibration." Component 3 (top, orange): "Residual calibration uncertainty — what remains after calibration." Below the corrected measurement bar, a label reads: "Calibration reduces the systematic bias component but introduces its own uncertainty: the calibration uncertainty propagates into the corrected measurement." To the right, a simple equation: "u_total² = u_noise² + u_calibration²" where each term is labeled. A note below: "Calibration uncertainty comes from: (1) reference standard uncertainty, (2) curve-fit residuals, (3) environmental drift since last calibration." The visual communicates that calibration is not free — it trades one source of uncertainty for another, smaller one.) Talking Point: Every calibration has its own uncertainty, and that uncertainty propagates into your final measurement outcome. The calibration uncertainty has three components. First, the reference standard uncertainty — the known reference used to calibrate your sensor is itself not perfectly accurate; it has a stated uncertainty that transfers directly to your calibration. Second, the curve-fit residuals — when you fit a calibration curve to the reference-versus-sensor-output data, the fit is not perfect; the residual scatter around the calibration curve represents uncertainty in the corrected sensor output at any given input. Third, environmental drift since the last calibration — sensors change over time and with environmental conditions, so a sensor that was calibrated six months ago may have drifted since then, and the magnitude of that drift is uncertain. The total calibration uncertainty is the root-sum-square (RSS) of these three components — the same quadrature combination you first used in E1 Week 2 — and it adds in quadrature to the random measurement noise in your total uncertainty budget. Calibration makes measurements more accurate by reducing systematic bias, but it does not make measurements perfect — it trades a large systematic error for a smaller residual one, and that residual must be documented and propagated.

Talking Point: Let me show you a concrete example of sensor verification using first principles — this is related to the P=IV lab you did in Week 2 of E1, and I want you to see the connection explicitly. A commercial power sensor is attached to a resistive load. You want to verify that the sensor's output is physically consistent — not to recalibrate it, but to check whether it agrees with first principles using standard instruments. You use two multimeters: one measuring voltage, one measuring current. You compute power from P equals V times I, using the same partial-derivative uncertainty propagation you applied in Lab 1. The power sensor reads 12.4 watts. The first-principles computation gives 12.2 watts. The discrepancy is 0.2 watts. The propagated uncertainties overlap — 12.4 plus-or-minus 0.3 watts and 12.2 plus-or-minus 0.15 watts. This is a verification result: you are not adjusting the sensor's calibration, you are confirming that its output is consistent with an independent physical measurement within the combined uncertainty bounds. If the results did not overlap, you would have grounds to suspect a calibration issue and seek a formal recalibration before using the sensor for a validation claim. This is the calibration-versus-validation distinction applied to a commercial sensor in a real scenario.

Talking Point: This two-by-two matrix captures the practical relationship between calibration status and validation credibility. The strongest validation case is the top-left cell: the instruments are in calibration, the calibration uncertainty is documented, and the model agrees with the data within the uncertainty bounds. You can make a credible claim about model adequacy because the uncertainty is bounded and characterized. The bottom-left cell is the credible diagnostic case: the instruments are in calibration, but the model disagrees with the data. Because calibration is not the issue, the discrepancy is real and you can investigate the model confidently. The weakest cases are on the right side of the matrix. If the instrument is out of calibration and the model appears to agree, you cannot determine whether the agreement is genuine or coincidental — the uncalibrated sensor may be biased in a way that makes the model look correct by accident. And if the instrument is out of calibration and there is a discrepancy, you cannot determine whether the discrepancy is from the model or from the unmeasured calibration drift. The practical implication is clear: calibration status must be verified before you run the experiment, not discovered during the analysis.

    slide 7: Propagating Calibration Uncertainty into the Final Result (Image: A step-by-step calculation layout. Step 1: "Certificate expanded uncertainty: U_cal = 0.12 N, k=2." Step 2: "Convert to standard uncertainty: u_cal = U_cal/k = 0.06 N." Step 3: "Repeated-trial noise: u_noise = 0.08 N (standard uncertainty)." Step 4: "u_c = sqrt(0.06² + 0.08²) = 0.10 N." Step 5: "U = 2u_c = 0.20 N; F = 23.4 ± 0.20 N (expanded uncertainty, k=2), subject to the certificate's stated coverage basis." A note: "Noise is the larger standard component: 0.08 N > 0.06 N.") Talking Point: The certificate reports an expanded calibration uncertainty of 0.12 newtons with k equals two. We must first convert it to the same standard-uncertainty basis as the repeated-trial noise: u-cal equals 0.12 divided by two, or 0.06 newtons. The noise component is already a standard uncertainty of 0.08 newtons. Root-sum-square combination gives u-c equals the square root of 0.06 squared plus 0.08 squared, exactly 0.10 newtons. If a k equals two expanded result is appropriate under the certificate's coverage basis, U equals 0.20 newtons, so the result is 23.4 plus-or-minus 0.20 newtons. Noise, at 0.08 newtons, is the larger standard component; calibration, at 0.06 newtons, is not dominant.

    slide 11: When Residuals Prompt Recalibration — Crossing from Calibration into Model Refinement (Image: A flow diagram with a branch point. Starting node: "Run experiment, compute residuals." First branch: "Residuals random and within uncertainty band" → "Model form adequate — document and proceed." Second branch: "Residuals systematic — trend present" → "Diagnose model-form error." Third branch: "Residuals show constant bias beyond uncertainty band" → Branch point: "Is the instrument in calibration?" → Yes: "Bias is real — investigate model parameters or systematic setup error." → No: "Recalibrate — then re-examine residuals with updated uncertainty." A special arrow exits the "Recalibrate" step and points back to "Run experiment" labeled: "If recalibration substantially changes the predicted value, you may have just performed model refinement disguised as measurement correction — document this explicitly." A warning icon appears on the recalibration loop with the label: "Check: are you correcting the sensor or fitting the model?") Talking Point: There is a subtle but important boundary between calibrating an instrument and refining a model, and it is possible to cross it without realizing it. When you observe a constant bias in the residuals and the instrument is out of calibration, the right first action is to recalibrate. But here is the warning: if the recalibration substantially changes the sensor's calibration coefficients in a way that happens to reduce the bias in your validation comparison — and if you discovered this by looking at the residuals rather than by comparing the sensor to an independent standard — you may have just used the experimental data to adjust the model rather than to test it. That is postdiction at the calibration level. Here is what the failure mode looks like in practice: a group finds their load cell consistently over-reads relative to the model. They "recalibrate" by adjusting the sensitivity coefficient until the readings match the model prediction. This is not calibration — it is model fitting. The correct approach: recalibrate the load cell against an independent reference force standard, then re-examine whether the residual remains. The protection is to ensure that recalibration is done against an independent reference standard, not in response to the model comparison. If you recalibrate against a reference and the residuals still show a bias, the bias is real and the model needs attention. If the recalibration resolves the bias, you have corrected a measurement error — and you need to document that the recalibration was performed against an independent standard, not in response to the data comparison.

    slide 13: Closure Calibration is a formal procedure with its own uncertainty budget — reference standard uncertainty, curve-fit residuals, and environmental drift since the last calibration — and that uncertainty propagates into your final measurement outcome as a root-sum-square contribution alongside random noise. Calibration status review is not a bureaucratic checkbox: it determines whether your measurement system is adequate for the intended use of the experiment, and it shapes the credibility of every validation claim you make. An out-of-calibration instrument does not invalidate your work, but it must be documented honestly and its implications for the uncertainty budget must be assessed. The distinction between calibrating an instrument — correcting its output against an independent standard — and using experimental data to fit a model remains critical here: if you discover a calibration issue by examining the residuals, the recalibration must be against an independent reference, not against the data itself. In the lab session today, you will locate the calibration documentation for the instruments in your Tier 2 experiment, complete the calibration status review, and begin the Section 3C analysis. That documentation is your evidence that you ran the measurement system correctly — and evidence is what makes validation credible.
