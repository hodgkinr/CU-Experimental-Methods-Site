E1 Week 2 (Course Week 2) · Lecture

Error Propagation, Sensitivity Analysis & Monte Carlo

Introduces first-order partial derivative error propagation using electrical power as the working example: P = IV, P = V²/R, and P = I²R. Each equation is a different model for the same quantity, and each has different sensitivities to variation in the measured inputs. Students see how Gaussian vs. flat variance assumptions in input measurements produce different uncertainty estimates in the derived output. Monte Carlo simulation is introduced as a computational complement to the partial derivative method, with the key lesson that truth lies in the overlapping region of the distributions and that every result must carry an estimate plus its uncertainty. **Graph presentation framework (Kathryn's framework):** This lecture should include a dedicated segment on how to present a graph — a sequential checklist that students will apply to every MATLAB figure they produce in the course: (1) motivation — why does this graph exist, what question does it answer; (2) axes — what is plotted, with units, at appropriate scale; (3) data source — where did the data come from, how was it collected; (4) uncertainties — error bars or uncertainty bounds shown and labeled; (5) trend — what pattern does the data show; (6) model comparison — how does the trend compare to the predictive model; (7) discrepancy — what are possible causes for any gap between data and model; (8) system-level implication — what does this graph tell you about the overall system. This framework should also be provided as a required handout/reading in E1 W3, and students should be held to it in every subsequent deliverable.

    slide 1: Title Slide Title: Error Propagation, Sensitivity Analysis & Monte Carlo Talking Point: Last lecture gave you the conceptual vocabulary — variability, error, uncertainty, and why the three-way distinction matters. Now I want to make uncertainty analysis computational. The core challenge is this: in most experiments, the quantity you care about is not what you measure directly — it is something you calculate from multiple raw measurements, each carrying its own uncertainty. Force from voltage. Power from current and voltage. Velocity from position and time. When your inputs are uncertain, how does that uncertainty flow through the calculation into the output? That question is what error propagation answers. And once you can propagate uncertainty, you need to communicate it — which is why this lecture also covers how to present a graph that makes your uncertainty visible and your argument legible.

    slide 2: The Problem — Uncertainty in Derived Quantities (Image: A schematic flow diagram on a white background. On the left, three labeled input boxes: "V = 12.0 ± 0.3 V," "I = 2.5 ± 0.1 A," "R = 4.8 ± 0.2 Ω." Arrows from each input converge on a central gray function box labeled "P = f(V, I, R) — model determines form." Talking Point: Here is the situation you will face in almost every experiment in this course. You measure several quantities — voltage, current, resistance, angle, period — and you compute a derived quantity from them. Each measured input has uncertainty. Your derived output inherits uncertainty from all of your inputs, combined through the functional form of your equation. The question is how much, and which input contributes the most. This matters practically: if you know which input is the dominant source of uncertainty in your output, you know where to invest effort — better instrumentation, more measurements, tighter control — to reduce the total output uncertainty most efficiently. Error propagation is the tool that answers this question quantitatively.

    slide 3: First-Order Error Propagation — The Partial Derivative Method (Image: A derivation box on a clean white background. Header: "Derived quantity Q = f(x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ)." Below: the first-order propagation formula in large, clean type: "δQ = √[(∂f/∂x₁ · δx₁)² + (∂f/∂x₂ · δx₂)² + ...]." A labeled decomposition shows each term "(∂f/∂xᵢ · δxᵢ)" with two annotations: the ∂f/∂xᵢ term labeled "Sensitivity coefficient — how much Q changes per unit of xᵢ" and δxᵢ labeled "Input uncertainty." A callout box: "Assumptions: (1) Input errors are independent. (2) Errors are small relative to input values — first-order approximation is valid." The formula is readable from the back of a classroom.) Talking Point: The partial derivative method is the formal tool for propagating uncertainty through a function. The logic is: if Q depends on multiple uncertain inputs, then the uncertainty in Q is approximately equal to the square root of the sum of squares of each input's contribution — where each contribution is the sensitivity coefficient for that input multiplied by the uncertainty in that input. The sensitivity coefficient is the partial derivative of Q with respect to that input — it tells you how much the output changes per unit change in the input. A large sensitivity coefficient on a noisy input is the signature of a dominant error source. The method assumes independence between inputs and that errors are small enough for the first-order linear approximation to hold. Both assumptions are reasonable for most engineering scenarios, and we will see what happens when they break in more complex problems later in the course. To make this concrete: for P = V²/R, the partial derivative with respect to V is ∂P/∂V = 2V/R — which means a 1 V error in a 12 V reading with R = 4.8 Ω contributes (2×12/4.8)×δV = 5 × δV to the uncertainty in P. That factor of 5 is the sensitivity coefficient, and it depends entirely on the operating point.

    slide 4: The Power Trio — Same Quantity, Three Equation Forms (Image: Three separate boxed equations arranged horizontally on a clean white background. Box 1: "P = IV" — labeled "Form 1." Box 2: "P = V²/R" — labeled "Form 2." Box 3: "P = I²R" — labeled "Form 3." Below each box, a stacked bar chart shows the fractional uncertainty contribution from each input for representative values (V = 12 V ± 2%, I = 2.5 A ± 4%, R = 4.8 Ω ± 3%). The bars are color-coded by input: blue for V, orange for I, green for R. The relative heights of the colored segments differ dramatically across the three forms — Form 2 has V dominating, Form 3 has I dominating. Bold caption: "Same physical quantity. Different equation form. Different sensitivity to input errors.") Talking Point: I want to use electrical power as the working example because it makes something visible that is true of every derived quantity in experimental engineering: the same physical quantity can be expressed by multiple different equation forms, and the different forms have different sensitivities to your measurement errors. Power equals IV. Power equals V-squared over R. Power equals I-squared times R. All three are valid equations for electrical power. But if I propagate uncertainty through each one, I get different total output uncertainty — because the partial derivatives are different. In Form 2, voltage appears squared, so errors in voltage are amplified. In Form 3, current appears squared. If your voltmeter is less precise than your ammeter, these forms give you different total uncertainty in P. Choosing the equation form that minimizes sensitivity to your most uncertain input is a real engineering decision — not a cosmetic one.

    slide 5: Sensitivity Analysis — A Map for Where to Invest (Image: A decision matrix styled as a 2x2 quadrant diagram. X-axis labeled "Sensitivity Coefficient (partial derivative magnitude)" running left (low) to right (high). Y-axis labeled "Input Uncertainty δxᵢ" running bottom (low) to top (high). Four quadrant labels: upper-right "HIGH PRIORITY — big sensitivity, big uncertainty. Fix this first." Upper-left "Monitor — big sensitivity, small uncertainty. Good control — maintain it." Lower-right "Investigate — small sensitivity despite large uncertainty. May not matter." Lower-left "Don't worry — small sensitivity, small uncertainty." Three labeled dots show where V, I, and R fall for a specific P = IV example, with V in the upper-left and I in the upper-right. Caption: "This matrix is a design tool — use it before you run the experiment, not only after.") Talking Point: The sensitivity breakdown from error propagation is not just a reporting exercise — it is a design tool. The most dangerous inputs to your derived quantity are the ones that combine high sensitivity — large partial derivative — with high uncertainty — large δx. Those are the inputs in the upper right of this matrix, and those are where you should focus your instrumentation budget, your calibration effort, and your additional measurements. High sensitivity with low uncertainty means you have done a good job controlling something important — do not let that slip. High uncertainty with low sensitivity means the input is imprecise but it barely moves your output — you have flexibility there. This framing applies to every experiment you will run in this course: before you set up hardware, you should be asking which input variables contribute most to the uncertainty in the quantity you care about, and designing the measurement chain accordingly.

    slide 6: Monte Carlo Simulation — When the Assumptions Break (Image: A four-panel flow diagram showing Monte Carlo steps. Panel 1: "Define Input Distributions" — three Gaussian curves for V, I, R with labeled means and standard deviations. Panel 2: "Draw N = 10,000 Random Samples" — a scatter cloud of sample triplets (V, I, R). Panel 3: "Compute P for Each Sample" — shown as a MATLAB-style code snippet: "P_samples = V_samples .* I_samples;" and the resulting output array. Panel 4: "Plot Output Distribution" — a histogram of computed P values with a fitted Gaussian curve overlaid. A caption below: "Monte Carlo does not require small-error or linearity assumptions — it directly samples the full nonlinear function.") Talking Point: The partial derivative method is powerful and computationally efficient, but it has two assumptions: errors must be small enough for the first-order approximation to hold, and inputs must be independent. When those assumptions fail — when your function is highly nonlinear, or when uncertainty is large relative to the mean — Monte Carlo simulation gives you a more reliable answer. The concept is simple: you define probability distributions for each of your input measurements, draw thousands of random samples from those distributions, compute the derived quantity for every sample, and look at the distribution of outputs. The spread of that output distribution is your uncertainty estimate, without any linearity assumption. MATLAB makes this straightforward — a loop over ten thousand samples runs in milliseconds — and this week's lab is built around implementing it. The important result to look for is whether the Monte Carlo output and the partial derivative output agree. When they do, the partial derivative method is validated. When they diverge, you have identified a nonlinear region worth understanding.

    ================================================================================

    slide 7: Gaussian vs. Flat Input Distributions (Image: A two-panel side-by-side comparison. Left panel: "Gaussian Input Distribution" — bell curve centered on the mean, labeled "Use when: errors are random, symmetric, and instrument precision is characterized as a standard deviation." Right panel: "Uniform (Flat) Input Distribution" — rectangular distribution between lower and upper bounds, labeled "Use when: only tolerance limits are known (e.g., ±X% from datasheet) and you do not know the distribution shape within that range." Below both panels, a third panel showing two output P distributions — one from Gaussian inputs, one from flat inputs — with the Gaussian-input result slightly narrower. Caption: "Your choice of input distribution changes your output uncertainty estimate. Be honest about what you actually know.") Talking Point: When you run a Monte Carlo simulation, you choose input distributions, and that choice matters. Gaussian is appropriate when your errors are random and symmetric and when the instrument precision is characterized well enough to specify a standard deviation. Uniform is the honest choice when all you know is the datasheet tolerance — the instrument is rated to within ±2% of reading, for example, but you have no information about how errors are distributed within that range. In that case, a flat distribution from minus-2% to plus-2% is more defensible than assuming a Gaussian with a specific standard deviation you invented. The output distributions in both cases will look roughly Gaussian by the central limit theorem, but their widths will differ. Choosing the right input distribution is not a technical detail — it is a statement about how much you actually know about your instrument, and intellectual honesty about that is part of sound uncertainty analysis.

    slide 8: The Overlapping Region — Where Multiple Models Converge (Image: A single probability density plot showing three overlapping Gaussian curves on the same horizontal axis. The three curves are labeled "P from IV," "P from V²/R," and "P from I²R." The curves have different widths and are slightly offset. The region where all three curves substantially overlap is shaded green and labeled "Region of maximum overlap — best estimate of true P." A vertical dashed line marks the "True Value" falling within the overlap region. An annotation: "Overlap is evidence of consistency. Non-overlap is a diagnostic signal.") Talking Point: When you run Monte Carlo simulations for the three power equation forms, you get three output distributions. Because all three equations are valid models for the same physical quantity, the true value of power must lie in the region where all three distributions overlap. The overlap region is where multiple independent lines of evidence converge, and that convergence is more convincing than any single model alone. If the three distributions substantially overlap, you have internal consistency — your measurements are coherent. If they do not overlap — if two equation forms give incompatible estimates — something is wrong: your measurements are inconsistent, or one of your models is invalid for these conditions. The overlap-or-not question is the first thing to look for when you generate these plots in the lab today, and it is the visual analogue of the validation question the whole course builds toward.

    slide 11: Evaluator Moment — Which Formula Would You Choose? (Image: A clean prompt box on a white background. Text: "Your instruments: voltmeter uncertainty = ±5% of reading. Ammeter uncertainty = ±1% of reading. Resistance from datasheet: ±2%. You need to compute electrical power with the smallest possible total uncertainty. Which formula — P = IV, P = V²/R, or P = I²R — should you use, and why? Do not compute — just judge." A small table shows the three formulas and space for the student's reasoning. A prompt: "You have 90 seconds.") Talking Point: Take 90 seconds and make a judgment — not a calculation. [Pause.] The answer is P = I²R. Here is the reasoning. In P = V²/R, voltage appears squared — so the squared sensitivity coefficient amplifies the largest uncertainty (5% on voltage). That is the worst choice given these instruments. In P = IV, voltage and current contributions add in quadrature — the 5% voltage term still dominates. In P = I²R, current is squared but current has the smallest uncertainty (1%), so the amplification applies to the smallest input. The 2% resistance term also enters, but that is still smaller than the 5% voltage contribution in the other forms. The key point: sensitivity analysis is a design tool. You can choose the equation form that minimizes sensitivity to your worst sensor — but only if you understand what the partial derivatives are doing. That reasoning is what the oral interview will ask for.

    slide 12: Closure Error propagation is the skill that makes uncertainty analysis computational rather than conceptual. By computing partial derivatives, you trace exactly how errors in your raw measurements amplify as they flow through your equations — and that tells you where the dominant uncertainty comes from and where effort is best spent. Monte Carlo gives you a computational check that does not require linearity assumptions. Both methods end in the same place: a result expressed as an estimate plus its uncertainty interval, at a stated confidence level, with the dominant source identified. And when you present that result in a figure, the eight-step framework is how you make the argument visible — so that someone reading your report can see not just what you measured, but what it means. What you build today — the habit of asking where uncertainty comes from, quantifying it, and presenting it in a way that makes an argument — is something you will carry through every experiment for the rest of your engineering career.
