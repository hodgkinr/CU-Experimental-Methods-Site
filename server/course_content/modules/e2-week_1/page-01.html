Why Prediction Must Precede Measurement

Examines the psychological and epistemic reasons why prediction must precede measurement in a credible validation effort. **Opening consolidation beat (10–15 min) — Where we are and what you should have solid before W7:** Before introducing E2's new material, this lecture opens with an explicit inventory of the two foundational nodes that E1 developed but did not fully consolidate, and which W7 will depend on heavily. (1) *Transducer and sensor principles (N001):* Students have been introduced to the idea that sensors transduce physical quantities and that no instrument behaves ideally — but this node was activated only once in E1 (W1 L2) and was never reinforced. W7 will build the entire signal conditioning chain (noise, filtering, DAQ) directly on top of this foundation. Students who are shaky on what a transducer actually does, or why sensor non-ideality matters at the signal level, will find W7 harder than it needs to be. A brief in-class check: "What does it mean for a sensor to transduce a physical quantity? Give me an example where the transduction mechanism itself introduces error." (2) *Statistical properties and distributions (N015):* Students have applied uncertainty propagation and Monte Carlo throughout E1, but the underlying distribution theory — what a Gaussian distribution is and why it models measurement scatter, what variance means and where it comes from — was introduced at introductory depth in W2 L1 and not revisited. W9 will extend Monte Carlo to multi-variable systems and introduce regression and confidence interval comparison. Students who are computing correctly but do not understand *why* the distributions behave as they do will struggle with the interpretation demands of those tools. A brief prompt: "If I repeat a measurement 100 times and compute the mean, why does the uncertainty in the mean decrease with more trials? What is the underlying statistical reason?" These are not graded checks — they are calibration signals for students and the instructor. Students who cannot answer should revisit E1 W2 L1 and the Coleman & Steele reference before W7. The opening consolidation beat should take no more than 15 minutes and transition naturally into the postdiction framing that follows. Covers the cognitive bias of postdiction — the tendency to construct a model after seeing data and treat the fit as evidence of predictive validity — and why this is structurally indistinguishable from curve fitting. Students see the AMVF flowchart from Week 1 again: it runs left-to-right because model development logically precedes experimental data collection, not the other way around. The Tier 1 Phase 2 cross-prediction experience is revisited explicitly — what students felt when predicting before data release is now formalized as a required discipline. **EAC motivational framing + test engineer identity:** This lecture should open with the professional context for the E2 lab activities: the skill of understanding how experiments were done in the past, identifying their limitations, and proposing improvements using current technology mirrors exactly what new engineers do when they join an organization. The Engineering Advisory Council (EAC) has identified this as a priority professional competency — the ability to walk into a legacy lab or experimental workflow, understand what was done and why, and recommend upgrades using current sensors, analysis methods, or understanding. E2's canned experiments are deliberately structured to give students practice with exactly this skill before they design from scratch in E3. **Test engineer framing (E2):** At the opening of E2, remind students explicitly that these competencies — evaluating legacy experiments, identifying limitations, proposing improvements — are core test engineer competencies. The EAC has flagged this as what new hires are expected to be able to do. The canned experiments in E2 are structured to be recognizable test engineer work, not academic exercises.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain why postdiction — fitting a model to data already collected — is fundamentally different from genuine validation, and identify cognitive biases that make postdiction feel like validation — CLO 3
  • Formulate a formal hypothesis for a complex aerospace system, specifying expected system response features and acceptable agreement criteria before any data is released — CLO 1, CLO 3
  • Define intended use as the primary framing constraint on all predictive modeling decisions — CLO 3
  • Submit a pre-experiment prediction document for a canned aerospace experiment with reasoning that is graded independently of whether the prediction is correct — CLO 1, CLO 5

    slide 4: The Cognitive Bias That Makes Postdiction Feel Like Validation (Image: A brain-shaped diagram divided into two halves. The left half is labeled "Before Data" and contains a simple idealized sine wave with the label "Mental model: clean, complete." The right half is labeled "After Data" and shows the same sine wave overlaid with noisy scatter data — but with a note bubble pointing to the brain saying "I knew it all along." Below the diagram is a short axis arrow labeled "Time →" with a lock icon at the "Before Data" end and an X-circle icon at the "After Data" end, illustrating that the window for honest prediction closes once data is seen. The tone is conceptual and slightly cautionary — this is a cognitive trap.) Talking Point: There is a well-documented cognitive bias that makes postdiction feel exactly like genuine prediction — it is called hindsight bias, and it affects trained engineers just as much as anyone else. Once you have seen the data, you cannot un-see it. Your brain retroactively adjusts your memory of what you expected, so the model you build after the fact feels like the model you would have built before. The insidious thing is that this happens without conscious intent — you are not trying to deceive anyone, including yourself. The result is a model that fits the data you have but may fail completely on data you have not collected yet. Postdiction produces models that describe observations rather than predict behavior, and in aerospace engineering, the difference between those two things has consequences. The pre-commitment document is the structural solution: it creates a record that cannot be retroactively adjusted.

Talking Point: You saw this diagram in Week 1 Lecture 1, and I told you then that the left-to-right flow is not arbitrary. Now I want to make that concrete. The AMVF runs from model development on the left to experimental data on the right because that is the logical sequence: you build a model, you make a prediction based on it, and then you measure to test whether the prediction holds. If you run the flowchart backwards — if you collect data first and build a model to match it — you are not using experimental data to validate a model. You are using experimental data to construct a model, and those are fundamentally different activities. In E2, we are going to hold ourselves to the forward direction, every time. Your prediction document is your evidence that you ran the flowchart in the right direction.

    slide 8: Closure This lecture opened E2 with the discipline that defines Phase II: prediction must precede measurement, and that sequence must be documented. Postdiction feels like validation because hindsight bias is invisible from the inside — but it is structurally indistinguishable from curve fitting and produces models that describe the past rather than predict the future. The AMVF flowchart runs left to right because model development is logically prior to data collection, and your prediction document is the evidence that you followed that sequence. In Lecture 2 this week, we will move from the epistemics of why prediction matters to the mechanics of how to build a defensible one: the five-element anatomy of the prediction document, the formal vocabulary for system response features and acceptance criteria, and the gate procedure before equipment access. Come ready to produce the real thing.
