Formal Hypothesis Framing for Complex Systems

Extends E1's hypothesis formulation vocabulary to more complex systems where the model has more parameters, more potential failure modes, and less obvious predictions. Covers: intended use as the framing constraint (the model is only validated for the purpose it is designed for), system response features as the specific quantities the hypothesis makes claims about, and defining acceptable agreement as a numerical criterion before any experiment begins. The connection to E1's Week 1 hypothesis work is made explicit — the vocabulary is the same, but the stakes and complexity are higher. **AMVF framing (W6):** This lecture should explicitly reconnect to the AMVF — the experiment is not an end in itself. The question students should carry into every E2 lab is "what does this experiment tell me about the system, and why does it matter?" This framing connects course activities to senior design projects and to the broader curriculum: experimental methods is the physical modeling branch that bridges mathematical models and the real world. A brief reminder of the AMVF diagram at the opening of this lecture reinforces the learning spine.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain why postdiction — fitting a model to data already collected — is fundamentally different from genuine validation, and identify cognitive biases that make postdiction feel like validation — CLO 3
  • Formulate a formal hypothesis for a complex aerospace system, specifying expected system response features and acceptable agreement criteria before any data is released — CLO 1, CLO 3
  • Define intended use as the primary framing constraint on all predictive modeling decisions — CLO 3
  • Submit a pre-experiment prediction document for a canned aerospace experiment with reasoning that is graded independently of whether the prediction is correct — CLO 1, CLO 5

    slide 3: What "Intended Use" Means and Why It Is the First Constraint (Image: A three-panel illustration showing the same physical system — a vibrating beam — at three different intended uses. Panel 1: "Intended use: predict resonant frequency within ±5% for mission planning." Panel 2: "Intended use: predict peak deflection under a specific load for structural margin." Panel 3: "Intended use: characterize damping ratio for control system design." Each panel shows a different set of highlighted model parameters and measurement requirements. Below the three panels, a single line reads: "The model that is adequate for one intended use may be completely inadequate for another." The panels are arranged horizontally, and each has a distinct colored border to help students visually separate the three cases.) Talking Point: Here is the first and most important framing constraint on any predictive model: intended use. A model is not validated in the abstract — it is validated for a purpose. The same physical system can require three entirely different models depending on what you are trying to predict and how accurately you need to predict it. If your intended use is predicting a resonant frequency to within five percent, a simple Euler-Bernoulli beam model with a mass correction might be perfectly adequate. If your intended use is predicting the damping ratio for a control system, you need a model that includes energy dissipation mechanisms that the simple beam model ignores entirely. The intended use statement drives every downstream modeling decision: what parameters need to be measured, how precisely, under what conditions, and what level of discrepancy counts as acceptable agreement. It is the first sentence of a defensible validation plan, and it must be written before any modeling decisions are made.

    slide 4: System Response Features — What You Are Actually Committing To (Image: A two-column layout. Left column header: "Vague system response feature (not acceptable)." Below it: three examples in red italic text — "The output will increase," "There will be oscillation," "The temperature will change over time." Right column header: "Committed system response feature (required)." Below it: three examples in green regular text — "Peak amplitude will reach 8.3 mm ± 0.4 mm by t = 0.5 s," "Oscillation frequency will be 47 Hz ± 2 Hz at the primary resonance mode," "Temperature will reach steady state within 90 seconds at a value of 52°C ± 3°C." At the bottom of the slide, a horizontal divider, then a caption: "A system response feature is falsifiable only if it specifies what would count as disagreement — meaning there exists a specific measurable outcome that would count as disagreement, not just one that would count as confirmation." The visual contrast between the red vague examples and the green specific examples is the central visual message.) Talking Point: A system response feature is a specific, falsifiable claim about what your model predicts the experiment will produce. Let me be direct about what "specific" means: it means a numerical value, a range, a time, a frequency — something that can be compared to a number. "The output will increase" is not a system response feature — it is an observation. "The output will reach a peak amplitude of 8.3 millimeters at approximately 0.5 seconds" is a system response feature — I can put data on the same axes and tell you whether the prediction held. The reason specificity matters is that a vague prediction can never be falsified, which means it can never teach you anything. If you said "the output will go up" and the output goes up, you have learned nothing. If you said "the output will reach 8.3 millimeters" and the output reaches 6.1, you have learned that your model is overestimating amplitude by 26%, and now you have a specific question: which model assumption is responsible for that gap?

    slide 5: Acceptable Agreement — What Does "Close Enough" Mean, and Who Decides? (Image: A single graph with a model prediction line shown as a solid gold curve and experimental data scatter plotted around it. The graph shows a clear acceptance band — a shaded region around the prediction line, labeled "Acceptance criterion: agreement within ±10% relative to predicted peak, or within uncertainty bounds, whichever is larger." Below the shaded region, two data points fall outside the band with arrows labeled "These points require explanation — not dismissal." The acceptance band is drawn with a dashed border to indicate it was defined before data collection. A note in the lower right corner reads: "The acceptance criterion must be stated before data is collected. It cannot be adjusted after." The graph style matches MATLAB-generated figures.) Talking Point: The acceptance criterion is the pre-stated answer to the question: how close does the data have to be to my prediction for me to say the model is adequate for the intended use? This is not a judgment you make after you see the results — it is a commitment you make before you collect any data. And it has to be numerical. "The model is good enough" is not an acceptance criterion. "Agreement within plus-or-minus ten percent relative to the predicted peak value, or within the propagated uncertainty bounds, whichever is larger" — that is an acceptance criterion. We use the larger criterion because claiming a meaningful disagreement when the difference is smaller than your measurement noise would be misleading — your uncertainty floor sets the minimum distinction you can honestly claim. Setting the acceptance criterion before data collection is what keeps you honest when the data comes back and you are tempted to judge whether it "looks good."

    slide 6: More Parameters, More Failure Modes — Hypothesis Framing for Complex Systems (Image: A comparison between a simple system and a complex system. Left side: "E1 system — pendulum" with a single-equation model and two parameters (length, gravitational acceleration). Two labeled failure modes: "Amplitude too large (nonlinear)," "Air resistance ignored." Right side: "E2 system — wind tunnel / vibration rig" with a multi-parameter block diagram showing three input parameters, two environmental factors, and four potential model failure modes. The failure modes on the right are: "Boundary condition assumptions," "Unmodeled damping," "Nonlinear stiffness beyond small deflection," "Aerodynamic coupling effects." A bracket spans the right side labeled "Each failure mode corresponds to a specific anomalous data pattern." The comparison illustrates escalation of complexity without making the task seem impossible.) Talking Point: The core challenge of hypothesis framing in E2 is that the systems are more complex — more parameters, more coupling between effects, and more ways for the model to be wrong in a structured and physically meaningful way. In E1, you had a pendulum with one primary model parameter, and the dominant failure mode was clear — once the amplitude gets large, the small-angle assumption breaks. In E2, you might be working with a vibrating structure where boundary conditions, nonlinear stiffness, aerodynamic coupling, and damping mechanisms can all produce model-data discrepancy independently or together. The good news is that the vocabulary is exactly the same: you specify system response features, you commit to an acceptance criterion, and you name the anomalous patterns that would indicate each type of model failure. The discipline is identical — you are just applying it to a harder problem, and the payoff for doing it correctly is higher.

    slide 7: The Risk Assessment — Naming What Wrong Looks Like in Advance (Image: A three-row table illustration. Column headers: "Anomalous pattern," "Physical cause," "Implication for model." Row 1: "Residuals increase systematically with amplitude — systematic trend" | "Nonlinear stiffness — model assumes small deflection" | "Model-form error: linear model inadequate above this amplitude range." Row 2: "Measurements oscillate at a frequency not in the model" | "Unmodeled structural mode" | "Model-form error: additional degree of freedom required." Row 3: "Bias offset between prediction and data at all conditions" | "Parametric error — wrong stiffness value used" | "Parametric uncertainty: model form may be correct but input is wrong." Each row is color-coded: systematic trend in orange, unmodeled mode in red, parametric bias in yellow. The table is clear and readable — not crowded. Note: "model-form error" is a term we will formally define in W8. For now, think of it as: the model's structure is missing a physical effect — not just a wrong parameter value, but a wrong equation. It stems from flaws in the model's fundamental formulation.) Talking Point: The risk assessment section of your prediction document asks you to name, in advance, at least one anomalous data pattern that would indicate a model-form limitation rather than measurement noise. This is not pessimism — it is predictive discipline. If you understand your model's assumptions, you can predict exactly what the data would look like if one of those assumptions breaks down. A linear spring model will produce residuals that increase systematically with amplitude if nonlinear stiffness is present — because the model cannot generate amplitude-dependent behavior. An unmodeled structural mode will produce oscillations at a frequency that is not in your prediction. A wrong parameter value will produce a bias offset that is consistent across all conditions. Each of these patterns is specific and distinguishable from random measurement noise, and the tool for distinguishing them is the uncertainty band you already computed. If a data pattern is inside the uncertainty band, it is consistent with noise. If it shows systematic structure that your model cannot produce, it is telling you something about the model.

    slide 8: What "Prediction Before Exposure" Actually Requires — The Five-Element Checklist (Image: A five-element checklist diagram — each element in its own horizontal band, numbered 1 through 5. Element 1: "System response features — specific values or ranges, shape of expected output." Element 2: "Model derivation — equations with substituted numbers, not just variable names." Element 3: "Estimated uncertainty — partial derivatives applied to sensor specs, result as estimate ± uncertainty." Element 4: "Acceptance criterion — numerical threshold committed before data collection." Element 5: "Risk assessment — at least one anomalous pattern named that would indicate model-form limitation rather than noise." Each band has a small checkmark icon at the left in gold. The overall layout is clean and structured — this is the checklist students will carry into the Tier 2 pre-lab session.) Talking Point: When I say prediction before exposure, I do not mean a vague directional statement — "I think the output will go up." A defensible prediction has five specific elements, and I want you to see them now because they are the required components of your Tier 2 pre-lab prediction document. First: system response features — what specific physical quantities do you expect, at what magnitudes, and in what shape over time or across the independent variable? Second: a model derivation — show the equations with numbers substituted, not just the symbolic form. Third: an estimated uncertainty in the primary derived quantity — apply partial derivative propagation to the sensor specs you have been given and express the result as estimate plus or minus uncertainty. Fourth: an acceptance criterion — a numerical threshold that defines what "agreement" means, stated before you collect any data. Fifth: a risk assessment — name at least one anomalous data pattern that would tell you the model form is wrong rather than the measurements being noisy. Each of these elements has a specific purpose, and we cover the reasoning behind each one in the slides that follow.

    slide 9: The Anatomy of a Tier 2 Prediction Document (Image: A visual breakdown of the prediction document structure, laid out as a one-page annotated mockup. The Tier 2 prediction document has five required sections, and here is what each one looks like in practice. The first section is your system response features — you commit in writing to what specific physical quantities you expect, with magnitudes, units, and expected trend shape. Not "the output will increase" — "the peak deflection will reach this value at this point in the test, under this loading condition." The second section is your model derivation — you show the governing equations with numbers substituted in, not just symbolic expressions. The third section is your estimated uncertainty — you apply partial derivative propagation to the sensor specifications you have been given and express the result in correct engineering form: estimate plus or minus uncertainty with units and method stated. The fourth section is your acceptance criterion — a numerical threshold. And the fifth section is your risk assessment. That document is shown to the TA or instructor before you are cleared for equipment access, and it is reproduced verbatim as Section 1 of your group report. Write it as if your professional credibility depends on it, because in a real test program, it would.

    slide 10: What Makes a Hypothesis Framing "Complete" vs. "Vague" (Image: A two-column side-by-side comparison of two hypothesis framings for the same experiment. Left column labeled "Incomplete (not acceptable)" in red — shows a multi-line hypothesis that reads: "We expect the system to respond as predicted by our model. The output should match the theoretical value within a reasonable range, and if it doesn't, there may be some error sources we haven't accounted for." Right column labeled "Complete (required)" in green — shows a structured framing with explicit entries for each of the five elements: system response feature with numbers, model equation with substituted values, uncertainty estimate with partial derivative shown, acceptance criterion as a numerical inequality, and risk assessment identifying one specific anomalous pattern. The contrast in specificity between left and right columns is stark and intentional.) Talking Point: I want to show you the difference between a hypothesis framing that is incomplete and one that is complete, using the same experiment. The incomplete version on the left is what many groups produce when they are in a hurry or when they are unsure of the model — it says something like "we expect the output to match the theoretical value within a reasonable range." There is nothing falsifiable in that sentence. "Reasonable" is doing all the work, and it is doing it after the fact. The complete version on the right specifies exactly what values are expected, where those values came from, how uncertain those values are, what threshold defines agreement, and what specific data pattern would tell you the model has a structural problem. Notice that the complete version is not longer — it is just more specific. Specificity is not extra work — it is the minimum requirement for a prediction that can teach you something when it fails.

    slide 11: How the Experimentally Informed Model Changes the Task (Image: A two-stage diagram. Stage 1 is labeled "Traditional view: Validate or reject" and shows a simple binary branch — a model on the left, experimental data on the right, and two outcome arrows: one labeled "Model accepted (data agrees)" and one labeled "Model rejected (data disagrees)." Talking Point: I want to reframe what you think the canned experiments are for. You might assume the goal is to run the experiment, compare to your prediction, and conclude "the model is good" or "the model is not good." That is not the goal. The goal is to use the experimental results to understand what the model is missing — and to propose, specifically, how you would improve it based on what you learned. This is the experimentally informed model concept. In E2, it is a required deliverable. Section 3D of your Tier 2 report asks you to answer specifically: what model improvement does your data suggest? That question starts here, in the prediction document — when you name in advance what would reveal a model limitation, you are already setting up the question that Section 3D must answer.

Talking Point: I want you to see that the prediction document and the group report are not two separate tasks — the prediction document is the first section of the report and the foundation of every analysis section that follows. The system response features you commit to become the benchmark against which you measure your residuals. The model derivation becomes the basis for your prediction-versus-outcome comparison. The uncertainty estimate feeds directly into the uncertainty analysis section and into whether the discrepancy you observe is larger than what measurement noise can explain. The acceptance criterion is what you apply in Section 3A to conclude whether the model is adequate for the stated intended use. And the risk assessment is what you compare against the anomalous patterns you actually observe. Every element of the prediction document does real analytical work later in the report. That is why we require it — not as a bureaucratic gate, but because a report without a committed prediction has no foundation.

    slide 13: The Pre-Commitment Gate — What to Expect in Week 6 Lab (Image: A simple process flow diagram showing four boxes connected by arrows. Talking Point: Here is exactly what you are doing in the Week 6 lab session. Your group receives the canned experiment document for your assigned Tier 2 experiment. You read it carefully, understand the physical system, and then — before anyone touches any hardware — you develop a predictive model from physical principles. You derive the equations, substitute the system parameters you have been given, estimate uncertainty from the sensor specs, sketch the expected output, and write down an acceptance criterion and a risk assessment. You bring that document to the TA or instructor, and they review it. If the prediction is documented and the reasoning is sound, you are cleared for equipment access in Week 8. If it is not, you are not cleared. And here is the key thing about the document itself: you do not revise it after the gate check. Whatever you commit to in Week 6 is reproduced verbatim as Section 1 of your group report. The honesty of that pre-commitment is what makes the comparison in Week 8 meaningful.

    slide 14: Closure Formal hypothesis framing for complex systems is the same discipline as the hypothesis work you did in E1, applied under higher stakes and with more parameters to account for. Intended use is the first constraint: it determines what model you need, how precisely you need it to be accurate, and what counts as adequate agreement. System response features must be specific enough to be falsifiable — a number, a range, a shape, something you can compare to data. The acceptance criterion defines agreement before the data exists. And the risk assessment names, in advance, what the data would look like if your model has a structural problem — which is the single best preparation for interpreting discrepancy correctly when it appears. In the Week 6 lab session, you will produce this prediction document for your Tier 2 canned experiment and present it for the pre-commitment gate check. The habits you build here — specificity, pre-commitment, honest acknowledgment of model limitations — are the habits of a test engineer, and they are what will make your validation report credible to someone who was not in the room when you ran the experiment.
