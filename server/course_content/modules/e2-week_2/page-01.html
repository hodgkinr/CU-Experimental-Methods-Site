Nyquist Sampling, Aliasing & Signal Bandwidth

Introduces the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem and aliasing as the fundamental constraints on any digital measurement system. Covers signal bandwidth as the property of the physical phenomenon being measured (not a property of the sensor), the aliasing mechanism — how undersampled signals fold back into the measured spectrum — and the practical consequences for aerospace measurements where signal content may span many orders of frequency. Students work through examples to develop intuition for when sampling rate matters critically.

Source Variants

    slide 1: Title Slide Title: Nyquist Sampling, Aliasing & Signal Bandwidth Talking Point: Before you can run a credible measurement on a complex aerospace system, you need to understand what you are asking the digital measurement system to do. A sensor converts a physical quantity into an analog electrical signal — a continuous stream of information about the world. A data acquisition system takes discrete snapshots of that signal at a fixed rate. Everything about whether those snapshots faithfully represent the original signal depends on one thing: how fast you sample relative to how fast the signal changes. That relationship is governed by the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem, and when it is violated, the result is not noise — it is a plausible-looking signal that is completely wrong. That is the subject of this lecture.

    slide 2: What Signal Bandwidth Actually Is Signal bandwidth — the range of frequencies that contain meaningful information in the signal — is a property of the physical phenomenon, not the sensor or DAQ. (Image: A two-panel illustration. Left panel: a time-domain signal showing a complex waveform — a mix of a slow sinusoidal component and a faster oscillation superimposed on it. The signal is continuous, plotted as a smooth analog curve, with a label "Measured physical phenomenon — continuous in time." Right panel: the same signal's frequency-domain representation (FFT magnitude spectrum) plotted as a bar chart, with two visible peaks labeled "Low-frequency component: 10 Hz" and "High-frequency component: 340 Hz." A bracket spans from 0 to 340 Hz and is labeled "Signal bandwidth: 340 Hz — this is a property of the phenomenon, not the sensor." The visual contrast between the time domain (messy, complex) and the frequency domain (clean, peaks visible) is the main teaching point of this slide.) Talking Point: Signal bandwidth is a property of the physical phenomenon you are measuring — it is not a property of your sensor or your DAQ system. It describes the range of frequencies that contain meaningful information about the signal. A structural vibration signal from an aerospace component might have a dominant resonance at 10 hertz and a higher-frequency mode at 340 hertz. The signal bandwidth of that measurement extends to 340 hertz — regardless of what sample rate you choose. Why does this matter? Because if you choose a sample rate without knowing the signal bandwidth, you are making a claim about the signal's frequency content without evidence. The claim you are making is: "I believe this signal has no content above half my sample rate." If that claim is wrong, your data is not merely imprecise — it is silently corrupted in a way that looks real.

    slide 3: The Sampling Theorem — The Fundamental Constraint (Image: A diagram showing a continuous analog signal (smooth sinusoidal curve) with two sets of sample points overlaid. The top set of samples is spaced closely — labeled "Sample rate = 10× signal frequency." The resulting reconstructed signal, shown as a dashed curve through the sample points, closely tracks the original. The bottom set of samples is spaced too widely — labeled "Sample rate = 0.8× signal frequency (undersampled)." The resulting reconstructed signal, shown as a red dashed curve, produces a much lower-frequency sinusoid that does not match the original. A boxed statement in the center of the slide reads: "Nyquist Criterion: sample rate must be greater than twice the highest frequency present in the signal." The original signal and the alias are clearly color-coded — original in gold, alias in red.) Talking Point: The Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem tells us the minimum sample rate required to faithfully represent a signal. The requirement is simple: your sample rate must be greater than twice the highest frequency present in the signal you are trying to measure. That frequency — half the sample rate — is called the Nyquist frequency, and it is the boundary above which information is lost. Note that I said "greater than twice" — not "equal to twice." In practice, engineers apply a margin above the theoretical minimum because real signals do not have perfectly sharp cutoffs. The practical rule is that your sample rate should be at least five to ten times the highest frequency of interest, depending on how well you can characterize the signal's frequency content. Choosing a sample rate is a modeling commitment: you are claiming that the signal has no content above the Nyquist frequency, and if that claim is wrong, you will not know it from the data alone.

    slide 4: What Aliasing Actually Looks Like — The Stroboscopic Wheel (Image: A three-frame stroboscopic effect illustration. Frame 1: A wheel with a single marker dot, spinning at 10 revolutions per second, is illuminated by a strobe light flashing at 12 Hz. The wheel appears to spin slowly forward — dotted arrow labeled "Apparent motion: slow forward." Frame 2: The same wheel spinning at 10 rev/s, strobe at 9 Hz. The wheel appears to spin slowly backward — arrow labeled "Apparent motion: slow backward." Frame 3: The same wheel spinning at 10 rev/s, strobe at 10 Hz. The wheel appears to stop — labeled "Apparent motion: stationary." Below the three frames: "None of these stroboscopic observations are noise. They are coherent, plausible, and completely wrong." A caption reads: "This is aliasing. The observed frequency is the difference between the signal and the strobe — it is not in the original signal at all.") Talking Point: The most important thing I want you to understand about aliasing is that it does not produce obvious noise — it produces a coherent, plausible-looking signal at a different frequency. The stroboscopic wheel is the perfect illustration. When a wheel spinning at ten revolutions per second is illuminated by a strobe flashing at twelve hertz, it appears to spin slowly forward at two revolutions per second. Your DAQ is the strobe — it observes the voltage signal at fixed intervals. If those intervals are too far apart relative to how fast the signal changes, the reconstructed waveform is as wrong as the backward-spinning wheel. The wheel is not actually slowing down — your measurement system is creating a false low-frequency signal out of the difference between the true signal and your sample rate. This is aliasing. The alias frequency is the difference between the true signal frequency and the sampling frequency, and it falls inside your measurement bandwidth — which is exactly why it is dangerous. You cannot look at an aliased signal and know it is wrong by visual inspection. It looks like a real, physical signal, because digitally, it is one.

    slide 5: Why Aliasing Is Invisible — The Silent Corruption Problem (Image: A side-by-side comparison. Left: a real physical vibration signal — a clean sinusoid at 380 Hz, shown in the frequency domain as a single spectral peak. Right: the same signal as captured by a DAQ system sampling at 500 Hz — the 380 Hz signal aliases to 500 - 380 = 120 Hz, appearing as a spectral peak at 120 Hz. Both frequency-domain plots are labeled with axes (frequency in Hz, magnitude in arbitrary units). The caption at the bottom reads: "The 120 Hz alias peak looks real. There is no visual marker in the data that indicates corruption." An annotation on the right plot reads: "You would report this as a 120 Hz structural mode — and you would be wrong.") Talking Point: Here is the scenario I want you to sit with. You are running a structural vibration test on an aerospace component. You expect significant frequency content up to about 300 hertz. You set your sample rate at 500 hertz, which gives you a Nyquist frequency of 250 hertz. You are reasonably satisfied. What you do not know is that there is a structural mode at 380 hertz — well above your Nyquist frequency. That 380 hertz signal aliases into your measurement at 500 minus 380, which is 120 hertz. You now have a perfectly clean, well-resolved spectral peak at 120 hertz that you did not expect. You write it up as a structural resonance you found, present it to your team, and potentially design a control system to avoid exciting it. None of that is real. There is no 120 hertz mode. The mode at 380 hertz is real, and it is invisible to your measurement. This is why aliasing is a silent corruption problem — it does not announce itself. The only protection is choosing a sufficient sample rate before you collect data.

    slide 6: Anti-Aliasing Filters — The Structural Solution (Image: A block diagram of the signal processing chain between a physical sensor and a digital data record. The chain has five stages from left to right: "Physical sensor" → "Amplifier/Conditioning" → "Anti-Aliasing Filter (Low-pass)" → "Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC)" → "Digital Data Record." The anti-aliasing filter stage is highlighted with a gold border. An annotation shows the filter's cutoff frequency labeled "fc < fs/2" (cutoff frequency must be below the Nyquist frequency). A second annotation on the ADC stage reads: "Once digitized, aliased content cannot be removed — information is permanently lost." A dashed vertical line between the anti-aliasing filter and the ADC is labeled "This line is the point of no return — aliasing must be prevented before digitization.") Talking Point: The structural solution to aliasing is the anti-aliasing filter: a low-pass filter placed in the signal conditioning chain before the analog-to-digital converter, designed to attenuate frequency content above the Nyquist frequency before it can be aliased into the digital record. The critical principle here is timing. The anti-aliasing filter must operate in the analog domain — before the ADC — because once the signal has been digitized, the alias is already mixed into the data. A digital filter applied after digitization cannot un-alias a signal, because the aliased content and the real content at the same frequency are indistinguishable in the digital record. The information that was lost when the signal was undersampled is permanently gone. This is why the design of the anti-aliasing filter must be decided before you set up your DAQ configuration — it is not a post-processing step, it is a pre-measurement design decision.

    slide 7: Choosing the Sample Rate — The Engineering Decision (Image: A horizontal decision diagram with three components. Component 1: "Characterize signal bandwidth" — shown as a frequency spectrum with the highest expected frequency labeled fmax. Component 2: "Apply Nyquist margin" — shown as the formula "fs ≥ 5 × fmax (practical engineering margin)" with a note "Theoretical minimum: fs > 2 × fmax." A footnote beneath Component 2 reads: "The 5–10× rule accounts for the anti-aliasing filter's transition band — it is not a universal constant. Lower margins may be acceptable with a steeper filter; wider margins may be needed when signal bandwidth is uncertain." Component 3: "Verify against DAQ hardware limits" — shown as a hardware specification block with maximum sample rate and resolution. The three components are connected by arrows. Below the diagram: "Sample rate selection is a modeling commitment — you are claiming no energy above fs/2. Document your reasoning." The diagram uses a clean flowchart style with the course color scheme.) Talking Point: In practice, how do you choose a sample rate? Start by characterizing the signal bandwidth — identify the highest frequency that contains information relevant to your measurement. For structural modes, this might come from a preliminary analysis of the natural frequencies using the geometry and material properties. For aerodynamic measurements, it might come from the expected turbulent fluctuation frequencies at your operating conditions. Once you have an estimate of the maximum frequency of interest, apply a practical margin: use a sample rate at least five to ten times that frequency, not just two times. The factor-of-two Nyquist minimum leaves almost no margin for filter roll-off, hardware imperfections, or unexpected high-frequency content. Then verify that your chosen sample rate is achievable with your DAQ hardware given the number of channels and the resolution you need. Document this decision in your report — not just what sample rate you used, but why you chose it based on the expected signal bandwidth.

    slide 8: Identifying Signal Bandwidth from Real Data — MATLAB in the Loop (Image: Two MATLAB figure panels. Left panel: a time-domain signal from a structural vibration measurement — a decaying oscillation with visible high-frequency content superimposed. The x-axis is labeled "Time (s)," y-axis "Acceleration (m/s²)." Right panel: the frequency-domain FFT of the same signal, with the x-axis labeled "Frequency (Hz)" and y-axis "Magnitude (m/s²)." Two spectral peaks are labeled: "Primary mode: 47 Hz" and "Secondary mode: 312 Hz." A vertical dashed red line is labeled "Nyquist frequency for this measurement: 500 Hz (sample rate = 1000 Hz)." The bandwidth bracket extends from 0 to 312 Hz, with the note: "312 Hz << 500 Hz — adequate margin." The figure style matches MATLAB default with course-consistent axis labels.) Talking Point: In the Week 7 lab session, you are going to do exactly this — use MATLAB to visualize the signal from your Tier 2 canned experiment in both the time domain and the frequency domain. The time-domain plot tells you a lot about the character of the signal: is it a steady oscillation, a decaying transient, a step response? But the frequency-domain FFT is what tells you about bandwidth. You are looking for the highest frequency at which there is meaningful spectral energy — not noise floor, but actual signal content. That frequency is the lower bound on your Nyquist frequency, and half of it is the minimum cutoff frequency for your anti-aliasing filter. In this example, the primary structural mode is at 47 hertz and the secondary mode is at 312 hertz. If the sample rate were 600 hertz instead of 1000, the secondary mode at 312 hertz would be right against the Nyquist limit with almost no margin — any filter imperfection would potentially alias secondary mode content into the measurement. The 1000 hertz sample rate gives you comfortable margin. Seeing this in your own data, for your own experiment, is what makes the Nyquist criterion real rather than abstract.

    slide 9: What Aliasing Looks Like in Aerospace Data — A Worked Example (Image: A simulated MATLAB FFT plot showing two scenarios side by side. Left: "Adequately sampled (fs = 2000 Hz)" — FFT shows peaks at 80 Hz, 420 Hz, and 780 Hz, all clearly resolved within the 0–1000 Hz bandwidth. Right: "Undersampled (fs = 800 Hz)" — FFT shows peaks at 80 Hz and 420 Hz, but the 780 Hz component has aliased to 800 - 780 = 20 Hz, appearing as a prominent low-frequency peak. The 20 Hz alias peak is labeled in red: "780 Hz signal aliased to 20 Hz — reported as a structural mode if not recognized." Vertical dashed lines show the Nyquist frequency for each case: 1000 Hz on the left and 400 Hz on the right. A banner across the top reads: "Same physical signal — two entirely different frequency-domain representations based solely on sample rate choice.") Talking Point: Let me make aliasing numerically concrete. Here is a physical signal with three real components at 80, 420, and 780 hertz. On the left, sampled at 2000 hertz — all three components are cleanly resolved in the FFT. On the right, the same physical signal sampled at 800 hertz. The Nyquist frequency is now 400 hertz. The 80-hertz and 420-hertz components are there — but the 420-hertz component is already very close to the Nyquist limit and may be attenuated by the anti-aliasing filter if it is too aggressive. And the 780-hertz component aliases to 800 minus 780, which is 20 hertz. That 20-hertz peak is prominent in the undersampled FFT. If you did not know the truth, you would report it as a 20-hertz structural mode. You might even try to correlate it to some physical mechanism at that frequency. This is not a rare failure mode — it is exactly the kind of error that gets made when sample rates are chosen without first characterizing the signal bandwidth.

    slide 11: Closure The Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem is not an abstract mathematical curiosity — it is the fundamental constraint on every digital measurement you will make in an aerospace career. Signal bandwidth is a property of the physical phenomenon, not the sensor, and choosing a sample rate without characterizing the bandwidth is making an untested claim about what the signal contains. Aliasing does not look like noise — it looks like a real signal at a plausible frequency, and the only protection against it is adequate sample rate and proper anti-aliasing filtering, both chosen before data collection begins. In the Week 7 lab, you will see this in real data from your Tier 2 experiment — the FFT analysis is not just a visualization exercise, it is the diagnostic tool for evaluating whether the sampling choices in your experiment are defensible.
