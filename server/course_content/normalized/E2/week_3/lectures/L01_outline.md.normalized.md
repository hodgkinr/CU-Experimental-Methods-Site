slide 1: Title Slide
Title: Model-Form Error & Discrepancy as Diagnostic Information
Talking Point: This lecture addresses what I consider the hardest conceptual shift of Phase II, and I want to state it plainly at the start. When the data comes back from your Tier 2 experiment and it does not match your prediction perfectly — and it will not — your first instinct is going to be to explain the discrepancy away. To say the sensors drifted, or something went wrong with the setup, or there was noise. That instinct is wrong. Discrepancy is not failure. Discrepancy is data about the model, and the structure of the discrepancy tells you something specific and true about what the model is missing. That is the subject of this lecture: learning to read the gap between prediction and measurement as information rather than embarrassment.

slide 2: Three Types of Discrepancy — A Taxonomy That Matters
**Residual** = predicted value − measured value at a single data point. A collection of residuals across all data points is the diagnostic fingerprint of your model's limitations.
(Image: A three-row table with clear visual differentiation. Column headers: "Type," "What it looks like in residuals," "Physical interpretation." Row 1 — "Parametric uncertainty": residuals are randomly scattered around zero with no trend, all within the uncertainty band. Accompanying mini-plot shows scatter plot of residuals vs. independent variable — points distributed randomly, bounded by ±uncertainty band. Label: "Model form is adequate — wrong parameter values." Row 2 — "Model-form error": residuals show a systematic trend — increasing with the independent variable, or consistently positive in one range and negative in another. Mini-plot shows a clear slope or curve in the residuals. Label: "Model is structurally missing a physical effect." Row 3 — "Measurement error": residuals are randomly scattered but exceed the uncertainty band consistently in one direction. Mini-plot shows scatter above the band. Label: "Systematic bias in measurement, not in the model." The three rows are color-coded: Row 1 in gold, Row 2 in orange-red, Row 3 in gray. The visual distinction between random scatter and systematic trend is the central teaching point.)
Talking Point: Before you can interpret a discrepancy correctly, you need a vocabulary for what kind of discrepancy it is. The first type is parametric uncertainty: the residuals — the point-by-point differences between your prediction and the measured data — are randomly scattered around zero, no consistent trend, and the magnitude of the scatter is consistent with your propagated uncertainty estimate. Random scatter within the uncertainty band means the model is not obviously wrong. If the scatter magnitude exceeds what your measurement noise predicts, better parameter values would reduce it — this is parametric. If scatter is consistent with the noise floor, the model may be adequate — but random scatter alone cannot prove this; it can only fail to show the model is structurally wrong. This pattern means the structure of the physics is probably right, but the parameter values you used may be imprecise. The fix is better characterization of the input parameters. The second type is model-form error: the residuals show a systematic trend. They increase with the independent variable, or they are consistently negative in one operating region and positive in another, or they curve in a way your model cannot produce. This pattern means the model is structurally missing something — a nonlinearity, an unmodeled coupling, a physical effect that your governing equation ignores. The third type is measurement error: the residuals are randomly scattered but are consistently offset from zero beyond what the uncertainty band predicts. This points to a systematic bias in the measurement — a miscalibrated sensor, a ground loop, or a systematic setup error — rather than a model problem. Each type has a different remedy, and confusing them leads to the wrong fix.

slide 3: Residuals as the Diagnostic Signal
(Image: Four residual plots in a two-by-two grid, each showing residuals (y-axis: prediction minus measurement) vs. an independent variable (x-axis). Plot 1: "Random scatter — within uncertainty band." Residuals distributed randomly, all bounded by dashed ±σ lines. Caption: "Consistent with measurement noise — model may be adequate." Plot 2: "Systematic linear trend." Residuals increase linearly with the independent variable — clear slope. Caption: "Model missing a first-order physical effect." Plot 3: "Nonlinear systematic pattern." Residuals curve upward at both ends and are near zero at the center. Caption: "Model misses nonlinearity or boundary effect." Plot 4: "Constant bias." Residuals are randomly scattered but consistently displaced above zero beyond the uncertainty band. Caption: "Systematic measurement error — not a model problem." All four plots use the same axis scales for direct comparison. The dashed uncertainty band is present in all four plots as a reference.)
Talking Point: These four residual plots are the interpretive toolkit for your Tier 2 data analysis. When you plot your residuals — prediction minus measurement, for every data point — the shape of that plot is the primary diagnostic signal. Random scatter within the uncertainty band is good news for the model: it means the structure of the physics is probably right, and the scatter you see is consistent with the measurement precision you quantified in the prediction document. A systematic linear trend means the model is missing a physical effect that scales with the independent variable — if you are testing a structural beam and the residuals increase with applied load, the model may be missing compliance or geometric nonlinearity. A curved pattern — near zero at intermediate conditions, large at the extremes — is a classic signature of a small-deflection or small-angle assumption breaking down at the limits of the operating range. And a constant bias means you have a systematic measurement error: a sensor that needs recalibration, or a zero-offset that was never corrected. Every one of these patterns has a specific physical interpretation, and your job in the Tier 2 analysis is to name which one you see and explain what it implies.

slide 4: Parametric Uncertainty — When the Form Is Right but the Numbers Are Wrong
(Image: A side-by-side comparison of two model predictions vs. the same experimental data. Left panel: "Original prediction" — a dashed prediction curve that consistently overestimates the measured data by a roughly constant multiplicative factor. The residuals at the bottom are flat (no trend) but consistently negative — indicating the model overpredicts everywhere. Right panel: "Revised prediction after parameter correction" — the prediction curve after the stiffness parameter is corrected using the data as a reference; the revised curve passes through the data scatter within uncertainty bounds. A caption reads: "Parametric uncertainty diagnosis: systematic bias with no trend → correct the parameter, not the model structure." The two panels have the same axis scales, with measured data shown as scatter points in both.)
Talking Point: When the residuals are flat — no trend, consistently negative or positive by a roughly constant amount — you have a parametric error. The model's structure is correct: it can produce the right shape of response, but it is using the wrong numerical value for one of the input parameters. In E2, the most common source of this pattern is an input parameter that you estimated from a textbook or datasheet that turns out to be different from the actual hardware — a material stiffness that has manufacturing variation, a mass that was estimated from a nominal drawing rather than measured directly, an aerodynamic coefficient that is sensitive to surface finish. The diagnostic step is to ask: which parameter, if adjusted by a physically plausible amount, would close the gap between the model and the data? If a small adjustment to one parameter brings the curves into agreement, that is strong evidence that the model form is correct and the parameter is the issue. This distinction matters because the fix for a parametric error — better parameter characterization — is completely different from the fix for a model-form error.

slide 5: Model-Form Error — When the Structure Is Wrong
(Image: A single graph showing a physical model prediction curve (linear, monotonically increasing) and experimental data scatter (which follows the prediction at low values of the independent variable but deviates nonlinearly at high values — the data bends upward, suggesting stiffening behavior not in the model). A residual plot below the main graph shows the characteristic systematic trend: residuals near zero at low independent variable values, growing positive at high values. An annotation points to the residual trend: "Systematic positive trend above x = 3 N — model missing geometric stiffening." A second annotation at the top: "This gap is not noise. The uncertainty band is shown. The trend is outside it." The uncertainty band (dashed lines) is present in both the main plot and the residual plot for reference.)
Talking Point: Model-form error is the most informative type of discrepancy because it is telling you something true about the physics that your model does not capture. When the residuals show a systematic trend — they increase with the independent variable, or they are consistently of one sign in one operating regime and the opposite sign in another — the model cannot produce that pattern through any choice of parameters. The model is structurally incomplete. In the example here, the model predicts a linear response and the data agrees at small values, but at larger values the data stiffens in a way the linear model cannot follow. The residuals grow systematically. This is a textbook signature of geometric nonlinearity: the linear beam model assumes small displacements, and at large displacements the geometry changes enough to increase effective stiffness. The model is not wrong in the absolute sense — it is inadequate beyond the small-displacement regime, for the intended use of predicting behavior at large loads. This is exactly the kind of diagnostic information that makes a model more useful: you now know the regime in which the linear model is adequate and the regime in which a geometric nonlinearity term is required. The same pattern appears in aerospace contexts: measured drag coefficient shows a positive trend with Reynolds number that the model does not predict — this is a model-form error indicating that the laminar-to-turbulent transition is not captured in the model's drag expression. Different domain, same diagnostic logic.

slide 6: Boundary Condition Realism — The Assumption You Did Not Know You Were Making
(Image: A two-panel illustration comparing two structural configurations. Left panel: "Idealized model: fully fixed boundary condition." Shows a cantilever beam with the left end drawn as a rigid, built-in wall, with the label "Model assumption: zero rotation and zero displacement at support." Right panel: "Real experimental setup: imperfect fixity." Shows the same beam but the support has a small angular deviation — the label reads "Actual boundary: slight rotation compliance — the support is not perfectly rigid." A comparison of residuals from each assumption is shown below as two short residual plots: the rigid assumption produces residuals with a systematic trend, while a flexible-support model reduces the trend substantially. Caption: "Boundary condition assumptions are often the dominant source of model-form error in structural experiments.")
Talking Point: One of the most common sources of model-form error in structural and mechanical experiments is not the governing equation itself — it is the boundary conditions. When you build a model, you make assumptions about how the system is supported, loaded, and constrained. In a textbook, boundaries are perfect: a fixed support is perfectly rigid, a pinned joint has zero friction, a load is applied at a mathematical point. In a real experiment, none of these idealizations are exactly true. A support that is intended to be fully fixed may have slight rotational compliance. A joint that is supposed to be pinned may have friction that introduces a restoring moment. These deviations from the ideal boundary condition appear in the residuals as systematic trends — typically largest near the boundary, decreasing with distance from it. When you see this pattern, the diagnostic question is: which of my boundary condition assumptions is least realistic? If a small adjustment to the boundary stiffness or the load application point closes the residual trend, that is the culprit. Naming this in your Tier 2 analysis — identifying which assumption was violated and what it implies for the model — is the highest-quality type of discrepancy analysis.

slide 7: Measurement Error vs. Model Error — A Case for Uncertainty Bounds
(Image: A comparison of two scenarios, presented as two MATLAB scatter plots side by side. Left: "Scenario A — discrepancy within uncertainty bounds." Measured data scatter plotted around a model prediction line. Horizontal dashed uncertainty lines at ±σ. All data points within the band. Caption: "No grounds to claim model failure — discrepancy is consistent with measurement precision." Right: "Scenario B — systematic bias beyond uncertainty bounds." Same model prediction line, but measured data is consistently below it, outside the uncertainty band at nearly every point. Caption: "Systematic bias: not random scatter. Source investigation required." Below the two panels, a question prompt: "Is the cause of the bias in the model, the measurement, or both? How would you distinguish?" A dotted arrow between the panels labeled "The uncertainty band is your null hypothesis — it defines what random scatter looks like.")
Talking Point: The uncertainty band you computed in your prediction document is not just a formality — it is your null hypothesis. It answers the question: what would the residuals look like if the only differences between prediction and data were due to measurement noise at the level you estimated? If the residuals are within the band, you cannot claim a meaningful disagreement — the data is consistent with your model within the precision of your measurement. If the residuals are consistently outside the band, something is wrong in a non-random way. The question then is: is the systematic bias coming from the model or from the measurement? To distinguish them, you need to ask whether the bias is constant across different operating conditions, different sensors, or different setups. If you repeat the measurement with a different sensor type or a different calibration reference and the bias shifts, it is a measurement error. If the bias is consistent regardless of how you measure, it is more likely a model error. This is exactly why the calibration status review — which we will cover in Lecture 2 today — is a required section of the Tier 2 group report: it is the systematic investigation of whether your measurement has a known bias source.

slide 8: Using Discrepancy to Improve the Model — The Experimentally Informed Model
(Image: A four-stage circular process diagram. Stage 1 (top): "Predictive model — physical equations, committed prediction." Arrow to Stage 2 (right): "Experiment — data collected." Arrow to Stage 3 (bottom): "Discrepancy analysis — classify residual pattern as parametric, model-form, or measurement." Arrow to Stage 4 (left): "Model revision proposal — specific update to the model based on what the discrepancy revealed." Arrow back to Stage 1 labeled "Updated model — inform future predictions." The diagram is rendered in the course color scheme with a clean circular layout. A gold star icon appears on the Stage 4 box with the label: "This is the required deliverable in the Tier 2 report — not just 'the model was close enough'.")
Talking Point: Returning to the experimentally informed model concept that was introduced at the start of E2: the goal of running a canned experiment in Phase II is not to validate the model in a binary sense and move on. The goal is to use the discrepancy analysis to propose a specific improvement to the model. Stage 1 is your committed prediction — you built a model, derived a prediction, and committed to it. Stage 2 is the experiment — you collected data. Stage 3 is the discrepancy analysis — you classified the residual pattern as parametric uncertainty, model-form error, or measurement error, and you named the physical cause. Stage 4 is the model revision proposal: based on what the residual pattern revealed, what specific change to the model — a different boundary condition assumption, an added nonlinear term, a corrected parameter value — would reduce the discrepancy? This is what the Tier 2 report asks you to provide. Not "the model was close enough" and not "there was some error in the experiment." A specific, physically justified proposal for how the model could be improved based on what the data told you.

slide 9: Applying the Taxonomy to Your Tier 2 Data
(Image: A structured worksheet-style layout. Three decision steps arranged vertically. Step 1: "Plot residuals — prediction minus measurement — versus the independent variable." Sub-question: "Are the residuals random or systematic?" Two branches: "Random → Step 2." "Systematic → Model-form error or boundary condition issue → name the physical effect." Step 2 (from "Random"): "Compare residual magnitude to uncertainty band." Sub-question: "Are residuals within the band?" Two branches: "Yes → Parametric uncertainty or adequate agreement." "No, consistently biased → Systematic measurement or parameter error." Step 3: "If constant bias beyond band — perform calibration status check." "Was the sensor within calibration interval? Was a zero offset correction applied?" A footer note reads: "This three-step process is the required analysis structure for Section 3A of the Tier 2 group report.")
Talking Point: Let me give you a concrete decision pathway for your Tier 2 analysis. Start by plotting the residuals — prediction minus measurement — as a function of your independent variable. Ask whether the pattern is random or systematic. If you see a clear trend, stop there: you have model-form error, and your job is to name the physical effect that could produce that trend. If the pattern looks random, the next question is whether the magnitude is within the uncertainty band you estimated in your prediction document. If yes, the model may be adequate for the intended use within the bounds of your measurement precision. If no — if the scatter is consistently outside the band, or if there is a consistent offset — you have a systematic error to investigate. The calibration status of your instruments is the first place to look, which is exactly why the calibration status review is Section 3C of your group report and not an optional appendix. These three steps are the core of Section 3A of the Tier 2 report, and we will work through them together in the lab session today.

slide 10: A Model That Disagrees Informatively Is More Valuable Than One That Agrees Approximately
(Image: A two-row comparison. Row 1 header: "Case A — approximate agreement, no diagnostic information." Shows a prediction curve overlaid on data scatter that roughly matches everywhere, residuals small and without trend. A note reads: "Conclusion: 'Model is close enough.' What was learned: nothing specific." Row 2 header: "Case B — structured disagreement, specific diagnostic." Shows a prediction curve that clearly overestimates at high values, with residuals showing a systematic trend. A note reads: "Conclusion: 'Model overestimates stiffening at large deflections — geometric nonlinearity not captured.' What was learned: the regime of model adequacy, a specific model improvement target, and the physical mechanism responsible." A banner at the bottom: "Case B is more scientifically valuable even though the model is 'less accurate.' The structure of the disagreement is the information.")
Talking Point: I want to leave you with a reframing that I think is genuinely important for how you approach engineering work. A model that agrees within uncertainty bounds has demonstrated adequacy — that is a success. A structured disagreement is a different kind of success: it reveals specifically where the model breaks down and why. Both outcomes have value; only one points toward model improvement. Approximate agreement everywhere means you learned that the model is not obviously wrong — you have limited information about where it breaks down, under what conditions, or why. Structured disagreement means you have learned something specific: the model is missing geometric stiffening above a certain deflection, or the aerodynamic drag coefficient is approximately 30% higher than the textbook value at these Reynolds numbers, or the support compliance reduces the apparent natural frequency by 15% compared to the fixed-end assumption. These are specific, actionable findings that make the model more useful for its intended purpose. The test engineer who delivers a report saying "the model is close enough" has done less valuable work than the one who delivers a report saying "the model is adequate within this operating regime, and here is the specific modification required to extend its validity." That is what Phase II is teaching you to do.

slide 11: Closure
Discrepancy between a model and experimental data is not embarrassment — it is information, and the structure of the discrepancy is the diagnostic signal. Parametric uncertainty produces random scatter within the uncertainty band and calls for better parameter characterization. Model-form error produces systematic trends in the residuals and calls for identifying the missing physical effect. Systematic measurement error produces consistent bias beyond the uncertainty band and calls for a calibration status review. The uncertainty band you computed in your prediction document is not a formality — it is your null hypothesis, defining what random scatter looks like. A model that disagrees in a structured way is telling you something true about the physics, and a test engineer who can read that structure and translate it into a specific model improvement proposal is delivering value that cannot be replaced by running more trials or using a better sensor. In Lecture 2 this week, we will go deeper on calibration — not just as a procedure, but as a source of uncertainty that propagates into your measurement outcome and must be documented to support a credible validation claim.
