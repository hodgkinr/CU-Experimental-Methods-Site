Experiment 1 — Lifting Gas (Helium) Balloon

Part 1: Achieve Neutral Buoyancy

Goal: Determine the payload mass (paperclips) required to hold the balloon in a stable vertical position — neither rising nor falling.

  1. Gather all balloon materials (balloon, string, paperclips). Using the PILOT lab mass scale, record the mass of each component separately before filling the balloon.
  2. With the assistance of a lab technician, fill the balloon with helium gas. Tie a string to the bottom with a loop to attach paperclips.
  3. Add paperclips one by one until neutral buoyancy is achieved (balloon holds position, not moving up or down).
  4. Record the final mass of the string and paperclips (payload mass at neutral buoyancy).
  5. Draw a free-body diagram of the system identifying all forces (buoyant force up; weights of envelope, gas, and ballast down).
  6. Record local ambient temperature and pressure from PILOT instrumentation or the NCAR website.

[SETUP_PHOTO NEEDED: Helium balloon setup — balloon with string and paperclip ballast, scale, and PILOT instrumentation visible]

Part 2: Modify Buoyancy with Heat

Goal: Make the balloon negatively buoyant by adding mass, then use heat to restore neutral buoyancy. Observe and characterize the thermodynamic process.

  1. Using the same configuration from Part 1, add more paperclips until the balloon is just negatively buoyant — it should fall slowly.
  2. Hold the balloon briefly in front of a heater to allow heat transfer to the gas.

[CAUTION: Do not hold the balloon too close to or for too long — risk of melting the mylar skin]

  1. Release the balloon and observe its behavior. Record observations.
  2. Define the thermal system and describe thermodynamically the processes taking place (processes and energy conversions).
  3. Use dimensional analysis to estimate the volume of the balloon while it is still at the elevated temperature.

Part 3: Estimate Mass of Helium

Goal: Estimate the mass of helium inside the balloon using two independent methods, then compare.

  1. Record the mass of the payload (string, paperclips, etc.).
  2. Estimate the volume of the balloon using rulers and/or other techniques with the balloon at room temperature. Compare this to your dimensional analysis estimate from Part 2 (near heater).
  3. Using the neutrally buoyant force balance (FBD from Part 1) and thermodynamic properties, estimate the required mass of helium to achieve buoyancy. [STUDENT TASK — not pre-solved; students derive from force balance in ASEN 3501]
  4. Using the measured volume and the Ideal Gas Law (P, V, T at room conditions), estimate the mass of helium independently. [STUDENT TASK — not pre-solved]
  5. Compare both estimates. Compute the total mass budget and the fractional percent of each balloon system component relative to total system mass.

Known pitfall: Volume estimates from ruler measurements are sensitive to assumed geometry. Document assumptions (e.g., sphere vs. oblate sphere) explicitly.

Experiment 2 — Mini Hot Air Balloon (Sky Lantern)

Part 1: Familiarize with Test Stand

Goal: Understand the test stand components and how the data acquisition system reports measurements.

  1. Identify all components of the test stand: sky lantern, heat gun, string, thermocouple array, differential pressure sensor, DAQ system, and the LabVIEW temperature sensor VI on the lab station computer.
  2. Review thermocouple color coding as displayed on the VI — each color corresponds to a specific thermocouple channel and location.
  3. Confirm the differential pressure sensor connection — it measures internal balloon pressure minus room pressure.
  4. Review the Sensor Supplemental Document. [MISSING: ASEN2802_Sensor_Informational_Packet.pdf is present in source directory as a binary PDF; content must be extracted and made available to students before this step can be completed as written]

Part 2: Estimate Filled Volume

Goal: Obtain an initial geometric volume estimate of the fully inflated balloon.

  1. Turn the heat gun to low and inflate the balloon using room temperature air until it takes shape.
  2. Using a tape measure, take geometric measurements (diameter, height, or other relevant dimensions depending on balloon shape).
  3. Calculate and record the estimated filled volume in ft³. [STUDENT TASK — geometric calculation from measurements]

Known pitfall: Balloon shape is irregular. Agree on a geometric approximation model (e.g., sphere, cylinder + cone) and document the assumption.

Part 3: Observe Temperature at Neutral Buoyancy

Goal: Record thermocouple temperatures and differential pressure at the moment the balloon achieves neutral buoyancy.

  1. Start the temperature sensor VI before beginning the experiment — data logging must be running before filling starts.
  2. Start the flow of room temperature air (heat gun on low) until the balloon begins to take shape.
  3. Switch the heat gun to hot to begin flowing hot air into the balloon and warm the internal air.
  4. Monitor the thermocouple temperature channels on the VI as the balloon heats.
  5. Also monitor the differential pressure sensor.
  6. When the balloon achieves neutral buoyancy (begins to lift), record:
  • All individual thermocouple temperatures [°C or K]
  • Differential pressure at that moment [Pa]
  • Any notes on which thermocouples are responding and which lag

Known pitfall: Thermocouples at different positions in the balloon will read different temperatures — the average internal temperature is not trivial to determine. Record all channels individually.

Common Mistakes Across Both Experiments

  • Failing to record component masses before filling — do all pre-fill measurements first.
  • Not noting ambient T and P at time of experiment — these values are required for the Ideal Gas Law calculations.
  • Starting the LabVIEW VI after the balloon is already being filled — VI must be running before heating starts.
  • Melting the mylar skin by holding it too close to the heater.

Model / Derivation

Theoretical Derivations — Buoyancy Balloon: High Altitude Vehicles

Lab: Buoyancy Balloon — High Altitude Vehicles Source: ASEN 2802: Aerospace Laboratory, CU Boulder, Spring 2025 Audience: Student or TA working through the theory

Background: Lighter-Than-Air Vehicles

There are two practical methods of achieving buoyant flight:

  1. Lifting gas balloon: Fill an envelope with a gas lighter than air (e.g., helium, hydrogen). The gas is denser than vacuum but less dense than air — the net upward buoyant force exceeds the weight of the system.
  2. Hot air balloon: Heat the air inside an envelope to reduce its density. A sufficient reduction in internal air density causes the total system weight to fall below the weight of displaced air.

Modern high-altitude research balloons (> 30 km) are deployed by NASA to carry scientific payloads above atmospheric interference. Two balloon types are relevant:

  • Zero-pressure balloons: Internal and external pressures equalize; volume varies with altitude. Example: Red Bull Stratos.
  • Super-pressure balloons: Internal pressure exceeds external; volume is fixed at design altitude. Example: Google Project Loon.

The same governing physical principles (Archimedes' Principle, Ideal Gas Law, Standard Atmosphere) apply to both types. The distinction lies in structural design and altitude control strategy.

References:

  • Anderson, J.D., Introduction to Flight, Chapter 3 (Standard Atmosphere)
  • MathWorks, atmoscoesa — COESA 1976 Standard Atmosphere implementation

Section 1 — Archimedes' Principle and Buoyant Force

The buoyant force on a body fully submerged in a fluid equals the weight of the displaced fluid:

$$F_b = \rho_{\text{air}} \cdot V_{\text{balloon}} \cdot g$$

where:

  • $\rho_{\text{air}}$: density of ambient air [kg/m³]
  • $V_{\text{balloon}}$: volume of the balloon [m³]
  • $g$: gravitational acceleration = 9.81 m/s² (assumed constant with altitude)

Neutral buoyancy condition: The buoyant force exactly equals the total weight of the system:

$$F_b = (m_{\text{envelope}} + m_{\text{gas}} + m_{\text{ballast}}) \cdot g$$

Simplifying (dividing through by $g$):

$$\rho_{\text{air}} \cdot V = m_{\text{envelope}} + m_{\text{gas}} + m_{\text{ballast}} \quad \text{[kg]}$$

Free-body diagram: Buoyant force acts upward. Gravitational weight of each component (envelope, gas, ballast/payload) acts downward.

The same framework applies to both helium and hot air balloons — only the gas properties differ. For helium, $m_{\text{gas}}$ is small but non-zero. For hot air, $m_{\text{gas}}$ is the mass of heated air retained inside (which decreases as temperature increases at fixed volume).

Assumption: Constant $g$ with altitude. Valid for altitudes below ~100 km where the fractional change in $g$ is less than 3%.

Section 2 — Ideal Gas Law

The mass-specific (intensive) form of the Ideal Gas Law:

$$P = \rho \cdot R \cdot T$$

Rearranged for density:

$$\rho = \frac{P}{R \cdot T}$$

where:

  • $P$: absolute pressure [Pa]
  • $\rho$: gas density [kg/m³]
  • $R$: specific gas constant [J/(kg·K)]
  • $T$: absolute temperature [K]

Gas constants:

  • Helium: $R_{\text{He}} = 2077$ J/(kg·K)
  • Air: $R_{\text{air}} = 287$ J/(kg·K)

Applications in this lab:

Estimate helium mass from balloon volume at known T and P: $$m_{\text{He}} = \frac{P \cdot V}{R_{\text{He}} \cdot T}$$

Estimate volume change when heated at constant mass and pressure: Volume increases proportionally with temperature — $V \propto T$ at constant $P$ and $m$.

Estimate internal hot air density as a function of temperature: $$\rho_{\text{hot}} = \frac{P_{\text{atm}}}{R_{\text{air}} \cdot T_{\text{inside}}}$$

Section 3 — Standard Atmosphere Model (COESA 1976)

The 1976 COESA Standard Atmosphere defines pressure $P(h)$, density $\rho(h)$, and temperature $T(h)$ as functions of geometric altitude $h$.

MATLAB implementation: MathWorks Aerospace Toolbox function atmoscoesa(h) returns [T, a, P, rho] at altitude $h$ [m].

Reference: Anderson, Introduction to Flight, Chapter 3.

This model is used in both balloon analyses to determine how atmospheric conditions change with altitude, which drives changes in balloon volume (lifting gas) or required internal temperature (hot air).

Assumption: Standard atmosphere is representative. Real atmospheric profiles deviate from standard — this is a modeling simplification.

Section 4 — Lifting Gas Balloon Volume vs. Altitude

Setup: A helium balloon floats upward into decreasing ambient pressure. The envelope is modeled as infinitely stretchable (zero structural constraint on expansion).

Governing assumption: Helium mass is constant; the envelope exerts no stress or strain force on the gas; pressure equilibrium is instantaneous.

At any altitude $h$, the internal helium pressure equals the ambient pressure $P(h)$. By the Ideal Gas Law at constant mass:

$$V(h) = \frac{m_{\text{He}} \cdot R_{\text{He}} \cdot T(h)}{P(h)}$$

where $T(h)$ and $P(h)$ are from the COESA Standard Atmosphere.

Expected behavior: As altitude increases, $P(h)$ decreases faster than $T(h)$ decreases, so $V(h)$ increases — the balloon expands with altitude.

MATLAB analysis: Plot $V(h)$ for $h = 0$ to 30,000 m using atmoscoesa. [STUDENT TASK — not pre-solved]

Assumptions to justify:

  • Infinitely stretchable envelope
  • No stress/strain forces on shell
  • Constant helium mass (no leakage)
  • Constant $g$

Governing assumption: Fixed volume; no pressure gradient between inside and outside of balloon ($P_{\text{inside}} = P_{\text{atm}}(h)$).

Maximum altitude: When $T_{\text{inside}}(h) = 522$ K (material failure temperature of the balloon envelope), the balloon has reached its maximum operational altitude.

3. Questions to Consider (verbatim from source)

Experiment 1 — Section 4.2

  1. If you were to make the party balloon neutrally buoyant in water, what would be the required mass of the payload?
  2. What is your estimate of the amount of helium in the balloon using force balance? How does that estimate compare to an estimate of the balloon volume?
  3. How sensitive are these estimates of amount of helium to uncertainties in the measured quantities (material mass, payload mass, temperature, pressure, distance measurements, etc.)?

Experiment 2 — Section 4.3

  1. Would the air temperature required to achieve positive buoyancy increase/decrease at standard temperature and pressure? Atop Vail Pass (67.9 kPa and 0°C)?
  2. Using the derived theoretical model, what average temperature of air is needed for the balloon to achieve positive buoyancy?
  3. The actual average temperature in the hot air balloon is very difficult to estimate accurately — using the 4 thermocouple measurements how well does the experiment agree with the theoretical model?
  4. We assume no pressure difference between the room and the inside of the hot air balloon — is this a good assumption, why or why not?
  5. How sensitive is the estimate of hot air temperature to uncertainties in the measured quantities (material mass, payload mass, volume estimate, room temperature and pressure, etc.)?
