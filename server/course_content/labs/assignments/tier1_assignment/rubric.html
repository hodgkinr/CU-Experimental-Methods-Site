ASEN 3501 — Tier 1 Lab Assignment: Student-Facing Rubric

Grading Criteria for All Three Phases

Assessment type: Phase 1 (written report, individual) + Phase 2 (prediction memo, individual) + Phase 3 (presentation, paired or group of 4) + Peer evaluations Overall outcome: Pass/fail for each phase independently. All three phases must be passed to satisfy the Tier 1 lab requirement.

Phase 1 — Forward Analysis Report

The Phase 1 report is graded on two equally weighted dimensions: technical completeness and argument quality. Technical completeness is about whether required elements are present and correct. Argument quality is about whether the reasoning that connects those elements is sound, explicit, and defensible.

A report that computes everything correctly but presents results without interpretation, or without explicit uncertainty bounds, does not demonstrate Phase 1 mastery.

Section-by-section criteria

SectionMinimum to passEvidence of excellence
Experimental designHypothesis is falsifiable (specific, physically motivated). Variables correctly identified. At least one confounding factor named and addressed.Confounding factor is non-obvious and the mitigation strategy is specific. Hypothesis is quantitative, not qualitative.
AMVF annotationAll major steps mapped to a framework node. Pre-lab plan and actual procedure compared — differences noted.Annotation reveals a genuine insight about the experiment (for example, where the workflow moves from mathematical to physical model).
Measurement recordAll measurements present with units and uncertainty. Uncertainty basis stated for each value.Uncertainty sources are distinguished clearly: resolution, manufacturer specification, and statistical variation.
Multi-sensor comparisonA named validation metric is applied. Threshold stated before comparison. Numerical conclusion stated.Student explains what the comparison reveals about sensor characteristics, not just whether they agree.
Uncertainty propagationPartial derivatives shown for the primary derived quantity. Dominant error sources ranked with numerical support.Student interprets the ranking and explains what would matter most for reducing uncertainty.
Model comparisonNamed validation metric and acceptance criterion applied. Discrepancy considered across random variation, known measurement bias, setup or installation effects, uncontrolled inputs, data-reduction error, parameter uncertainty, and model-form discrepancy.Preferred explanation is supported by evidence and at least one competing hypothesis is named.
Experimentally informed modelAt least one specific proposed model change, with physical reasoning.Proposed change is testable and includes what additional measurement would confirm or refute it.
MATLAB figuresAll required plot elements present. Caption states the takeaway explicitly.Figures work together as a coherent visual argument across the report.
Plain-language summaryAccessible to a non-engineer, with no unexplained jargon, and placed in the introduction or conclusion.A non-engineer who reads only this section would still understand what was done and why it matters.

Phase 2 — Prediction Memo

Phase 2 is graded on reasoning quality, not prediction accuracy. A prediction that turns out to be wrong, derived from sound physical reasoning and committed uncertainty analysis, is more valuable than a prediction that turns out to be right from vague intuition because it shows a deeper attempt at understanding the system.

SectionMinimum to passEvidence of excellence
Predicted system responseSpecific, quantitative prediction with committed values or ranges. Physically motivated.Prediction explains the expected shape of the time series or distribution, not just a single number.
Predicted uncertaintyPartial derivatives applied to sensor specifications. Result stated as value plus or minus uncertainty.Student identifies which input dominates and explains the implication for experiment design.
Predicted plot sketchSketch with labeled axes and uncertainty bounds. Committed before data release.Sketch anticipates trends, peaks, plateaus, or the noise floor.
Dominant uncertainty sourcesRanked with numerical support from propagation.Ranking is connected to a practical recommendation for what to improve first.
Acceptance criterionSpecific numerical threshold stated before data release.Criterion is physically motivated, not arbitrary.
Risk assessmentAt least one specific anomaly scenario identified with a physical basis.Student explains what anomalous data would look like and how to distinguish it from a genuine physical phenomenon.

Grading note on prediction accuracy: After data is released, students compare their prediction to the actual results in Phase 3. The Phase 2 grade is assigned based on the memo alone, before data release. Do not revise your reasoning retrospectively.

Phase 3 — Combined Presentation

The presentation is graded on two dimensions: your own presentation and your peer evaluations of others. Both are required to pass Phase 3.

Presentation rubric

SegmentMinimum to passEvidence of excellence
Segment 1 — ForwardResult presented with uncertainty. Experimentally informed model change stated.Visual comparison between data and model is shown and annotated with a validation metric.
Segment 2 — ReversePrediction compared to actual data using the pre-defined acceptance criterion. Discrepancy interpreted physically.Student explains what the discrepancy revealed that the forward analysis alone could not have shown.
Segment 3 — SynthesisAt least one cross-experiment insight that goes beyond saying both experiments show uncertainty.Synthesis connects to a broader principle or a design implication about the phenomenon, the sensors, or the experimental approach.
CommunicationClear structure, audience-accessible language, and effective figures.The audience learns something new and the presentation does not assume prior knowledge of the specific experiment.

Peer evaluation rubric

A peer evaluation is graded on quality, not generosity. You are not rewarded for giving a positive evaluation. You are rewarded for giving a specific and useful one.

CriterionMinimum to passEvidence of excellence
StrengthNamed with a specific reference to what was said or shown.Explains why the strength reflects real understanding.
WeaknessNamed with a specific reference to what was missing or incorrect.Connects the weakness to a missed learning objective, not just a presentation preference.
ImprovementConcrete and actionable so the presenter could implement it.Specific to the experiment or analysis, not generic advice.

Minimum quality threshold: An evaluation like “great presentation, maybe add more data” does not pass. An evaluation that points to a missing uncertainty bound, explains why that blocked the comparison, and recommends a specific fix does pass.

Overall Pass/Fail Summary

PhasePass condition
Phase 1All required sections present with no fundamental errors in uncertainty propagation or model comparison. Experimentally informed model proposal present and physically motivated.
Phase 2Prediction is committed, the derivation is traceable from sensor specifications, and the acceptance criterion is stated before data release.
Phase 3 — PresentationAll three segments delivered. At least one cross-experiment synthesis insight. Communication is audience-accessible.
Phase 3 — Peer evaluationAt least one evaluation meets the minimum quality threshold above.

A student who passes all four components has satisfied the Tier 1 lab requirement.
