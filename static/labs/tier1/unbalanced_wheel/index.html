Procedure — Unbalanced Wheel: Inertia Distribution and Rolling Dynamics

Overview

This lab investigates the dynamics of a cylindrical wheel rolling down a ramp under two configurations: balanced (no eccentric mass) and unbalanced (eccentric mass installed). Students build and validate a four-model hierarchy before arriving at the apparatus.

The lab has two distinct parts that must be done in sequence:

  1. Pre-Lab (before the lab session): Derive all four models and implement the MATLAB function. This must be complete before you handle the apparatus.
  2. In-Lab: Run the apparatus, collect data, and perform analysis.

Part 1 — Pre-Lab: Model Derivation and MATLAB Setup

[STUDENT TASK — derive all four models and implement MATLAB function before the lab session]

Before arriving at the apparatus:

  • Plot expected ω vs. θ for all four models (0 ≤ θ ≤ 15 rad) before starting the experiment

Model / Derivation

Model Derivation — Unbalanced Wheel: Inertia Distribution and Rolling Dynamics

Section 1 — Physical Setup and Coordinate System

A cylindrical wheel (mass M, radius R, radius of gyration κ) rolls down a ramp (slope angle β) without slipping. A trailing support apparatus (mass M₀) is rigidly attached to the wheel; it translates but does not rotate. An eccentric mass (mass m) can be bolted to the wheel at radius r from the wheel center.

Coordinate system:

  • θ = rotation angle, measured clockwise from the perpendicular vector into the ramp
  • θ = 0 defined as the position when the wheel starts moving from rest
  • For unbalanced trials: eccentric mass is positioned perpendicular to the ramp surface at θ = 0

Rolling constraint (no-slip): v_cm = R · ω

where v_cm is the velocity of the wheel center and ω = dθ/dt.

System Parameters:

SymbolValueUnitsDescription
M11.7kgMass of cylindrical wheel
M₀0.7kgMass of trailing support apparatus
m3.4kgMass of eccentric (extra) mass
R0.235mRadius of cylinder
κ0.203mRadius of gyration of wheel
IMκ²kg·m²Moment of inertia of wheel about its center
β5.5degRamp slope angle
r0.178mDistance from wheel center to eccentric mass center
r_extra0.019mRadius of eccentric mass (solid rod, for rigid body model)
g9.81m/s²Gravitational acceleration

Section 2 — Work-Energy Principle (General Form)

The work-energy principle applied to the complete system:

W_net = ΔKE = KE_final − KE_initial

Starting from rest: KE_initial = 0 for all trials (θ = 0, ω = 0 at start).

The kinetic energy of the rolling system includes:

  • Rotational KE of the wheel: (1/2) · I · ω²
  • Translational KE of the wheel center: (1/2) · M · v_cm²
  • Translational KE of the trailing apparatus: (1/2) · M₀ · v_cm²

Using the rolling constraint v_cm = R · ω, all KE terms can be expressed as functions of ω.

For the unbalanced configuration, the eccentric mass adds both translational and rotational KE (the latter only in MODEL_4).

Work done by gravity = (total mass) · g · (vertical displacement of center of mass) Work done by friction moment = −Q · θ (where Q is the constant negative moment)

Section 3 — MODEL_1: Balanced Wheel, No Friction

Configuration: Wheel and trailing apparatus only; no eccentric mass; no friction moment.

Assumptions:

  • Cylinder rolls without slipping
  • Trailing apparatus translates but does not rotate
  • No friction at the axle

[STUDENT TASK — derive ω(θ) using work-energy. Result is a closed-form expression for angular velocity as a function of angular position.]

Parameters used: M, M₀, R, κ, β, g

Section 4 — MODEL_2: Balanced Wheel, Constant Friction Moment

Modification of MODEL_1: Add a constant negative moment −Q applied to the shaft of the wheel. Q represents friction at the axle; its value is unknown and must be determined empirically.

Assumptions: Same as MODEL_1 plus constant negative moment −Q.

[STUDENT TASK — derive ω(θ, Q) using work-energy with the friction moment included.]

Empirical determination of Q:

  • Q is not known from first principles; it must be fit to balanced experimental data
  • Method: try 5 or more values of Q; identify which value best matches the experimental ω(θ) curve for both balanced trials
  • The same value of Q must work for both balanced trials
  • Q determined here is then carried forward into MODEL_3 and MODEL_4 unchanged

Parameters used: M, M₀, R, κ, β, g, Q (empirically determined)

Section 5 — MODEL_3: Unbalanced Wheel, Eccentric Mass as Particle

Modification of MODEL_2: Add the eccentric mass m at radius r, modeled as a point particle (all mass concentrated at a point — no rotational inertia about its own center).

Assumptions: Same as MODEL_2 plus eccentric mass modeled as particle at radius r.

The eccentric mass center moves in a circle of radius r about the wheel center. Its velocity has two components that depend on both ω and the current angular position θ.

[STUDENT TASK — derive ω(θ, Q) using work-energy. The extra mass at radius r contributes both translational KE and additional gravitational PE terms that vary with θ.]

Parameters used: M, M₀, m, R, r, κ, β, g, Q (from MODEL_2)

Section 6 — MODEL_4: Unbalanced Wheel, Eccentric Mass as Rigid Body

Modification of MODEL_3: Model the eccentric mass as a rigid body (solid cylinder/rod) rather than a point particle. This adds a rotational kinetic energy term for the eccentric mass rotating about its own center of mass.

Moment of inertia of the eccentric mass about its own center: I_extra = (1/2) · m · r_extra² where r_extra = 0.019 m

[STUDENT TASK — derive ω(θ, Q) including the rotational KE of the eccentric mass. Compare with MODEL_3 — note the additional inertia term and assess its significance.]

Parameters used: M, M₀, m, R, r, r_extra, κ, β, g, Q (from MODEL_2)

Section 7 — Residual Analysis Framework

After fitting models to data, quantify the agreement using residual statistics.

Residual definition: ε_i = ω_observed,i − ω_predicted(θ_i)

Important: Do not take the absolute value of residuals. A non-zero mean residual indicates systematic bias in the model.

Evaluation rule: Evaluate the model at the experimentally measured θ values — not at a uniform grid. The residuals must be computed at the same θ_i values where ω_observed,i was measured.

Statistics to compute:

StatisticFormulaInterpretation
Mean residualmean(ε) = (1/N) Σ ε_iNon-zero → systematic bias
Standard deviationσ(ε) = std(ε)Magnitude of random error
Uncertainty of meanσ_mean = σ / √NConfidence in mean estimate
Ncount of observationsNumber of data points in reliable range
Outlier countcount(ε_i> 3σ)Number of residuals beyond 3σ

Table to produce: One row per model per trial; columns: σ, mean(ε), σ/√N, N, count(|ε_i| > 3σ)

Histogram: Plot histogram of residuals scaled by σ (i.e., ε_i / σ); superimpose a standard normal distribution N(0,1) for comparison. This reveals whether errors are normally distributed.

References

  • Taylor, J.R. (1997). An Introduction to Error Analysis. University Science Books. (Residual statistics and error analysis framework)
  • Meriam, J.L. & Kraige, L.G. Engineering Mechanics: Dynamics. (Work-energy methods for rigid bodies)

4. Adaptation Notes for ASEN 3501

Placement: Strong E2 candidate (Predictive Modeling & Comparative Validation phase). Could also be used in late E1. Requires solid dynamics knowledge (work-energy, rolling without slip) and MATLAB skills; the model hierarchy and residual analysis are at the right level for 3501's comparative validation phase.

AMVF alignment: The four-model hierarchy directly demonstrates the AMVF concept of incrementally informing a model:

  • Model 1 → idealized baseline
  • Model 2 → empirical refinement (friction from data)
  • Model 3 → added physics (eccentric mass, particle approximation)
  • Model 4 → refined physics (eccentric mass, rigid body)

This is a concrete, well-structured example of using experimental data to improve a model — a strong CLO 3 anchor.

CLO 2 anchor: The residual analysis (mean, std dev, uncertainty of mean, histogram, outlier count) is the most complete statistical analysis task among the source labs reviewed in this compilation series (aeroelasticity, bouncing ball, buoyancy balloon, spin modules, unbalanced wheel). It maps directly to CLO 2 and to Coleman & Steele's framework for model-experiment comparison.
