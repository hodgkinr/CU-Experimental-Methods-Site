Part 1 — Experiment Overview

What Is Being Measured

The coefficient of restitution e characterizes how much kinetic energy is retained after an impact. This lab measures e for a bouncing ball using three independent methods derived from first principles. The three methods differ in which physical quantities are measured:

  • Method 1 (Height of Bounce): Measure heights of successive bounces.
  • Method 2 (Time of Bounce): Measure the duration of adjacent individual bounces.
  • Method 3 (Time to Stop): Measure the total time from first impact until the ball stops

bouncing, plus the initial drop height.

Each method yields an independent estimate of e. Students derive the governing equations for all three methods before running any experiments.

Ball Characteristics to Record

Before any trials, measure and record the following for each ball used:

  • Mass (g)
  • Diameter (mm or cm)
  • Material and surface type (rubber, plastic, etc.)
  • Any visible deformities or wear

Record these for both the primary ping-pong ball and the second ball of your choosing.

Part 2 — Guidance for Designing Each Measurement Method

This lab does not prescribe a specific measurement procedure. Designing a measurement procedure for each method is a student task. The guidance below describes what must be measured and what factors to consider — not how to measure it.

Method 1: Height of Bounce

What to measure: The height of the ball's center of mass at its peak immediately after each bounce — i.e., the successive bounce heights h₁, h₂, ..., hₙ relative to the drop height h₀.

Possible approaches:

  • Mark initial drop height h₀ on a wall or ruler. After each bounce, observe or record peak

height visually or from video frame-by-frame.

  • Use a meter stick mounted vertically near the bounce point for visual reference.
  • Record on video and extract heights frame by frame in Tracker or Logger Pro.

Key error sources to consider:

  • Parallax error when reading height from the side
  • Difficulty identifying the exact moment of peak height
  • Ruler placement and resolution
  • Ball is not always released from exactly h₀

What constitutes one trial: One drop from h₀ with recording of all bounce heights until the ball effectively stops. Each trial yields one value of e per bounce pair (or the cumulative estimate using h₀ and hₙ).

Method 2: Time of Bounce

What to measure: The duration of each individual bounce — from impact to impact — for adjacent bounces tₙ and tₙ₋₁.

Possible approaches:

  • Use a stopwatch or timer with manual triggering at each impact (audio cue from bounce sound

is useful; visual trigger is harder).

  • Record on video and extract impact times from frame timestamps.
  • Use audio recording to identify impact events with higher time resolution.

Key error sources to consider:

  • Human reaction time dominates when using a stopwatch — this is a major error source
  • Video frame rate sets time resolution; 30 fps gives ±1/30 s per event
  • Distinguishing the exact moment of impact from the bounce sound

What constitutes one trial: Recording the time sequence t₁, t₂, ..., tₙ for one drop. Each adjacent pair (tₙ, tₙ₋₁) yields one estimate of e.

Method 3: Time to Stop

What to measure: The total time tₛ from first impact until the ball stops bouncing, plus the initial drop height h₀.

Possible approaches:

  • Start a stopwatch at first impact (triggered by sound or visual); stop when ball ceases

audible bouncing.

  • Define a threshold for "stopped" — e.g., bounces too small to hear or see — and note that

this threshold introduces a systematic bias.

  • Record on video to more precisely determine the final bounce.

Key error sources to consider:

  • Defining when the ball has "stopped" (ball may continue micro-bounces)
  • Reaction time at start and end of timing
  • Accuracy of h₀ measurement

What constitutes one trial: One drop from h₀ with recording of tₛ. Each trial yields one estimate of e.

Part 3 — Refinement and Second Ball

After completing preliminary experiments (10 trials × 3 methods):

  1. Analyze results and error for all three methods (see model derivations for error propagation).
  2. Identify which method produces the most consistent or lowest-error estimates.
  3. Refine the experimental procedure for that method — improve measurement setup, reduce error

sources identified in preliminary results.

  1. Collect at least 10 additional refined trials with the ping-pong ball using the best method.
  2. Using the same best method, collect 10 trials with a second ball of your choice.

After Tracking

Compute e from the video-extracted position and time data using Methods 1, 2, and 3. Compare these estimates to those obtained from manual measurement. Assess whether video tracking reduces error, and identify which error sources are eliminated vs. which remain.

Known Pitfalls / Common Mistakes

  • Not recording ball characteristics: Mass and diameter are required; measure these before

starting trials.

  • Releasing with spin: The model assumes no spin (direct central impact). Hold the ball

gently and release by opening fingers — do not throw or flick.

  • Inconsistent drop height: Mark h₀ clearly. Use a fixed reference point for each trial,

not a new visual estimate each time.

  • Stopwatch human reaction time: This is often the largest error source in Methods 2 and 3.

Design your measurement to minimize it (audio cue, video extraction, or two-person triggering).

  • Too few data points: Minimum is 10 trials per method. More is better for statistical

confidence.

  • Not defining "stopped": For Method 3, document your criterion for what counts as the

ball having stopped bouncing. Consistency within trials matters more than the exact threshold.

Theoretical Framework — Bouncing Ball: Coefficient of Restitution

Section 1 — Physical Setup and Variable Definitions

A ball is released from rest at a height h₀ above a flat, horizontal surface. The ball is modelled as a particle undergoing direct central impact with the surface — meaning the ball travels vertically with no spin, and the impact force acts along the line connecting the center of mass of the ball and the contact point.

This is Figure 1 / bb_diagram from both source versions — it is essential for understanding all three methods and must accompany any presentation of the theory.]

Modelling assumptions:

  • Ball modelled as a particle (no rotational dynamics)
  • No spin — direct central impact only
  • Horizontal surface is effectively infinitely massive (negligible velocity change of Earth)
  • Atmospheric drag neglected during free-flight phases
  • e is treated as constant across all bounces (same ball/surface interaction each time)

Section 2 — General Coefficient of Restitution

For a direct central impact between two bodies A and B, the coefficient of restitution is defined as:


 v_B' - v_A'
e = ─────────────
 v_A - v_B

where:

  • v_A, v_B = velocities of bodies A and B before impact (positive upward)
  • v_A', v_B' = velocities of bodies A and B after impact (positive upward)
  • Body A = Earth (surface); Body B = ball

Because the Earth is effectively infinitely massive, its velocity before and after impact is negligible: v_A ≈ v_A' ≈ 0. This simplifies the equation to:


 v_B'
e = ──────
 -v_B

i.e., e is the ratio of the ball's outgoing speed to its incoming speed at each impact.

[STUDENT TASK — students derive all three method equations starting from this base definition, using energy conservation and kinematics to connect ball velocities to the observable quantities h₀, hₙ, tₙ, and tₛ.]

Section 3 — Method 1: Height of Bounce

Governing equation:


 ⎛ hₙ ⎞^(1/2) ⎛ hₙ ⎞^(1/2n)
e = ⎜────⎟ = ⎜────⎟
 ⎝hₙ₋₁⎠ ⎝ h₀ ⎠

The left form uses the ratio of adjacent bounce heights (nth to (n-1)th). The right form uses the nth bounce height relative to the initial drop height h₀.

Derivation:

[STUDENT TASK — derive from energy conservation and kinematic relationships:

  • Ball in free fall: velocity just before nth impact = sqrt(2g·hₙ₋₁)
  • Ball after nth impact (rising): velocity just after = sqrt(2g·hₙ)
  • Substitute into the general CoR definition: e = v_B' / (-v_B)
  • Simplify to obtain e = (hₙ/hₙ₋₁)^(1/2)
  • Extend to the cumulative form using e^n = (hₙ/h₀)^(1/2) to get e = (hₙ/h₀)^(1/2n)]

Sensitivity to measurement error:

[STUDENT TASK — derive via propagation of errors (see Taylor, "Introduction to Error Analysis"; ASEN 2012 notes):

  • For the adjacent-bounce form: propagate uncertainty in hₙ and hₙ₋₁ to uncertainty in e
  • Identify which quantity (height measurement) dominates the error
  • Express sensitivity as ∂e/∂hₙ and ∂e/∂hₙ₋₁]

Section 4 — Method 2: Time of Bounce

Governing equation:


 tₙ
e = ────
 tₙ₋₁

where tₙ is the duration of the nth bounce and tₙ₋₁ is the duration of the preceding bounce.

Derivation:

[STUDENT TASK — derive from kinematics of free-fall and bounce:

  • Ball leaves surface after nth bounce with velocity v_B' = sqrt(2g·hₙ)
  • Time for one complete bounce (up and down): tₙ = 2·v_B' / g = 2·sqrt(2hₙ/g)
  • Similarly tₙ₋₁ = 2·sqrt(2hₙ₋₁/g)
  • Form the ratio tₙ/tₙ₋₁ and simplify using e = (hₙ/hₙ₋₁)^(1/2) from Method 1
  • Result: e = tₙ/tₙ₋₁]

Sensitivity to measurement error:

[STUDENT TASK — propagate uncertainty in tₙ and tₙ₋₁ to uncertainty in e. Note: human reaction time is a significant and systematic error source for this method when using a manual stopwatch — students should identify this explicitly.]

Section 5 — Method 3: Time to Stop

Governing equation:


 tₛ - sqrt(2h₀/g)
e = ─────────────────
 tₛ + sqrt(2h₀/g)

where tₛ is the total time from first impact until the ball stops bouncing, and h₀ is the initial drop height.

Derivation:

[STUDENT TASK — derive by summing the geometric series of bounce times:

  • First fall time (from h₀ to first impact): t_fall = sqrt(2h₀/g)
  • Time of nth bounce: tₙ = 2·e^n·sqrt(2h₀/g) (using the relation developed in Method 2)
  • Total time tₛ = t_fall + sum of all bounce times (infinite geometric series)
  • Sum the series: sum = 2·e·sqrt(2h₀/g) / (1-e)
  • Total: tₛ = sqrt(2h₀/g) + 2·e·sqrt(2h₀/g) / (1-e)
  • Solve for e to obtain the Method 3 equation.]

Sensitivity to measurement error:

[STUDENT TASK — propagate uncertainty in tₛ and h₀ to uncertainty in e. Note: defining "stopped" introduces a systematic measurement bias — students should address this in their error analysis.]

Section 6 — Statistical Analysis Framework

This section describes the statistical analysis required for each method. The computations are student tasks in MATLAB; this section documents what is expected.

For each method and each ball, compute the following statistics across N trials:

StatisticSymbolFormula
Meanēē = (1/N) Σ eᵢ
MedianẽMiddle value of sorted {eᵢ}
Standard deviationσσ = sqrt[ (1/(N-1)) Σ (eᵢ - ē)² ]
Standard deviation of the meanσ_meanσ_mean = σ / sqrt(N)

The standard deviation of the mean (σ_mean) quantifies uncertainty in the mean estimate as a function of sample size N. It is distinct from σ, which describes spread of individual trial values.

Final estimate of e should be reported as: ē ± (some bound), with the bound justified by the error analysis and statistical results.

Predictive Model Value — Source and Interpretation

The typical CoR range for a regulation ping-pong ball on a hard floor is 0.85–0.90 (dimensionless). This value reflects an experimentally established material property for standard impact conditions — it is not derivable from the method equations above and cannot be predicted from first principles using the constant-e model alone.

For Phase 1 students: use this range as your predictive model anchor in Section 7. Treat e_predicted = 0.875 ± 0.025 (midpoint and half-range) and cite this file as the source.

For Phase 2 students: your Phase 2 experiment packet will include this same range. It is an appropriate basis for your predicted system response — but note the distinction: this is a material reference property, not a calibrated instrument output. State explicitly that your predicted e is based on published material behavior for this ball and surface type, and that the actual value may vary with temperature, ball age, and surface compliance.

peak height are the primary error sources. Ruler resolution is secondary to observer judgment error.

  • Time measurement (Methods 2 and 3): Human reaction time is typically 0.1–0.3 s and

is the dominant error source when using a manual stopwatch. Video tracking effectively eliminates this; reaction time error is replaced by frame-rate-limited time resolution.

  • Ball spin: The model assumes no spin (direct central impact). Real drops on hard

surfaces can introduce small rotational motion, particularly if the surface is not perfectly flat or the ball has surface irregularities. This is a known model limitation.

  • Definition of "stopped" (Method 3): There is no universal criterion for when a ball

has stopped bouncing. This introduces a systematic measurement bias. Students should define their criterion explicitly and apply it consistently across all trials.

  • Consistency of h₀: Releasing from a slightly different height each trial introduces

variability that is not captured in the statistical analysis unless h₀ is measured independently for each trial.

Units Convention

Equations 1 and 2 yield dimensionless e (ratio of lengths or times). Equation 3 requires h₀ in meters if g = 9.81 m/s² is used, or h₀ in feet if g = 32.2 ft/s² is used. Within-trial unit consistency is more important than which system is used — choose one and apply it throughout all calculations. Document units at the top of each MATLAB function.

4. Adaptation Notes for ASEN 3501

This lab maps well onto the E1 phase (Execution & Quantification) of ASEN 3501. It aligns closely with the "Are You Smarter Than a 5th Grader?" anchor concept — a deceptively simple experiment that demands rigorous analysis.

Scope

The original lab spans ~2–3 weeks for a group of 3 in a dynamics course. For 3501's individual or small-group structure, scope reduction may be appropriate:

  • Option A: Assign all three methods as the complete E1 experimental sequence. This

  • Option B: Assign one or two methods for a single week, treating the third as an

  • Recommendation: Retain all three methods if used in E1; the comparison between

methods IS the learning objective for uncertainty and experimental design.

Derivation Requirement

The original lab asks students to derive all three equations from first principles. This aligns directly with 3501's principle that students should own the theoretical framework before collecting data. Keep this requirement.

The derivation distinguishes this lab from a recipe-following exercise. It also sets up the error propagation task naturally: once students have derived the equations symbolically, deriving the sensitivity expressions is a straightforward next step.

Video Tracking (Tracker / Logger Pro)

The Tracker/Logger Pro component is a strong candidate for retention in 3501. Comparing manual timing to video-extracted timing directly demonstrates the effect of measurement tool selection on uncertainty — a core E1 concept. Specifically:

  • Manual stopwatch: dominated by human reaction time (~0.1–0.3 s per event)
  • Video extraction: dominated by frame rate (±1/30 s at 30 fps)
  • The difference in e estimates between these approaches quantifies the impact of instrument

choice — this is exactly what CLO 4 is preparing students to reason about, even though this lab uses no electronic sensors

Recommendation: Include the video tracking component. It is low-cost (free software, student's own phone) and directly motivates the "why does instrument choice matter" question.

No DAQ

This lab fills CLOs 1, 2, 3, and 5 without CLO 4. That is a feature — it makes it a clean early-course experiment that does not require lab hardware access. Place it before any electronics labs. Students who complete this lab will have the statistical vocabulary (mean, std, std_of_mean, error propagation, final estimate with bounds) needed for all subsequent instrumented experiments.

Multiple Balls

The comparison between ball types (ping-pong vs. student-chosen) is a natural entry point for discussing how material properties affect model assumptions. Consider retaining this: it is the first place in the course where students encounter the idea that e is not a universal constant but depends on the specific ball-surface pair.
