AI Literacy & Fluency

AI Literacy → AI Fluency Progression

AI literacy is knowing what AI is and what it can do. AI fluency is knowing when, how, and why to use it — and when not to. This course expects AI fluency, not just literacy.

The adoption/capability curve: AI capability is rising rapidly. Adoption by the general workforce is slower. The gap between capability and adoption is where human value currently lives. To remain valuable, you need to build foundations that AI cannot yet provide — and to use AI as a tool to amplify those foundations, not as a replacement for them.

_Diagram concept for lecture/slides: Draw two curves on a time axis. Curve 1 (AI Capability) — rising steeply. Curve 2 (Workforce Adoption) — rising more slowly, lagging behind. The gap between the two curves is labeled: "Human value zone." The message: right now, humans who can effectively use AI and evaluate its outputs have maximum value. Engineers who develop durable skills — V&V judgment, critique quality, physical intuition — will remain in that value zone as the curves converge._

Why authentic assessments? As AI gets better, the only assessments that reliably reveal real understanding are the ones AI cannot take for you: in-person oral assessments (you explain your reasoning to another human in real time), in-class exercises where you have no external tools, and the E3 oral defense. That is why those assessments are the backbone of this course.

What AI cannot do (yet): AI can generate technically plausible content. It cannot tell you whether that content is correct — without a human who deeply understands the domain. That verification skill is the most durable thing this course builds. It is also what engineering employers are increasingly paying for. Engineers whose primary role is alignment, verification, and validation will remain valuable as capability curves continue to rise.

The core question every student should keep in mind: "How will this task make me a valuable asset to a future employer?"

Profile of an AI-Ready Graduate — Culatta (ISTE+ASCD, 2025)

_Source: Richard Culatta, "Profile of an AI-Ready Graduate," ISTE+ASCD Blog, July 14, 2025. https://www.ascd.org/blogs/profile-of-an-ai-ready-graduate_

A persistent gap in how AI is taught in schools is the conflation of AI literacy with AI fluency. Culatta's distinction is worth naming explicitly: literacy means knowing what AI is, how it works, and how to fact-check it. Fluency means using AI to think deeper, create better, and solve problems more effectively than you could alone. Literacy is the floor. Fluency is the target.

This distinction maps directly onto the philosophy of this course: AI usage is expected, but what is assessed is your ability to evaluate, critique, and improve AI-generated content — not merely generate it. That is fluency in action.

Culatta proposes six roles that an AI-ready graduate should be able to take on. Each is relevant to how this course expects you to engage with AI:

Learner — Using AI to set goals, identify gaps, get unstuck, and seek immediate, specific, actionable feedback on your work. In this course: use AI to identify where your lab report falls short before submitting. Ask it to find weaknesses in your reasoning, not just errors in grammar.

Researcher — Using AI to investigate topics systematically, evaluate competing claims, and surface patterns within and across fields. In this course: when building your predictive model, use AI to surface relevant literature, identify conflicting assumptions, and locate sources that disagree with each other — then evaluate those conflicts yourself.

Synthesizer — Using AI to bring information together from multiple sources, adapt complexity to context, and change media format when needed. In this course: use AI to help you explain a technical result in plain language for your executive summary, or to restructure your findings for a different audience (e.g., a decision-maker vs. a technical reviewer).

Problem Solver — Using AI as a brainstorming partner to generate alternatives, break through creative blocks, and consider options you might not have reached independently. In this course: use AI to generate alternative sensor selections for your E3 design, then evaluate each against your requirements — don't default to the first option either you or the AI produces.

Connector — Using AI to facilitate collaboration, bridge communication gaps, and build shared understanding across different perspectives or backgrounds. In this course: use AI to find common ground between conflicting model predictions and experimental results — help it help you articulate why the discrepancy exists, not just that it does.

Storyteller — Using AI to present complex ideas through text, visuals, audio, or video in ways that resonate with a specific audience. In this course: use AI to help you sharpen your lightning talk narrative or find a better visual metaphor for an abstract measurement concept. The goal is communication that lands — not just information transfer.

The key point Culatta makes — and the key point this course makes: Teaching students about AI is necessary but not sufficient. What prepares students for an AI-integrated future is sustained practice using AI to accomplish real tasks — and developing the judgment to know when AI is right, when it is wrong, and what to do about it. That is the skill this course is designed to build.

Executive Summary Format

In professional engineering practice, the most valuable written format is often the shortest. An executive summary communicates a complete result — context, method, key finding, implication — in one page or less. Unlike a full lab report, it does not show all the work; it shows what matters and why.

Structure of an effective executive summary:

  1. What was the goal? One sentence: what question were you trying to answer?
  2. How did you do it? Two to three sentences on method and approach.
  3. What did you find? The primary result stated as a number with uncertainty bounds.
  4. What does it mean? One sentence on the engineering implication — what decision does this result support?

When to use it: The executive summary may be offered as an alternative format to the full lab report for E1 W5 (see E1 W5 blueprint for details). In professional engineering, this format is how you communicate results to decision-makers who do not have time to read a full report.

Presentation Best Practices — "Disembodied" Parts and Audience Awareness

_Supplemental reference for students preparing lightning talks (E1 W5), oral defenses (E3 W13–W14), or any technical presentation. A dedicated version of this guidance should also be linked from the E1 W5 and E3 W14 lightning talk assignments._

The "disembodied" problem: In engineering presentations, students often show a component — a sensor, a plot, a diagram — without establishing where it fits in the larger system. The component appears disembodied: removed from its context, unexplained, confusing to anyone who wasn't in the room when the experiment was designed. The fix is simple but requires discipline.

Always assume the audience knows nothing. Before showing any figure, diagram, or component, answer these questions for your audience:

  1. What system are we talking about?
  2. Where does this thing (component, figure, data) fit in that system?
  3. Why does it matter?

Only then show the thing.

The 8 questions for any figure (Kathryn's framework):

  1. Why does this figure exist — what question does it answer?
  2. What is on each axis, with units, at appropriate scale?
  3. Where did this data come from?
  4. What are the uncertainties, and are they shown?
  5. What trend does the data show?
  6. How does the trend compare to the model?
  7. What could explain any discrepancy between data and model?
  8. What does this figure tell you about the overall system?

For lightning talks (E1 W5, E3 W14): Your job is to tell a story. Start with what you were trying to learn and why it matters. Then show what you did and what you found. End with what it means for the system. The audience should be able to follow the logic without having read your report.

For oral defenses (E3 W13–W14): You will be asked to justify every decision you made. Know your experiment well enough to explain why you chose each sensor, each sampling rate, each validation metric — not just what you chose.

Getting Started with Overleaf and LaTeX for Lab Reports

_Lab reports for Lab 1 (P=IV), the Tier 1 assignment, and the Tier 2 assignment are submitted as a LaTeX-compiled report: a .zip of your .tex source and figures, plus the compiled PDF. This section is a getting-started guide for students who haven't used LaTeX before, and explains why the course uses this format._

Why LaTeX instead of Word or Markdown? LaTeX is the standard authoring tool for technical documents in aerospace engineering and most STEM fields — equations, figures, and references are handled consistently and reproducibly. Submitting the LaTeX source alongside the compiled PDF also lets AI-assisted grading tools work directly against your document's structure (sections, equations, figures) rather than trying to parse a PDF, which improves grading consistency and turnaround. This also mirrors the submission format used in other aerospace courses at CU, so the earlier you're comfortable with it, the smoother later courses will be.

Step 1: Create a free Overleaf account. Overleaf (https://www.overleaf.com) is a browser-based LaTeX editor. No local installation is required. Sign up with your CU email. CU Boulder students may have access to Overleaf's premium features through the university; check the Overleaf CU Boulder portal if available.

Step 2: Start from a template. [PLACEHOLDER — instructor to provide or link a course-specific Overleaf report template before Lab 1.] If no course template is provided, start from Overleaf's built-in "Article" template (New Project → Templates → search "Article"). It has everything you need for a short technical report: title, sections, figures, and equations.

Step 3: Learn the basics you'll actually use. You don't need to learn all of LaTeX for these reports. You need:

  • Sections: \section{Title} and \subsection{Title}
  • Figures: \begin{figure} ... \includegraphics{filename} ... \caption{...} \end{figure}
  • Equations: \begin{equation} ... \end{equation} or inline math with $...$
  • Basic text formatting: \textbf{bold}, \textit{italic}, and bullet lists with

\begin{itemize}

Overleaf's built-in documentation ("Learn LaTeX in 30 minutes") covers all of this and is the fastest way to get oriented if you've never used it: https://www.overleaf.com/learn/latex/Learn_LaTeX_in_30_minutes

Step 4: Ask an AI tool for help with LaTeX syntax. LaTeX syntax errors, like a missing brace or a misplaced \begin/\end pair, are common and usually easy for an AI tool to spot and fix. If your document won't compile, paste the error message and the surrounding code into Claude or ChatGPT and ask what's wrong. This is an appropriate and encouraged use of AI for this course, since it's a tooling question, not a content question.

Step 5: Export your submission. When your report is ready, use Overleaf's Menu → Download → Source to get a .zip of your .tex file(s) and figures, and Menu → Download → PDF for the compiled PDF. Combine both into a single .zip archive (source files plus the compiled PDF) for submission. Check the specific lab or Tier assignment for the exact required contents.

Group projects (Tier 2): Overleaf supports real-time collaborative editing, similar to Google Docs. Create one shared Overleaf project for your group and invite your teammates by email (Menu → Share) so everyone can edit the same document simultaneously rather than merging separate files.

How to Generate an AI Lab Report (and Why)

_A supplemental guide for students who want to use AI-generated content as a learning tool. The comparative analysis exercises in this course (E1 W3, E2 W9, E3 W14) will ask you to evaluate provided content — this guide helps you generate your own practice material._

How to Generate an AI Lab Report for Your Experiment

  1. Describe your experiment in detail. Provide the AI with: (a) what physical system you measured, (b) what quantities you measured and how, (c) the predictive model you used, (d) any data you collected (summary statistics are fine — don't upload raw data without checking permissions).
  1. Ask the AI to write a lab report. A starter prompt: "I am an aerospace engineering student. Here is my experiment [description]. Please write a complete lab report including: measurement procedure, error source classification, uncertainty propagation, a quantitative comparison to the predictive model, and a conclusion. Be as technically specific as possible."
  1. Review the report critically. Apply the same evaluation framework used in the in-class comparative analysis exercises:
  • What is correct and well-supported?
  • What is incorrect or physically wrong?
  • What is missing that should be present?
  • What specific change would most improve the report?
  1. Compare to your own analysis. Where does the AI's report agree with yours? Where does it differ? Which differences reflect genuine errors in the AI's reasoning vs. genuine improvements on your own draft?

Why this is valuable: The primary assessment mechanism in this course is critical evaluation — not production. Generating an AI lab report for your own experiment and critiquing it is self-directed practice of the exact skill that is graded in this course. It is also the best preparation for the in-class comparative analysis exercises.

Note: Generating an AI lab report for comparative analysis purposes is entirely within the course's AI policy. Submitting the AI-generated report as your own work without engagement or critique is not.

Chatbot as Subject Matter Expert (SME) — Draft Review Exercise

_A supplemental exercise for students who want practice getting structured critique from an AI before submitting work. This connects to the broader "Aidan R. / Blue" workflow: requirements → draft → AI-SME review → refine. The idea: It is much easier to critique a draft than to originate from nothing. An AI chatbot configured as a skeptical Subject Matter Expert will "tear up" a draft in ways that are immediately useful — it gives you something concrete to engage with, respond to, and improve. This is the same workflow used in professional engineering organizations where a draft is circulated to SMEs for red-team review before submission.

How to run it:

  1. Write your draft (V&V plan, lab report, prediction memo — any structured deliverable).
  2. Upload or paste your draft into ChatGPT, Claude, or similar.
  3. Use the following kickoff prompt to configure the AI as an SME reviewer:

> "You are an experienced aerospace test engineer and subject matter expert reviewing a student's [V&V plan / lab report / prediction memo] for an experimental methods course.   1. Review the AI's critique critically. It may be wrong. Ask yourself: is this critique accurate? Is the AI identifying a genuine gap, or is it confused about the context?
  2. Revise your draft based on the critique — or document why you disagree with specific feedback.
  3. If you use this workflow, note it in your submission transparency statement (per the AI usage policy).

Virtual Multimeter & Resistor Circuit Simulator — Bonus Activity

_A supplemental bonus activity connected to E1 W2 (measurement resolution, digit fluctuation, and uncertainty recording) and Lab 1 (P=IV pre-lab preparation). The idea: Use Claude or ChatGPT to build an interactive resistor circuit simulator with a virtual multimeter interface. The simulator gives you a safe environment to practice the E1 W2 uncertainty recording technique — recording the stable digit and assigning ±½ of the last stable digit as measurement uncertainty — before working with real benchtop instruments in Lab 1.

What the simulator should do:

  1. Allow the user to define a simple resistor circuit (e.g., a single resistor with a voltage source, or a small network)
  2. Display a virtual multimeter readout that simulates the fluctuating digit behavior seen on real benchtop instruments — the least significant digit should visibly fluctuate to simulate real instrument noise and resolution limits
  3. Include a precision/accuracy knob or slider that the student can adjust to simulate different instrument quality levels — changing the knob changes how many digits fluctuate and by how much, illustrating the difference between a cheap and an expensive multimeter
  4. Compute and display V, I, and R readings consistent with the circuit, with appropriate simulated noise on each channel
  5. Connect explicitly to the E1 W2 recording technique: cover the fluctuating digit, record the stable digit, and assign ±½ of the last stable digit as measurement uncertainty

Connection to Lab 1 and E1 W2: The simulator is direct pre-lab preparation for Lab 1 (P=IV). Students who use the simulator before Lab 1 arrive with a concrete mental model of what real multimeter noise looks like and why the recording convention exists.

  • Instructor-provided version (class demonstration): The instructor may provide a pre-built artifact (HTML/JavaScript, Python, or similar) that the whole class can use as a shared demonstration tool.
