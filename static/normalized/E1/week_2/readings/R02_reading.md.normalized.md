## What the Reading Should Contain (once source is confirmed)

The purpose of this reading is to give students a framework for technical communication in engineering — specifically, the kind of direct, evidence-driven writing that professional engineers use in lab reports, memos, and technical documentation. It should be assigned in W2 to support the Lab 1 brief report deliverable, where students write their first structured engineering memo.

When this reading is developed, it should address one or more of the following topics (instructor should confirm which match the Scheeres paper's content):

1. **Structure of a technical argument** — the logical flow from claim → evidence → reasoning that characterizes professional engineering writing, contrasted with the narrative or descriptive style of general writing.

2. **Hedging and precision** — how to state results with appropriate confidence bounds, avoid overclaiming, and write conclusions that are defensible given the evidence. This connects directly to the uncertainty analysis framework from Week 2.

3. **Audience calibration** — how to write for a technical audience vs. a non-technical audience. Week 2 requires both: a full technical analysis and a plain-language explanation accessible to a non-engineer.

4. **Figure captions and data presentation** — how to write a caption that communicates a conclusion, not just a description. (Note: if this topic is the primary focus, this reading should be coordinated with or folded into E1_W3_R1, which covers Kathryn's graph presentation framework.)

5. **The revision mindset** — treating writing as a technical process of iterative improvement, not a transcription task. Connects to the course's use of AI for drafting and the student's role as critical evaluator.
