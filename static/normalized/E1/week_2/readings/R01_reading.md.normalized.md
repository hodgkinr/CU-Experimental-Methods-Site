# Uncertainty Analysis Reference — Coleman & Steele, 4th Edition
## A Field Guide for Engineers Who Take Their Results Seriously

The moment you accept that every measurement is a distribution, not a number, your entire relationship with experimental data changes.

---

This reading introduces a reference text you'll use repeatedly throughout this course: *Experimentation, Validation, and Uncertainty Analysis for Engineers* by Hugh W. Coleman and W. Glenn Steele, Fourth Edition. It's not a cover-to-cover assignment. It's a field guide: different chapters address different problems you'll encounter, and this reading orients you to the book's structure so you can find what you need when you need it.

The [Coleman & Steele OER](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/epub/10.1002/9781119417989) is available online as the course reference copy.

Coleman & Steele belongs to a tradition of engineering texts that treat measurement uncertainty not as an afterthought (the error bar you add before submitting a figure) but as the primary product of an experiment. The core thesis of the book matches the core thesis of this course: the goal of an experiment is not to produce a number, it is to produce a defensible claim. A defensible claim requires not just a result but a quantified bound on how uncertain you are about that result, derived by methods that a critical reader can evaluate and reproduce.

**Part I of Coleman & Steele** (Chapters 1–4) is the section most relevant to E1. Chapter 1 establishes the conceptual framework: the three-way distinction between **elemental error sources** (individual contributors to uncertainty), **random errors** (scatter that reduces with repeated measurements), and **systematic errors** (bias that does not reduce with repetition). This structure and its vocabulary are exactly what Week 2's lectures develop. If a concept from lecture feels imprecise, Chapter 1 is the first place to check; it provides the careful definitions and worked examples that lecture time does not always allow.

Chapter 3 is where the **partial derivative propagation method** is fully derived. If you have seen the formula for propagating uncertainty through a derived quantity (summing the squared products of partial derivatives and input uncertainties in a root-sum-of-squares combination), that formula is the central result of this chapter. Coleman & Steele derive it carefully, identify the assumptions it requires (first-order Taylor series expansion, uncorrelated error sources), and work through engineering examples of the same form you'll encounter in Lab 1. The three power equations (P = IV, P = V²/R, and P = I²R) are precisely the kind of derived-quantity example this chapter addresses. Different equation forms for the same physical quantity yield different partial derivatives and therefore different sensitivities to the same input errors. Chapter 3 is where that result is grounded in theory.

**Chapter 3** is also where Coleman & Steele introduce the Monte Carlo Method for propagating uncertainty through a result determined from multiple measured variables. Where the Taylor Series Method gives you an analytical estimate of output uncertainty, Monte Carlo simulation draws many samples from the input distributions and propagates them through the full equation, revealing the output distribution directly. The two methods agree when the assumptions of the Taylor-series approximation hold well; they diverge when the output function is highly nonlinear or when input distributions are non-Gaussian. Week 2 develops both methods in the context of Lab 1. Chapter 3 is the main reference for that side-by-side propagation comparison; Chapter 4 then shows how Taylor-series uncertainty analysis is used during experiment planning.

**Part II** of Coleman & Steele addresses validation, comparing experimental outcomes to model predictions within uncertainty bounds. This section becomes more relevant in E2 and E3, but the same reference you use for uncertainty propagation in Week 2 also develops the conceptual framework for the quantitative comparison methods you'll use in Week 3 and beyond. The book carries you through the full measurement-to-validation pipeline.

The relationship between Coleman & Steele and the ASME standards from Week 1 is deliberate. The book's methodology is consistent with ASME PTC 19.1 (the measurement uncertainty standard for performance testing) and with the uncertainty quantification framework assumed by ASME V&V 10-2006. The international reference for measurement uncertainty is the **ISO GUM** (*Guide to the Expression of Uncertainty in Measurement*). Coleman & Steele's methods are consistent with the GUM framework, making the book an accessible entry point into what is otherwise a dense international standard. Applying Coleman & Steele's methods in your lab reports means applying an approach that satisfies recognized professional standards, which matters when engineering results are submitted for design decisions, regulatory review, or publication.

**The Takeaway:** Coleman & Steele is the methodological backbone behind every uncertainty propagation exercise in this course. Keep it as a reference, not a reading, and use it to ground your understanding in the professional standards that this course's framework is built on. Chapters 1 and 3 are your Week 2 anchors; Chapter 4 supports Monte Carlo; Part II becomes relevant in Week 3 and beyond.
