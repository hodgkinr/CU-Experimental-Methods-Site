Title: What a Good Prediction Looks Like — Committing Before You Look
Talking Point: This lecture exists to prepare you for something specific: the Phase 2 Prediction Memo, which you will begin in today's lab session. Before we do that, I want to be direct about what a prediction actually is, why it matters that you commit to it before seeing any data, and what distinguishes a prediction that a test engineer would respect from a guess dressed in engineering vocabulary. The six elements of a defensible prediction are the subject of this lecture. By the end, you will know exactly what the Phase 2 memo requires — and more importantly, you will understand why each element exists.

slide 2: Prediction vs. Guess — The Difference That Matters
(Image: A split-panel comparison. Left panel header: "Guess" — icon of a shrugging figure. Text: "The output will probably go up. It should be around 30 watts or so. I think it will match the model pretty well." Red annotations: "No committed value," "No physical reasoning," "No mechanism," "Cannot be evaluated after the fact." Right panel header: "Prediction" — icon of a test engineer at a whiteboard. Text: "Based on F_c = m·r·ω², I predict the centripetal force will reach 28.4 ± 3.2 N at ω = 100 rad/s, with current uncertainty dominant at 11.3% of output. If the data shows hysteresis, the model is missing a damping term." Green annotations: "Committed specific value," "Physical mechanism stated," "Uncertainty estimated from specs," "Anomalous pattern named." Caption: "The distinguishing feature is falsifiability: can the prediction be clearly contradicted by the data?")
Talking Point: A prediction and a guess are not different by degree — they are different by structure. A guess is directional and vague: "it will probably go up," "it should be around 30," "it will match pretty well." None of those statements can be clearly falsified by a measurement. If the value is 38 instead of 30, a person who said "around 30" can always reinterpret their prior statement. A prediction is falsifiable in the precise sense: it commits to a specific value or range, derived from a specific physical mechanism, with a specific uncertainty estimate, so that a measurement can clearly confirm it, approximately confirm it, or clearly contradict it. The ability for the prediction to be contradicted is not a weakness — it is the entire point. A prediction that cannot be contradicted does not say anything about the physics. If you learn nothing when the prediction is right, you also learn nothing when it is wrong.

slide 3: The Six Elements of a Defensible Prediction
(Image: A vertical numbered list with six labeled items, each in a distinct color-coded card. Card 1 (blue): "System Response Features — specific committed values or ranges. Not 'it increases' but 'it reaches 28.4 N at ω = 100 rad/s.'" Card 2 (green): "Propagated Uncertainty — derived from sensor specs using partial derivatives or Monte Carlo. Stated as estimate ± U at confidence level." Card 3 (gold): "Committed Sketch — a hand-drawn or MATLAB plot of the expected output with labeled axes, units, and uncertainty bounds drawn before data is seen." Card 4 (purple): "Ranked Uncertainty Sources — which input contributes most? In what order? Specific estimated magnitudes." Card 5 (red): "Named Acceptance Criterion — a numerical threshold, stated as a specific inequality or metric value, defined before the comparison is computed." Card 6 (orange): "Risk Assessment — at least one specific anomalous data pattern identified with physical basis. What would 'wrong' look like, and what physical effect would cause it?" Below all six: "All six are required in the Phase 2 Prediction Memo. Missing any one makes the prediction incomplete.")
Talking Point: The Phase 2 Prediction Memo requires all six of these elements — and I want to walk through why each one exists. System response features commit you to something specific: a number, a range, a shape, a direction with a magnitude. The uncertainty estimate connects your prediction to the measurement apparatus — it is derived from the sensor spec using the propagation methods you know from Week 2. The committed sketch forces you to visualize what the data should look like before you see it — axes, units, scale, and uncertainty bounds drawn by hand or in MATLAB. The ranked uncertainty sources tell me what you understand about the dominant error in this measurement. The named acceptance criterion commits you to the standard you will use for evaluation — before you see whether your prediction was close or not. And element six: risk assessment. Name at least one specific anomalous data pattern that would indicate the model is wrong — not just "the data might be noisy," but a specific physically-grounded signature: "if the output shows amplitude-dependent behavior, the linear model is missing a nonlinear term." The risk assessment is what connects your prediction to the anomalous data thinking from Week 3. Six elements. All six are required.

slide 4: Element 1 — System Response Features
(Image: A comparison of two prediction statements for the same experiment. Statement A: "The measured temperature will be high." Red annotations: "No units," "No direction," "No mechanism," "Cannot be falsified." Statement B: "The thermocouple will read 87 ± 4°C at a heater power of 25 W, based on the steady-state energy balance Q = h·A·ΔT with h = 35 W/m²K from the correlation for natural convection from a horizontal surface." Green annotations: "Specific value with units," "Physical mechanism (energy balance) stated," "Parameter source identified (convection correlation)," "Falsifiable: a reading of 110°C would clearly contradict this." Caption: "A system response feature is a committed, specific, mechanism-grounded prediction — not a directional tendency.")
Talking Point: A system response feature is a specific prediction about what the measured quantity will be, or how it will behave as the independent variable changes. "It will increase" is not a system response feature. "The temperature will reach 87 plus or minus 4 degrees Celsius at 25 Watts of heater power, based on the steady-state energy balance with a natural convection coefficient derived from the correlation for a horizontal surface" — that is a system response feature. It commits to a value. It names the physical mechanism behind the prediction. It gives me a basis for evaluating whether the prediction was right or wrong, and more importantly, for understanding what a discrepancy means physically. In the Phase 2 memo, every system response feature must be tied to a physical equation or correlation — not to intuition, not to "it seemed reasonable."

slide 5: Element 2 — Propagated Uncertainty from Sensor Specs
(Image: A three-step workflow diagram. Step 1: "Read the spec sheet — extract sensor accuracy, resolution, and measurement range." A sample spec card shows: "Thermocouple accuracy: ±0.5°C. Data acquisition resolution: ±0.2°C. Combined standard uncertainty: √(0.5² + 0.2²) = 0.54°C." Step 2: "Apply partial derivative propagation to the governing equation." Shows a partial derivative expression for ΔT from the energy balance equation. Step 3: "State the result: Uncertainty in predicted temperature = ±4°C at 95% CI (dominant source: uncertainty in convection coefficient h, contributing 78% of total variance)." A gold callout: "This is predict-from-specs — estimating uncertainty before you have data, using only the sensor spec sheet.")
Talking Point: The uncertainty estimate in your prediction is not a guess — it is a calculation from the sensor spec. This is the same predict-from-specs workflow you began in Week 4: read the supplied sensor information, apply partial derivative propagation to the governing equation, and report the predicted output uncertainty before you rely on measured data. You are doing exactly the same thing for the Phase 2 memo, but now you have not run the experiment yourself — you are working from the spec sheet included in the experiment packet. The calculation is the same: take the sensor accuracies and resolutions from the spec, propagate them through the governing equation, rank the contributions, and report the dominant source. The fact that you have not seen the physical hardware does not change the calculation. If you can read a spec sheet and apply the propagation formula, you can estimate the uncertainty before the experiment runs.

slide 6: Element 3 — The Committed Sketch
(Image: A hand-drawn style sketch of a time-series plot. X-axis labeled "Time (s)" from 0 to 60. Y-axis labeled "Temperature (°C)" from 20 to 100. A smooth S-shaped curve rises from ambient temperature to an asymptote around 87°C, labeled "Expected steady-state: 87°C." Two dashed lines parallel to the curve form an uncertainty band labeled "±4°C (propagated from sensor spec)." A horizontal target line at 87°C is labeled "Predicted steady-state." Hand-drawn annotations in a different style: "First 10s: rapid rise." "30-40s: rate slows." "After 50s: near steady-state." "Scatter within this band is measurement noise — consistent with sensor spec." Caption: "Drawn before data is released — axes, units, and bounds committed in advance.")
Talking Point: The committed sketch is not a formality. It is the most direct test of whether your prediction is concrete. If you cannot draw the expected output — with labeled axes, units, approximate scale, and an uncertainty band — you do not yet have a prediction. You have a direction. The sketch forces you to make a sequence of commitments: what quantity is on each axis, at what scale, over what range. The shape of the curve commits you to the physical mechanism — linear growth, exponential approach, oscillation. The uncertainty band commits you to the magnitude of expected scatter. When the data comes in, you overlay it on your sketch. If it falls within the band, you have learned something positive. If it falls outside, you have a discrepancy to explain. But none of that analysis is possible if you did not draw the sketch before you saw the data. Draw it first.

slide 7: Elements 4 and 5 — Ranked Sources and Acceptance Criterion
(Image: A two-panel slide. Left panel: "Element 4 — Ranked Uncertainty Sources." A horizontal bar chart showing three inputs, with bars proportional to their contribution to total output uncertainty: Input A (convection coefficient h) — 78%. Input B (thermocouple accuracy) — 14%. Input C (DAQ resolution) — 8%. Annotations: "Input A dominates — improving the convection correlation would most reduce uncertainty." "Inputs B and C are secondary — better sensors would help marginally." Right panel: "Element 5 — Acceptance Criterion." A text box: "Pre-committed criterion: the prediction is considered successful if the measured steady-state temperature falls within the propagated uncertainty interval of 87 ± 4°C. If the measured value falls outside this band, the discrepancy exceeds measurement noise and requires a physical explanation." A red annotation: "This criterion was stated before data release — it cannot be revised retroactively.")
Talking Point: Element four — ranked uncertainty sources — is the output of the sensitivity analysis from this week's lecture. Once you have propagated uncertainty through the governing equation, you know which input contributes the most. State it explicitly, with estimated magnitudes. This is not just bookkeeping — it tells me whether you understand the dominant error in the measurement and whether your prediction accounts for the right sources of uncertainty. Element five — the named acceptance criterion — is the pre-commitment that makes the comparison defensible. You state a specific threshold before you see the data: if the measured value falls within this range, the prediction holds; if it falls outside, the discrepancy requires a physical explanation. Pre-committing to the criterion means you cannot move the goalposts after seeing the result. That is not a bureaucratic rule — it is the logical requirement that makes validation meaningful.

slide 8: The Grading Philosophy — Right Prediction, Wrong Reasoning Scores Lower
(Image: A 2x2 matrix with "Prediction Correctness" on one axis (Right / Wrong) and "Quality of Reasoning" on the other axis (Sound / Flawed). Four quadrants: Top-left "Right prediction, sound reasoning" — gold star label "Excellent — the ideal outcome." Top-right "Right prediction, flawed reasoning" — red label "Suspicious — you may have gotten lucky. Less informative than bottom-left." Bottom-left "Wrong prediction, sound reasoning" — green label "Good — the analysis was correct; the discrepancy teaches you something." Bottom-right "Wrong prediction, flawed reasoning" — red label "Poor — both the analysis and the outcome fail." A caption: "A prediction that turns out to be wrong but was derived from sound physical reasoning is more valuable than a prediction that turns out to be right from vague intuition.")
Talking Point: I want to communicate the Phase 2 grading standard as explicitly as I can, because it is not the grading standard you may be used to. A prediction that turns out to be wrong but was derived from sound physical reasoning and a complete, committed analysis is more valuable than a prediction that turns out to be right but was derived from vague intuition with no mechanism, no calculation, and no committed uncertainty. The goal of Phase 2 is not to produce a correct prediction. The goal is to demonstrate that you understood the physics of the experiment before you saw the data — well enough to build a specific, mechanistic, falsifiable prediction. If your prediction was right for the right reasons, that is excellent. If your prediction was wrong for the right reasons, you have learned something real. If your prediction was right for the wrong reasons — if you got lucky — you have learned nothing durable from the result. This philosophy is not idiosyncratic — it is the professional norm for test engineers, and you should hear it clearly before Phase 2 begins.

I read the theory primer, identify the governing equation — one-dimensional Fourier conduction — and extract the system response feature: steady-state temperature at the second sensor, predicted to be 54 degrees Celsius. That is a complete prediction.

slide 11: Closure
Pre-commitment is not a bureaucratic requirement. It is the logical structure that makes experimental learning honest. If you construct your interpretation of the data after seeing it, you will inevitably see what you expected to see, or what you hoped to see. The committed prediction — specific, mechanistic, falsifiable, with an uncertainty estimate and an acceptance criterion — is your promise to yourself and to the reader that your interpretation of the data was not reverse-engineered from the outcome. The six elements of a defensible prediction are the six components of that promise. In the Phase 2 memo you are writing today, every one of them is required. And the grading philosophy is explicit: sound reasoning in the wrong direction is more valuable than vague intuition in the right one. That is the standard this course holds you to. That is the standard your profession will hold you to. Build the habit now.
