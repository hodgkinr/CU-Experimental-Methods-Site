slide 3: Step 1 — Extract the Governing Equation and System Response Features
(Image: A derivation slide. The governing equation: "I·θ̈ + κ·θ = 0," leading to the natural frequency expression: "ωₙ = √(κ/I)" and the period "T = 2π√(I/κ)." Below: a table showing the values from the theory primer: "I = 0.0248 kg·m² (disk geometry, measured with calipers and a scale)," "κ = 0.0315 N·m/rad (from wire calibration data in the packet)." Calculation: "T = 2π√(0.0248/0.0315) = 2π × 0.887 = 5.57 s." A gold box: "System Response Feature: The torsional pendulum period is predicted to be 5.57 s. The oscillation amplitude should decay due to air damping — but the model assumes undamped oscillation. Flag this assumption explicitly.")
Talking Point: Step one: read the theory primer and extract the governing equation. For a torsional pendulum, the equation of motion is I times theta double-dot plus kappa times theta equals zero, giving a natural frequency of the square root of kappa over I. With the values from the primer — moment of inertia 0.0248 kilogram-meter-squared, torsional stiffness 0.0315 Newton-meters per radian — the predicted period is 5.57 seconds. That is the system response feature I am committing to. I also note immediately that the model assumes undamped oscillation — there is no damping term. The actual disk oscillates in air, which has some drag. This means I should expect the measured period to be slightly longer than the undamped prediction, and the amplitude to decay. That is my first model-form assumption flagged before I see the data.

slide 4: Step 2 — Propagate Uncertainty from the Spec Sheet
(Image: A partial derivative propagation table. Column headers: "Parameter," "Value," "Partial Derivative ∂T/∂(param)," "Uncertainty δ(param)," "Contribution |∂T/∂(param) × δ(param)|." Rows: "I = 0.0248 kg·m²" — partial derivative = π/√(κI) — δI = ±5% from geometry measurement — contribution = ±0.14 s. "κ = 0.0315 N·m/rad" — partial derivative = −π√(I)/κ^(3/2) — δκ = ±8% from wire calibration uncertainty — contribution = ±0.22 s. "Combined uncertainty (quadrature sum): δT = √(0.14² + 0.22²) = ±0.26 s." A gold box: "Predicted period: 5.57 ± 0.26 s (95% CI, partial derivative method). Dominant source: torsional stiffness κ, contributing 72% of total output variance.")
Talking Point: Step two: propagate the uncertainty from the spec sheet values. I take the partial derivative of the period with respect to I and with respect to kappa. The stiffness kappa has larger uncertainty than the moment of inertia — the wire calibration in the packet carries 8% uncertainty, while the geometry measurement for I carries about 5%. Evaluating the propagation, the kappa term dominates: it contributes 72% of the total output variance. The combined uncertainty in the predicted period is plus or minus 0.26 seconds at 95% confidence. So my prediction is: the period is 5.57 plus or minus 0.26 seconds. That is a specific, defensible claim derived from the spec sheet — without having seen the angular encoder measure a single oscillation.

slide 6: Step 4 — Acceptance Criterion and Risk Assessment
(Image: Two clearly separated text boxes. Box 1 — "Acceptance Criterion": "The prediction is successful if the measured oscillation period is within 5.57 ± 0.26 s (the propagated uncertainty interval at 95% CI). A measured period outside this interval requires a physical explanation — either an unaccounted-for uncertainty source or a model-form limitation." Box 2 — "Risk Assessment — Two Anomalous Signatures to Watch": "(1) If the period decreases significantly with amplitude (nonlinearity), the small-angle approximation in the model has failed. The linear model is invalid at large angular displacements. (2) If the period is significantly shorter than predicted — less than 5.2 s — the torsional stiffness in the spec may be miscalibrated, or the wire has hardened since the calibration was performed." Both boxes use consistent formatting. Caption: "Commit to what wrong looks like before you see the data.")
Talking Point: Step four: state the acceptance criterion and the risk assessment. The acceptance criterion is numerical and pre-committed — if the measured period is within 5.57 plus or minus 0.26 seconds, I will classify the comparison as supporting agreement. If it falls outside, I need a physical explanation. The risk assessment names two specific anomalous patterns I should watch for. First: if the measured period decreases with amplitude — if early oscillations are shorter than later ones as the amplitude decays — the small-angle approximation in the linearized model has failed. The linear model is only valid for small angular displacements, and this failure is predictable in advance. Second: if the measured period is significantly shorter than predicted — say, below 5.2 seconds — the torsional stiffness in the packet may be wrong, or the wire properties have changed since calibration. Both anomalous signatures are physically motivated, and both are commitable before the data is released.

So the difference is just barely within the combined uncertainty. I will classify this as marginal agreement — but I should investigate the discrepancy rather than simply accepting agreement.

slide 8: What Does the Discrepancy Tell Us?
(Image: A residual analysis plot. X-axis: "Oscillation Number" from 1 to 8. Y-axis: "Period (s) for each oscillation." The individual measured periods are plotted as dots. A horizontal dashed line marks the predicted value of 5.57 s. All measured periods are above the prediction line, and there is a slight trend: earlier oscillations (when amplitude is large) have slightly longer periods — around 5.90 s — while later oscillations (when amplitude is small) converge toward 5.65 s. A gold annotation: "Systematic trend: period decreases as amplitude decreases — consistent with nonlinear stiffness at large displacement." A second annotation: "Residuals are NOT random. The small-angle approximation fails at large initial displacement — this was the first risk assessment item.")
Talking Point: Look at the residuals. The measured period is not just slightly high — it shows a systematic trend. The first few oscillations, when the amplitude is large, have longer periods. The later oscillations, as amplitude decays, have shorter periods approaching the linear model prediction. That is not random scatter. That is the small-angle approximation failing at large displacement — exactly the first risk assessment item I committed to before seeing the data. The linear model predicts a constant period regardless of amplitude. The physical system shows amplitude-dependent period because the linearization I·θ-double-dot plus kappa·theta is only valid for small theta. At large theta, the nonlinear sine term in the full restoring force equation matters. The anomalous signature I predicted is the one I see. This is what it looks like when a model-form failure is predicted in advance and then confirmed: you learn something specific about the physics of the system, not just that the model was approximately right.
