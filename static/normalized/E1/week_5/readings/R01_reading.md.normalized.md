# Discrepancy Is Data — Interpreting What the Gap Tells You
## Three Categories of Mismatch, and What Each One Reveals About Your Model

The most instructive moment in any experiment is not when your result matches the prediction — it is when it doesn't, and you are forced to explain why.

---

Every experiment ends with a comparison: measured result on one side, model prediction on the other. When the two agree, confidence builds. When they don't, something more interesting happens — the disagreement carries information that the agreement never could. A result that matches your model tells you the model is not obviously wrong, for this experiment, at this scale, under these conditions. A result that doesn't match tells you something specific about what the model is missing or what the measurement got wrong. But only an engineer who knows how to read the gap can extract that information.

The discipline of discrepancy interpretation begins with classification. Not all gaps between measurement and prediction are the same, and the same physical magnitude of gap can mean fundamentally different things depending on its relationship to the propagated uncertainty bounds and the structure of the residuals.

**Case 1: The gap falls within the propagated uncertainty band.** The measured result and the model prediction are statistically consistent — the scatter is explained by measurement noise, and no model revision is required by this result alone. This is the resolution you hoped for when you designed the experiment. Note carefully what it does *not* mean: consistency within uncertainty says only that you cannot distinguish the model from noise with this experiment, this instrumentation, and this level of precision. A tighter measurement — fewer sources of random error, a better-characterized sensor — might reveal a real gap that the current uncertainty band was hiding. Consider measuring the natural frequency of a spring-mass system: if the scatter in your repeated frequency measurements is larger than the difference between measured and predicted frequency, the two are statistically consistent. Tighten the measurement, and a real discrepancy may emerge.

**Case 2: The gap is larger than the uncertainty band, but has no systematic structure.** The data points scatter above and below the model, but their distribution is consistently displaced — the mean of the measured values does not fall within the predicted range, even accounting for propagated uncertainty. The residuals show no trend; they are randomly scattered but uniformly offset. This is a signal that your uncertainty analysis is incomplete: a significant error source was omitted from the propagation. The diagnostic question is which source. Review the measurement chain — are there sensor specifications you rough-estimated? An installation effect you assumed was negligible? A calibration offset you did not propagate? Consider a pendulum period measurement where you identified reaction time as the dominant uncertainty source but forgot that the pendulum's initial release angle affects the linearization assumption. That systematic physical effect, omitted from the uncertainty model, produces exactly this pattern: a gap larger than the stated bounds that has no systematic structure.

**Case 3: The gap shows systematic structure.** The residuals are not random — they grow monotonically with the independent variable, or they are consistently positive over one range and negative over another, or they follow a pattern that mirrors the shape of a physical effect your model ignores. This is **model-form error**: the model's mathematical structure is wrong, not just its parameter values. Recalibrating the sensor or tightening the uncertainty bounds will not close this gap, because the gap is not caused by measurement variability at all. Consider a ball launched at an angle, where the model ignores air resistance: the predicted trajectory is systematically too long and too high at late times. The residuals grow with time, and they have a consistent sign — positive in the height direction throughout the flight. That is the fingerprint of the missing drag term. The pattern in the residuals is not noise. It is a physical signal, and it points to exactly what the model is missing.

The value of this taxonomy is not just retrospective — it shapes how you design experiments and write predictions before any data is seen. When you commit to a prediction for the Tier 1 Phase 2 Prediction Memo, understanding which category of discrepancy is plausible helps you define your acceptance criterion precisely. If you expect only Case 1 (noise within uncertainty), a tight propagated uncertainty band is your pass/fail gate. If you suspect Case 3 (model-form error), your acceptance criterion should explicitly describe what systematic structure in the residuals would constitute evidence of a model-form failure — and your risk assessment should name the physical effect you think the model might be missing.

This taxonomy carries directly into E2, where residual plots are formalized as a required analytical tool and where the consequences of misclassifying a Case 3 gap as Case 1 (ignoring systematic model-form error as "just noise") become more consequential as the models grow more complex.

---

**The Takeaway:** Discrepancy between prediction and measurement is not a grade penalty — it is an opportunity to identify which part of your model or measurement chain failed. The answer depends entirely on whether the gap is within your uncertainty bounds (Case 1), uniformly displaced beyond them (Case 2), or systematically structured (Case 3), and each category points to a different corrective action.
