slide 1: Title Slide
Title: Predicting What Wrong Looks Like — Anomalous Data Signatures
Talking Point: In Lecture 2 we established that calibration and validation are different activities — calibration is about the sensor, validation is about the model. Now I want to extend that idea in a direction that has real professional consequences. If you understand what your model assumes, you should be able to describe not just what the expected data looks like — but what specific patterns in the data would tell you the model is fundamentally wrong. This lecture is about developing that predictive habit before you run an experiment. By the end, you should be able to look at a model, name its assumptions, and predict the specific anomalous signatures that would indicate model-form failure — not measurement noise, but a signal that the physics are telling you the model is missing something.

slide 2: Predicting What Right Looks Like
(Image: A simple force-displacement graph for a spring-mass system. X-axis: "Displacement (mm)." Y-axis: "Force (N)." A solid straight diagonal line labeled "Expected model response: F = kx (linear)." Two uncertainty bands flank the line — one for measurement uncertainty (gray shading) and one for model parameter uncertainty (lighter outer shading). Annotations: "Slope = k (spring constant)" and "Expected scatter: within these bounds for any individual measurement." A caption: "Before touching hardware: commit to what the expected data signature looks like — the slope, the linearity, the magnitude, and the noise floor.")
Talking Point: One of the habits I want you to develop before the end of this course is this: before you run an experiment, commit to what the right result looks like — specifically. Not "force increases with displacement," but "force increases linearly with displacement at a slope equal to k, with scatter bounded by the propagated uncertainty from my sensor spec." The sketch on this slide is what that commitment looks like. You have drawn the expected data signature, including the expected noise floor, before any hardware has been touched. This sketch is worth something — it is a commitment that can be evaluated after the fact. And it has a corollary that is just as important: if you can describe what right looks like, you should also be able to describe what wrong looks like.

slide 3: Predicting What Wrong Looks Like — Anomalous Data Signatures
(Image: Three side-by-side force-displacement plots for the same spring-mass system. Plot A (left): "Expected — scatter around a straight line. Scatter is symmetric, consistent with measurement noise." Dots scattered around the model line within the uncertainty band. Plot B (center): "Anomalous — hysteresis loop. Loading and unloading traces follow different paths, forming a closed loop." A label box reads: "Hysteresis: output depends on history — loading and unloading follow different paths because energy is dissipated. This is not noise — it is a systematic pattern that the linear model cannot explain. Evidence of damping or friction not in the model." Plot C (right): "Anomalous — nonlinearity at large displacement. Slope increases at large displacement." Labeled: "Model assumes constant k. Data shows k is displacement-dependent at large deflection — geometric nonlinearity or material yielding." Caption: "You can predict which anomalous signatures are possible — if you understand your model's assumptions.")
Talking Point: Here is the key insight of this lecture. If you understand your model's assumptions, you can predict not just what the expected data looks like, but what specific anomalous patterns would indicate model-form failure — failure that is not measurement noise, but a sign that the model is fundamentally wrong for these conditions. For a linear spring model, the expected signature is scatter around a straight line within the propagated uncertainty. But there are specific signatures that would tell you the model is wrong. Hysteresis — different loading and unloading paths — is evidence of energy dissipation: the model does not include damping or friction, so it cannot produce a loop. Hysteresis is a specific term worth knowing: it describes a system whose output depends not just on the current input, but on the history of how it got there — loading and unloading follow different paths because energy is dissipated along the way. Nonlinearity at large displacement is evidence of material or geometric effects the model treats as negligible. These anomalous signatures are predictable in advance if you understand what assumptions your model is making. Commit to them before you run the experiment. Then, if you see them in your data, you will know exactly what physical effect they represent — and you will not mistake them for measurement noise.

slide 4: Scatter vs. Anomaly — The Diagnostic Distinction
(Image: Two plots shown side by side, both using the same force-displacement setup. Left plot: "Measurement Noise — scatter." Multiple data points scatter around the model line, some above and some below, with no systematic pattern. The scatter is bounded by the propagated uncertainty band (gray shading). Label: "This is consistent with random measurement error. No model revision required." Right plot: "Model-Form Error — hysteresis." The data forms a distinct loading and unloading loop that is clearly outside the uncertainty band and clearly systematic. Label: "This is NOT measurement noise. The uncertainty band does not explain this pattern. The model is missing an energy dissipation term." A central annotation: "The distinguishing feature is structure — systematic patterns are model-form errors. Random scatter within the uncertainty band is measurement noise.")
Talking Point: The distinction between scatter and anomaly is the practical form of the calibration-versus-validation distinction. Scatter that is bounded by your propagated uncertainty band is consistent with measurement noise — random, symmetric, not systematic. It tells you your measurement process is working as expected. A pattern that is systematic — like hysteresis, or a trending residual, or data that falls consistently above or below the model across a range of conditions — is not measurement noise. It cannot be explained by your sensor spec. It is evidence that the model is missing a physical effect. The uncertainty band is your null hypothesis: data within it is consistent with your model being adequate. Data outside it, in a systematic way, is evidence that the model form is wrong. Commit to this diagnostic before the experiment, and you will recognize a model-form failure immediately when you see it in your data.

In Tier 1 Phase 2, you will be given the experiment packet for an experiment you did not run — theory primer, sensor specs, physical setup description — and before any data is released, you will submit a Prediction Memo. You are committing, before seeing any data, to what wrong looks like — and why. That commitment is what makes your subsequent interpretation defensible.

slide 7: Closure
The extension of the calibration-versus-validation distinction — committing before an experiment to what both the expected and the anomalous results look like — is the discipline that transforms experiment design from observation to hypothesis testing. When you know what wrong looks like before you start, you will recognize it when you see it, and you will be able to name what it means physically. The uncertainty band is your null hypothesis. Data outside it, in a systematic way, is your evidence of model-form failure. Pre-committing to the specific signature — hysteresis, nonlinearity, a consistent offset, an amplitude-dependent period — is what makes the Phase 2 Prediction Memo's risk assessment section more than a formality. That is the skill you need for every validation activity in the rest of this course.
