slide 1: Title Slide
Title: Structured Comparison & Validation Metrics
Talking Point: Weeks 1 and 2 gave you the tools to measure a quantity and express your confidence in it as an uncertainty interval. Week 3 asks the next question: now that you have a measurement with quantified uncertainty and a model prediction, how do you decide whether they agree? Not visually — not by eyeballing a plot and saying "they look close" — but with a structured, defensible, numerical argument. That shift, from visual judgment to structured comparison, is one of the most important cognitive moves in this course. This lecture gives you the tools to make it, including how to put your comparison criteria directly on the figure where anyone reading the report can see them.

slide 2: The Problem with Visual Inspection
(Image: Three side-by-side plots showing identical data and model, differing only in axis scale. Plot 1: axes zoomed far out — data points and model line appear to coincide perfectly. Plot 2: axes zoomed tightly — data and model appear to diverge dramatically. Plot 3: intermediate scale — judgment is genuinely ambiguous. All three plots have the same axis labels and the same data values; only the scale differs. A caption below: "Same data. Same model. Three different visual impressions. Which one represents agreement?" A red annotation: "Agreement cannot depend on how you made the figure.")
Talking Point: I want you to feel this problem before we solve it. I have shown you the same dataset and the same model prediction three times, with three different axis scales. In the first plot, they look identical. In the second, they look like significant disagreement. In the third, you are genuinely unsure. But the data and model have not changed — only the axis scale has. This is the fundamental problem with visual inspection as the basis for an engineering conclusion. A plot is a presentation choice, and presentation choices are not physics. If your conclusion about agreement changes depending on how you drew the figure, then your conclusion is not grounded in the data — it is grounded in aesthetics. The rest of this lecture is about replacing aesthetic judgment with a defined numerical protocol.

slide 3: What Does "Agreement" Mean Technically?
(Image: A horizontal number line diagram. A vertical dashed line labeled "Model Prediction μ_model" is shown at a fixed position, with a light shaded band around it labeled "Model Uncertainty ±U_model." A measurement point labeled "Experimental Outcome μ_exp" is shown with a horizontal two-headed arrow labeled "±U_exp." Three cases are stacked vertically below the number line. Case A: the two uncertainty bands overlap broadly — green label "Overlap — agreement plausible." Case B: the bands just barely touch — yellow label "Marginal — quantify carefully." Case C: the bands are fully separated — red label "No overlap — genuine discrepancy requires explanation." A note: "Agreement depends on both uncertainties, not just one.")
Talking Point: Agreement in the engineering sense means that the difference between your measurement and your model prediction is not larger than what can be explained by the combined uncertainty of both quantities. Not just the measurement uncertainty — both. A model prediction carries uncertainty too, from approximations, parameter assumptions, and model-form limitations. The question is not "is the measurement equal to the prediction?" but "is the difference between measurement and prediction consistent with zero, given the combined uncertainty of both?" When uncertainty intervals overlap, agreement is plausible. When they do not overlap at all, you have a genuine discrepancy that requires explanation — either the model is wrong, the measurement is wrong, or your uncertainty estimates are wrong. And "plausible" is not enough — you still need to quantify it with a specific metric.

slide 4: Validation Metrics — Name Your Criterion Before You Look
(Image: Three labeled formula boxes arranged vertically on a clean white background. Box 1: "Relative Difference: d_rel = |Q_exp − Q_model| / Q_model × 100%. Interprets as: how far apart are the values as a percentage?" Box 2: "Within Combined Uncertainty: |Q_exp − Q_model| < √(U_exp² + U_model²). Interprets as: is the difference smaller than the combined uncertainty at the stated confidence level?" Box 3: "Normalized Agreement Index: NAI = |Q_exp − Q_model| / √(U_exp² + U_model²). Interprets as: how many combined standard deviations apart are the two values? NAI < 1 suggests agreement." A red text box at the bottom: "REQUIRED: state your metric and acceptance threshold BEFORE computing the comparison. Post-hoc metric selection is not defensible.")
Talking Point: A validation metric is a defined, reproducible procedure for quantifying the distance between a measurement and a model prediction in units that account for the relevant uncertainties. Three representative forms are shown here. Relative difference is the simplest — it gives you the percentage gap — but it does not account for uncertainty. The combined-uncertainty check adds the critical test: is the gap smaller than what your uncertainties can explain? The normalized agreement index generalizes this by expressing disagreement in units of combined standard uncertainty — values below one suggest agreement. The critical rule is at the bottom: you must state your metric and acceptance threshold before you look at the result. If you choose your metric after seeing the data — post-hoc, meaning after the fact — you can always find one that confirms what you hoped to find. That is a well-documented bias in experimental reporting, and it is why pre-commitment is required in every deliverable in this course. The term "post-hoc" is worth knowing: it appears in academic literature, review comments, and professional engineering standards, and recognizing it will help you identify this specific failure mode when you see it in your own work or someone else's.

slide 5: A Worked Comparison — Full Numerical Example
(Image: A structured step-by-step worked example box. Measurement: "Q_exp = 14.7 N, U_exp = ±0.4 N (95% CI, partial derivative method)." Model prediction: "Q_model = 15.3 N, U_model = ±0.6 N (from model parameter uncertainty)." Step 1: "Relative difference: |14.7 − 15.3| / 15.3 × 100% = 3.9%." Step 2: "Combined uncertainty: U_comb = √(0.4² + 0.6²) = 0.72 N = 4.7% of model prediction." Step 3: "Comparison: 3.9% < 4.7% — the difference is within combined uncertainty." Step 4 (highlighted in gold): "Conclusion: Agreement is supported at 95% confidence using the relative-difference-within-combined-uncertainty metric, pre-committed before this calculation." A note at the bottom: "All steps must appear in your report in this order.")
Talking Point: Let me walk through a complete worked example so you see exactly what the report section looks like. I have a force measurement of 14.7 N with a 95% uncertainty interval of plus or minus 0.4 N, and a model prediction of 15.3 N with an estimated model uncertainty of plus or minus 0.6 N. Step one: compute the relative difference — 3.9%. Step two: compute the combined uncertainty as the quadrature sum of the two individual uncertainties — 0.72 N, or 4.7% of the model value. Step three: compare — 3.9% is less than 4.7%, so the difference falls within the combined uncertainty. Step four: state the conclusion, tied to the metric and the confidence level. Every step must appear in your report, in this order. Skipping steps, or stating the conclusion without showing the calculation, is insufficient documentation for an engineering comparison.

slide 6: When Agreement Fails — Disagreement Is Diagnostic
(Image: A two-branch flowchart starting from "Comparison Metric Exceeds Threshold." Branch 1: "Measurement problem?" — sub-nodes: "Calibration error? Installation effect? Uncontrolled confounder?" Branch 2: "Model problem?" — sub-nodes: "Wrong equation form? Invalid assumption? Missing physical effect?" Both branches converge at "Investigate root cause — then classify the discrepancy type." Below: two residual plots. Left: residuals randomly scattered around zero — labeled "Random residuals: consistent with measurement noise." Right: residuals trending upward with the independent variable — labeled "Structured residuals: evidence of missing physics, not noise.")
Talking Point: When your validation metric tells you that measurement and model do not agree within your defined criterion, that is not a failed experiment — it is a finding. The disagreement is diagnostic: it tells you that either your measurement has an unaccounted-for problem or your model is missing something. Your job is to diagnose which. The residuals — defined as: residual = measured value − model prediction at each data point — carry that diagnostic information. Random residuals that scatter around zero with no pattern suggest measurement noise. Residuals that show a systematic trend — always positive, or increasing with the independent variable — suggest a model-form limitation: the model is missing a physical effect that grows with that variable. Structured disagreement is more useful than agreement, because it tells you something specific about what the model is missing.

slide 7: Comparing Two Measurements — The Multi-Sensor Case
(Image: Two side-by-side numerical scenarios. Scenario A: "Sensor 1: 14.7 ± 0.4 N. Sensor 2: 14.2 ± 0.5 N. |14.7 − 14.2| / √(0.4² + 0.5²) = 0.5 / 0.64 = 0.78 — within 1 combined σ. Agreement supported." Below: green checkmark. Scenario B: "Sensor 1: 14.7 ± 0.4 N. Sensor 2: 16.1 ± 0.5 N. |14.7 − 16.1| / √(0.4² + 0.5²) = 1.4 / 0.64 = 2.19 — exceeds 2 combined σ. Disagreement: investigate sensor calibration or installation." Below: red annotation. A caption: "The same metric applies whether you are comparing measurement to model or measurement to measurement.")
Talking Point: The same validation metric framework applies when you are comparing two measurements of the same quantity using different sensors — which is exactly what the lab activity this week requires. The calculation is identical: compute the difference between the two sensor readings, compute the combined uncertainty, ask whether the difference falls within the combined uncertainty. If yes, the two sensors are internally consistent — you have cross-validated one against the other. If no, at least one sensor has a problem: calibration error, installation effect, or a systematic difference in what the two sensors are physically responding to. Using two independent sensors to measure the same quantity is one of the most powerful tools in experimental practice, because independent evidence is more convincing than a single instrument, and this lab is designed specifically around that.

slide 8: Showing Margins and Requirements on Plots
(Image: A clean figure showing experimental data (blue dots with error bars) and a model prediction (solid red curve) on a plot with labeled axes. In addition to the data and model, three horizontal reference lines are added: a solid gold line labeled "Requirement: P ≤ 35 W," a dashed green line labeled "Design Margin: P ≤ 30 W," and a light gray shaded band between 30 and 35 W labeled "Margin Zone." Several data points fall below 30 W (in the "pass" region), one falls in the margin zone, and none exceed 35 W. A caption: "The plot itself communicates whether the result passes or fails — no text narrative required.")
Talking Point: A brief but important practical skill: when your experiment is connected to a design requirement — a load limit, a temperature bound, an efficiency threshold — you can and should annotate your figure with that requirement directly. Put a horizontal line on the plot at the requirement value. Add a second line at the design margin if one exists. Now the figure communicates the conclusion without requiring the reader to cross-reference a separate document: every data point is visually classified as passing or failing. This technique connects your experimental result to the engineering decision the experiment was designed to inform. It is not a deep dive into systems design — that is for other courses — but it is a practical communication skill that will appear in every technical report you produce in industry. In this course, when your experiment has a defined acceptance criterion, that criterion should appear on the figure.

slide 9: Building a Defensible Comparison Argument — The Required Structure
(Image: A structured six-step outline formatted as a numbered list, styled like a report section template. "(1) State validation metric — name it, explain why it is appropriate for this data type." "(2) State acceptance threshold — defined before the comparison." "(3) Apply metric numerically — show all intermediate calculations." "(4) State conclusion — agreement or disagreement — tied to the confidence level and the stated metric." "(5) If disagreement: describe residual structure — random or systematic?" "(6) If agreement: note the conditions under which agreement holds and any caveats about range or extrapolation." A red annotation at steps 1 and 2: "Pre-commitment required — these two steps must precede step 3.")
Talking Point: I want to give you the required structure for a defensible comparison argument, because this structure will appear in every deliverable from this point forward. First: name your validation metric and justify why it is appropriate. Different data types and different modeling claims call for different metrics. Second: state your acceptance threshold before computing. Third: show the full numerical calculation with all intermediate steps. Fourth: state the conclusion explicitly — agreement or disagreement — tied to the metric and the confidence level. Fifth: if the comparison reveals disagreement, classify the residual structure. And sixth: if the comparison supports agreement, note the conditions and limits of that agreement. This is the argument structure for the Comparative Analysis Memo due this week, and it is the same structure I will look for in every subsequent lab report.

slide 11: Evaluating Another's Work — The Four-Part Critique Structure
(Image: A clean four-box layout on a white background, each box color-coded. Box 1 (green): "(1) What is correct and well-supported? — Name specific evidence. Identify where the report does what it claims." Box 2 (red): "(2) What is incorrect or unsupported? — Identify claims that are wrong, or conclusions that go beyond what the data shows." Box 3 (gold): "(3) What is missing? — Identify required elements that are absent: uncertainty quantification, pre-committed metric, stated confidence level, or required calculation steps." Box 4 (blue): "(4) What specific improvements would most strengthen this report? — Be actionable. Not 'add more analysis' but 'state the validation metric before applying it and show the intermediate calculation.'" A red caption below: "Name the criteria before you read — for the same reason you commit to a metric before you compute." A note: "This structure applies to the in-class comparative analysis exercise and to every peer evaluation in this course.")
Talking Point: In a moment, we are going to apply everything from today's lecture to a sample lab report. Before you open it, I want to give you the four questions that organize a defensible critique — the same way I asked you to commit to a validation metric before computing. First: what is correct and well-supported? Identify what the report does right, with specific evidence. This matters because vague praise is useless — if you cannot say why something is correct, you have not evaluated it. Second: what is incorrect or unsupported? Find claims that are wrong, or conclusions that extend beyond what the data actually shows. Third: what is missing? This is the category most students underuse. Missing elements — no uncertainty quantification, no pre-committed metric, no confidence level stated — are not vague weaknesses; they are specific absences that a checklist can identify. Fourth: what specific improvements would most strengthen the report? Actionable, specific, tied to the assignment requirements. "Add more analysis" is not a critique — "state the validation metric before applying it and show the propagation calculation" is. You are committing to these four criteria now, before you read — for the same reason we commit to a metric before we compute. The quality of your critique will be evaluated on how specifically and accurately you apply all four.

slide 12: Evaluator Moment — Is This Comparison Defensible?
(Image: A quote box on a clean white background showing a student's lab report excerpt: "After computing the data, I tried both the relative difference metric and the combined-uncertainty metric. The relative difference showed agreement (4.2% < 5.0% threshold). I used that metric for my conclusion." A bold prompt below: "Is this comparison defensible? What, if anything, is wrong with the approach?")
Talking Point: Take two minutes and evaluate this claim. [Pause — allow student responses.] The approach is not defensible. The student computed the data first, tried multiple metrics, and then chose the one that gave agreement. This is post-hoc metric selection — a form of motivated reasoning that allows any dataset to be made to "agree" by selecting the metric that best suits the outcome. The rule is explicit: the metric and acceptance threshold must be stated before the comparison is computed. If the student genuinely believes relative difference is the right metric for this data type, they should be able to explain why on first principles, independent of which metric produces the more favorable answer. The fact that they tried both and chose the favorable one is the problem — not the metric itself. Real credibility requires that the comparison could have shown disagreement, and the student was committed to reporting it regardless.

slide 13: Closure
Structured comparison is not complicated, but it requires discipline at a moment when most people are tempted to eyeball the result and call it good. The discipline is this: define the metric and commit to the threshold, compute it numerically, show your work, and state the conclusion clearly — including the conditions under which it holds. When the comparison reveals disagreement, read the residuals and diagnose before you conclude. When it supports agreement, annotate your figure with the acceptance criterion so the result is visible to anyone who reads the report. This week's Comparative Analysis Memo is your first practice of this full argument structure. What you build this week is the foundational skill for every validation activity in Phase II and Phase III — and for every engineering report you write after this course is over.
