slide 1: Title Slide
Title: Why Prediction Must Precede Measurement
Talking Point: Welcome to Phase II. Everything we did in E1 — measuring, propagating uncertainty, comparing to a model — was preparation for the discipline we are about to practice: prediction before exposure. In Phase I you were reactive analysts. In Phase II you become disciplined modelers, and the single most important habit of a disciplined modeler is this — you commit to what you expect before you see what happened. That is what this lecture is about, and it is the governing principle of everything we do in Weeks 6 through 10.

slide 2: Two Scenarios, Same Data
(Image: A split panel illustration — left side labeled "Scenario A: Postdiction" shows a scatter plot with a smooth curve clearly fitted through the data points, with an annotation reading "model adjusted after data collected." Right side labeled "Scenario B: Prediction" shows the same scatter plot with an identical-looking curve, but the curve was drawn before the data points were revealed, annotated "model committed before data released." Both panels look identical at a glance, but only Scenario B has an arrow pointing to a lock icon labeled "Pre-commitment document." Background tone is dark with gold text to match course theme.)
Talking Point: I want you to look at these two panels and tell me which one represents valid engineering. At first glance, they look identical — the model matches the data in both cases. But there is a fundamental difference. In Scenario A, someone built a model after they already saw the data, and they adjusted the parameters until the curve fit well. In Scenario B, someone committed to the model before the data was released, and the match — or mismatch — tells us something true about how well the model captures reality. These two scenarios are structurally indistinguishable without documentation. That is why the pre-commitment document is not paperwork — it is the evidence that separates validation from curve fitting.

slide 4: The Cognitive Bias That Makes Postdiction Feel Like Validation
(Image: A brain-shaped diagram divided into two halves. The left half is labeled "Before Data" and contains a simple idealized sine wave with the label "Mental model: clean, complete." The right half is labeled "After Data" and shows the same sine wave overlaid with noisy scatter data — but with a note bubble pointing to the brain saying "I knew it all along." Below the diagram is a short axis arrow labeled "Time →" with a lock icon at the "Before Data" end and an X-circle icon at the "After Data" end, illustrating that the window for honest prediction closes once data is seen. The tone is conceptual and slightly cautionary — this is a cognitive trap.)
Talking Point: There is a well-documented cognitive bias that makes postdiction feel exactly like genuine prediction — it is called hindsight bias, and it affects trained engineers just as much as anyone else. Once you have seen the data, you cannot un-see it. Your brain retroactively adjusts your memory of what you expected, so the model you build after the fact feels like the model you would have built before. The insidious thing is that this happens without conscious intent — you are not trying to deceive anyone, including yourself. The result is a model that fits the data you have but may fail completely on data you have not collected yet. Postdiction produces models that describe observations rather than predict behavior, and in aerospace engineering, the difference between those two things has consequences. The pre-commitment document is the structural solution: it creates a record that cannot be retroactively adjusted.

slide 5: The AMVF Runs Left to Right — And That Direction Is Not Arbitrary
(Image: The AMVF flowchart diagram reproduced in the course color scheme — gold nodes on dark background. The flowchart shows the mathematical modeling branch on the left and the physical modeling branch on the right, with the model node at top-left, the experimental data node at bottom-right, and validation as the comparison step in the center. A bold left-to-right arrow runs across the top of the diagram labeled "Model development logically precedes data collection." A second arrow in red, pointing right-to-left, is labeled with an X: "Postdiction: building the model after seeing data." The physical modeling branch nodes are highlighted to show they are the focus of E2.)
Talking Point: You saw this diagram in Week 1 Lecture 1, and I told you then that the left-to-right flow is not arbitrary. Now I want to make that concrete. The AMVF runs from model development on the left to experimental data on the right because that is the logical sequence: you build a model, you make a prediction based on it, and then you measure to test whether the prediction holds. If you run the flowchart backwards — if you collect data first and build a model to match it — you are not using experimental data to validate a model. You are using experimental data to construct a model, and those are fundamentally different activities. In E2, we are going to hold ourselves to the forward direction, every time. Your prediction document is your evidence that you ran the flowchart in the right direction.

Talking Point: Many of you remember what it felt like in Week 5 when you had to commit to a prediction for an experiment you did not run, before seeing any data. Some of you found it uncomfortable — you wanted to see the data first and then decide what to say about it. That discomfort is exactly the right signal. What you felt is the gap between knowing something and predicting something, and in Phase II that gap is what we are asking you to cross every time, for every experiment. The Tier 1 Phase 2 prediction memo was practice. The Tier 2 pre-lab prediction document is the real thing, and it is a gate — not a suggestion. You do not get equipment access until your prediction is documented and reviewed. The quality of your reasoning in that document is what you are being evaluated on, not whether your prediction turned out to be correct.

Talking Point: The Engineering Advisory Council — the external board of working aerospace engineers who give us feedback on this curriculum — has told us explicitly that what E2 is teaching is exactly what new hires are expected to do when they join an organization. You walk into a legacy lab or an existing test program. Someone hands you documentation for an experiment that was designed ten years ago. Your job is to understand what was done and why, identify the limitations of that approach, and propose improvements using better sensors, better analysis methods, or better understanding. That is precisely what the Tier 2 canned experiments are structured to give you practice with. These are not academic exercises where you run a script and fill in blanks. They are recognizable test engineer work — and the skill of walking up to someone else's experimental infrastructure and knowing how to evaluate it is one that gets you hired and keeps you employed.

slide 8: Closure
This lecture opened E2 with the discipline that defines Phase II: prediction must precede measurement, and that sequence must be documented. Postdiction feels like validation because hindsight bias is invisible from the inside — but it is structurally indistinguishable from curve fitting and produces models that describe the past rather than predict the future. The AMVF flowchart runs left to right because model development is logically prior to data collection, and your prediction document is the evidence that you followed that sequence. In Lecture 2 this week, we will move from the epistemics of why prediction matters to the mechanics of how to build a defensible one: the five-element anatomy of the prediction document, the formal vocabulary for system response features and acceptance criteria, and the gate procedure before equipment access. Come ready to produce the real thing.
