# Sampling, Aliasing & the Limits of Digital Measurement
## Why the fastest thing you can't see can still destroy your data

Watch a helicopter's rotor blades in a film and you will sometimes see them appear to rotate backward — or hover perfectly still at full throttle. Nothing is wrong with the blades. Something is wrong with the camera.

A film camera captures still frames at a fixed rate — typically 24 per second. If the blades complete almost exactly one full rotation between each frame, they appear stationary in the footage. If they complete slightly more than a full rotation, they appear to spin slowly backward. The blades are moving at full speed. The camera, sampling the scene too slowly relative to the rotation rate, produces an artifact that looks like real motion. This optical illusion is not just a curiosity. It is a precise physical analogy for one of the most consequential pitfalls in digital data acquisition: **aliasing**.

---

Every time you ran data collection in E1, someone had already chosen the sample rate for you. In E2, that decision is yours to justify. Every digital measurement system — every DAQ board, every microcontroller analog input, every data logger — works the same way the film camera does. It takes samples of a continuous physical signal at discrete moments in time. Between samples, it knows nothing. The physical signal could be doing anything, and the measurement system will never know.

The theorem formalizes the helicopter illusion precisely. The same artifact appears in accelerometer data from a vibration test when sample rate is insufficient: the 380 Hz structural mode appears in your data file as a 120 Hz signal, and there is nothing in the data that tells you it is wrong. Here is the formal statement: the **Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem** establishes the fundamental limit: to faithfully capture a signal that contains frequency content up to some maximum frequency *f_max*, you must sample at a rate of at least *2f_max*. This threshold — twice the highest frequency in the signal — is called the **Nyquist rate**. Sample faster than this and you can reconstruct the original signal. Sample slower, and you cannot.

What happens when you sample slower than the Nyquist rate? The same thing that happened to the helicopter blades. High-frequency content in the signal does not disappear — it folds back into the measured spectrum and appears as a false low-frequency signal. A vibration at 500 Hz, sampled at 600 samples per second, will appear in your data as a 100 Hz oscillation. That 100 Hz component is not in the physical system. It is an artifact of the sampling process. And critically: it looks exactly like a real signal. There is no asterisk next to it, no error flag, no warning in the data file. It is a ghost that wears the costume of truth.

Here is the part that trips up experienced engineers: **signal bandwidth** is a property of the physical phenomenon you are measuring, not a property of your sensor or your DAQ system. If you are measuring acoustic vibrations in a rocket nozzle, the signal bandwidth is determined by the dynamics of the combustion process — not by your microphone, not by your DAQ board, and not by whatever sampling rate you happened to inherit from a previous project. Before you choose a sampling rate, you must ask: what is the highest frequency my signal of interest actually contains?

This sounds simple. In practice it requires genuine knowledge of the physics. A structural vibration test on a beam might have fundamental modes below 100 Hz and essentially no relevant content above 1 kHz — a 2,500 samples-per-second rate would be conservative and appropriate. A combustion pressure transient in a rocket engine test might contain critical content up to 50 kHz — that same 2,500 samples-per-second rate would alias catastrophically, producing clean-looking data that is entirely fictitious.

When you commit to a sampling rate, you are making a claim. You are stating, before you collect any data, that your signal contains no frequency content above half your chosen sampling rate. If that claim is wrong — if the signal has energy at higher frequencies that you didn't anticipate — the data is silently corrupted. No error message. No obvious artifact. Just a dataset that reflects a physical reality that doesn't exist.

**Anti-aliasing filters** exist to enforce this claim in hardware. By attenuating signal content above the Nyquist frequency before the signal reaches the analog-to-digital converter, they prevent high-frequency content from folding into the measured spectrum. They are not optional safeguards — they are the mechanism that makes the Nyquist guarantee meaningful. Their design (specifically, where to set the cutoff frequency relative to the signal bandwidth and the sampling rate) is one of the topics the W7 lectures will address directly.

This reading stops here, before filter design, before DAQ configuration choices, before MATLAB analysis. Those topics are covered in the companion reading (R2) and in the Week 7 lectures. The job of this reading is narrower: ensure that before you walk into class, you understand *why* these choices matter.

Sampling rate is not a technical setting you accept as a lab default. It is a modeling commitment. It encodes assumptions about the physical world that, if wrong, produce data that looks real and is wrong. When your Tier 2 canned experiment uses a specific sampling rate, you should be able to defend that choice on physical grounds — and identify what you would be missing if the rate were too low.

---

**The Takeaway:** Aliasing is not an obscure artifact of bad equipment — it is a fundamental consequence of digital sampling that is invisible in the data itself, and the only defense is understanding the physics of your signal well enough to make the Nyquist commitment confidently before you collect the first data point.
