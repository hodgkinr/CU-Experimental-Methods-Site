slide 1: Title Slide
Title: Comparing Datasets — Intervals for Differences & Engineering Equivalence
Talking Point: Every comparison we have made in this course so far has been between a model prediction and a single experimental measurement, or between a single measurement and an accepted reference value. Week 9 extends the toolkit to a harder and more common case: comparing two or more datasets — multiple test conditions, multiple groups, multiple configurations — and making a quantitative, defensible statement about whether they are meaningfully different. This is the question that drives most real engineering decisions: is Configuration A statistically distinguishable from Configuration B? Is the treatment effect real, or is it within the noise? The statistical tools we cover today give you the vocabulary to answer those questions without relying on visual inspection.

Talking Point: Let me frame the challenge before introducing the tools. In E1, the comparison question was simple: does this measurement agree with a prediction? One number, one comparison, one uncertainty estimate. In E2 Weeks 6 through 8, we extended that to comparing a full dataset to a model prediction curve — the residual analysis we covered in Lecture 1 this week. But the full comparison problem is often harder than either of those cases. In a real experimental campaign, you might run the same experiment at two different operating conditions, or with two different configurations, or with two different test setups. The question is whether those two datasets are meaningfully different from each other — whether the difference you observe is real, or whether it is within the noise of your measurement system. That question requires comparing means, comparing distributions, and making a statement about whether the observed difference is statistically distinguishable. That is what today's lecture provides.

slide 3: Standard Error of the Mean — Why More Trials Reduce Uncertainty
**Use standard error (σ/√n) when comparing means. Use standard deviation (σ) when characterizing individual measurement scatter.**
(Image: A conceptual illustration showing three sample distributions, each representing the distribution of a sample mean from an experiment with n = 5, n = 20, and n = 100 trials respectively. All three distributions are centered at the same true value. The n=5 distribution is wide and low. The n=20 distribution is narrower and taller. The n=100 distribution is very narrow and tall. Each distribution is labeled with its standard error: "σ/√5," "σ/√20," "σ/√100" respectively, where σ is the population standard deviation. An annotation reads: "The uncertainty in the mean decreases as 1/√n — this is why averaging reduces the mean's uncertainty even if each trial has the same noise." A box below the three distributions reads: "Key distinction: σ is the standard deviation of individual measurements; σ/√n is the standard error — the uncertainty in the mean of n measurements." The visual clearly shows the distributions narrowing as n increases.)
Talking Point: Before we compare datasets, I need to make sure the statistical foundation from Week 2 is solid — specifically the distinction between the standard deviation of individual measurements and the standard error of the mean. The standard deviation characterizes how much scatter there is in any single measurement trial — it describes the width of the measurement distribution. The standard error of the mean is the uncertainty in the average value computed from n trials, and it is equal to the standard deviation divided by the square root of n. This is the answer to the question I posed at the start of E2 Week 6: why does the uncertainty in the mean decrease with more trials? Because the mean is a statistic computed from n measurements, and each additional measurement provides additional information that narrows the estimate of the true value. The standard error is what we use when comparing means across datasets — not the standard deviation. If you compare means using standard deviations, you are overestimating the uncertainty in the comparison by the factor of square root of n.

slide 4: Compare the Difference, Not Two Separate Intervals
(Image: Two sample means feed into one horizontal number line labeled "Difference Δ = mean_B − mean_A." A 95% Welch confidence interval is drawn around Δ. A vertical zero line marks "no difference," and a shaded engineering equivalence region spans −2.0 to +2.0 N. A note reads "Separate confidence-interval overlap is a visual clue, not the decision rule.")
Talking Point: When the question is whether two means differ, build an interval for the difference itself. For independent groups with unequal or unknown variances, a Welch analysis is a defensible default; for measurements made in matched pairs, analyze the paired differences. The interval for delta directly answers whether zero is plausible under the procedure and shows the range of differences compatible with the data. Overlap between two separate confidence intervals can be a quick visual clue, but it is not the formal comparison and cannot establish engineering equivalence.

slide 5: What Statistical Significance Actually Means — And What It Does Not
(Image: A conceptual diagram with two independent axes. X-axis: "Statistical significance" — a bar ranging from "Not statistically significant" to "Statistically significant" with a vertical dashed line labeled "Significance threshold (α = 0.05)." Y-axis: "Engineering significance" — a bar ranging from "Below engineering threshold" to "Above engineering threshold" with a horizontal dashed line labeled "Engineering acceptance criterion." Four quadrants: Top-right: "Statistically significant AND engineering significant — the difference matters." Top-left: "Statistically significant but NOT engineering significant — real difference, but too small to matter for the intended use." Bottom-right: "Engineering significant but NOT statistically significant — the difference would matter, but sample size is too small to detect it." Bottom-left: "Neither — no meaningful difference." Each quadrant has a brief decision note. The diagram communicates that statistical significance and engineering significance are independent dimensions.)
Talking Point: Statistical and engineering significance answer different questions. Statistical inference asks what differences are compatible with the data under stated assumptions. Engineering significance asks whether those differences matter for the intended use. A p-value is not the probability that a difference arose by chance; it is the probability, assuming the null model and analysis assumptions are true, of obtaining a test statistic at least as extreme as the observed one. The engineering threshold must be committed separately and before looking at the result.

slide 6: Worked Welch Comparison — Statistical Difference, Engineering Equivalence
(Image: A worked comparison card. A: n=25, mean=100.0 N, s=0.5 N. B: n=25, mean=100.3 N, s=0.5 N. Difference Δ=B−A=0.30 N. Standard error of Δ=0.141 N. Welch 95% CI for Δ=[0.02, 0.58] N. A number line shows zero just outside the interval and the full interval inside the pre-specified equivalence region [−2.0, +2.0] N.)
Talking Point: The interval for the difference is the centerpiece. With 25 independent observations in each configuration, sample standard deviations of 0.5 newtons, and means of 100.0 and 100.3 newtons, the estimated difference is 0.30 newtons. A Welch calculation gives a standard error of about 0.141 newtons and a 95 percent confidence interval for the difference of about 0.02 to 0.58 newtons. Because zero is outside that interval, the data support a nonzero mean difference at the five-percent level under the Welch assumptions. But the entire interval lies inside the pre-specified engineering equivalence region of minus 2.0 to plus 2.0 newtons. The configurations are statistically distinguishable and engineering-equivalent for this intended use. That conclusion comes from the difference interval and the committed region, not from eyeballing two separate error bars.

slide 7: When Datasets Disagree — Three Interpretive Paths
(Image: A flowchart with three branches from the root question "Two datasets show a meaningful difference. What does it tell you?" Branch 1: "Both datasets from different test conditions → The system responds differently under the two conditions — the model should account for the sensitivity." Branch 2: "Both datasets from nominally identical conditions → Measurement system issue OR uncontrolled variable — investigate repeatability and setup." Branch 3: "One dataset is a model prediction, one is experimental → Model-data discrepancy — apply residual analysis from W8." Each branch has a MATLAB analysis type labeled below it: Branch 1 → "Confidence interval comparison + regression across conditions." Branch 2 → "Repeatability analysis — standard deviation of repeated trials at same condition." Branch 3 → "Residual plot + discrepancy classification (W8 vocabulary)." The flowchart is clean with the same visual style as previous flowcharts in the course.)
Talking Point: When two datasets differ, the interpretation depends on what the two datasets represent. If the datasets are from two different test conditions — different configurations, different operating points — then the difference is a characterization of the system: it responds differently under the two conditions, and that information is useful for understanding the model. If the datasets are from nominally identical conditions and should be repeatable, then a meaningful difference indicates a measurement system problem or an uncontrolled variable. And if one of the datasets is a model prediction and the other is experimental data, you are back in the residual analysis territory from Lecture 1. The statistical comparison tools are the same across all three cases — confidence intervals, standard errors, overlap tests — but the engineering interpretation depends entirely on what the datasets represent. Before you compute, ask: what are these two datasets, and what would a difference between them mean?

slide 8: Hypothesis Testing — Conceptual Level
(Image: A conceptual diagram of hypothesis testing without any formulas. Two states are shown: "Null hypothesis H₀: the two population means are the same — any observed difference is due to sampling variability." "Alternative hypothesis H₁: the two population means are genuinely different — the observed difference is real." A sample difference distribution is shown in the center, labeled "Distribution of possible differences if H₀ is true." A vertical dashed line on the right tail of the distribution is labeled "Observed difference." A shaded region in the right tail beyond the observed difference is labeled "p-value: the probability of observing a difference this large if H₀ is true." A decision box below reads: "If p-value < α (typically 0.05), we reject H₀ — the difference is statistically significant." A caution note: "p < 0.05 does NOT mean: there is a 95% probability that H₁ is true. It means: the data is unlikely under H₀. These are different statements.")
Talking Point: The null model specifies no population-mean difference, together with the sampling and analysis assumptions. The p-value is the probability, under that null model, of obtaining a test statistic at least as extreme as the observed one. It is not the probability that the null is true, not the probability that the result arose "by chance," and not the probability that the difference is real. For engineering decisions, the confidence interval for the difference is especially useful because the same interval can be compared with zero and with the pre-specified acceptance or equivalence region.

slide 9: Implementation Pattern — One Interval for Δ
(Image: A compact MATLAB-style workflow: choose independent Welch or paired design; compute delta; compute its standard error and degrees of freedom; obtain the t critical value; form CI_delta = delta ± tcrit*SE_delta; compare CI_delta with zero and with [−Delta_accept,+Delta_accept]. A warning reads "Do not decide from separate-CI overlap.")
Talking Point: You are not expected to memorize the Welch degrees-of-freedom equation. You are expected to choose the design correctly: independent groups call for a two-sample method such as Welch; repeated measurements on matched articles call for a paired-difference method. Your output must show the estimated difference, its confidence interval, and the pre-specified engineering region. Then state two conclusions separately: what the statistical interval says about zero and what the engineering comparison says about adequacy or equivalence.

Talking Point: The statistical comparison tools you learn today connect to the Tier 2 report in two directions. First, they are analysis tools: you use confidence interval comparison to evaluate whether the model prediction falls within the uncertainty bounds of your experimental mean, and you report the standard error and CI in the uncertainty analysis section. Second, and less obviously, they are diagnostic tools for the improvement proposal. If your confidence intervals were too wide to distinguish the model prediction from the data — not because the model is good, but because your measurement was imprecise — then the improvement proposal should address that: more trials at each condition, or a more precise sensor, or reduced noise through better signal conditioning. The improvement must be grounded in the statistical result. "Our CI was too wide to make a conclusion about the model at the required precision" is a specific finding, and the proposed fix — a specific increase in sample size or measurement precision — is the corresponding improvement. That is the quality of improvement proposal that earns full credit.

slide 11: Closure
Comparing datasets requires more than visual inspection — it requires computing confidence intervals for each mean, evaluating overlap to test for statistical distinguishability, and connecting the statistical result to an engineering conclusion about whether the difference matters for the intended use. Statistical significance and engineering significance are independent: a statistically significant difference may be too small to matter, and an engineering-significant difference may not be detectable at your current sample size. The null hypothesis framework tells you whether the data is inconsistent with the assumption of identical populations — it does not tell you whether the difference is important. That judgment belongs to you and depends on the pre-stated acceptance criterion, which is why the acceptance criterion must be written before the data is collected. In Lecture 2 today, we return to Monte Carlo simulation and introduce regression — tools that extend the comparison from means at single operating points to models evaluated across full datasets. Together, these two lectures complete the E2 statistical comparison toolkit that you will apply in the lab session and in Sections 3 and 4 of your Tier 2 group report.
