slide 2: Regression — What It Is and What It Is Not
(Image: A scatter plot with two regression fits overlaid. The x-axis is labeled "Applied load (N)" and the y-axis is labeled "Measured deflection (mm)." Scatter data points are plotted in gray. Two model fit lines are overlaid: a linear regression line labeled "Linear fit: R² = 0.97" in gold, and a physical model prediction curve labeled "Theory (Euler-Bernoulli): no free parameters" in a dashed white line. The linear regression line passes through the scatter well, but diverges slightly from the theory curve at the upper end of the range. An annotation on the linear fit: "High R² — but this is not the same as validating the physical model." An annotation on the theory curve: "Physical model prediction — tested against data independently." Caption: "Regression fits a curve to data. Validation tests a physical model against data. These are different activities.")
Talking Point: Regression and model validation are not the same activity, and the distinction matters. Regression fits a mathematical curve — linear, polynomial, exponential — to a dataset to describe how the output varies with the inputs. A high R-squared value tells you that the curve explains most of the variance in the data. What it does not tell you is whether the curve is the physically correct model. In this example, a linear regression achieves R-squared of 0.97 — the fit is good. But the physical model for this system predicts a specific nonlinear relationship based on the governing equations. The regression line does not know about the physics — it only knows about the data in this range. If you tested outside this range, the linear regression might extrapolate poorly even though it fits well here, while the physics-based model extrapolates correctly because it captures the governing behavior. This is why regression is a diagnostic tool for model adequacy — you compare the residuals from the physical model to the residuals from a purely data-driven fit — not a validation verdict in itself.

slide 3: Goodness-of-Fit Measures — What R² Does and Does Not Tell You
(Image: Three scatter plots in a row, each showing a model fit and residual plot below it. Plot 1: "R² = 0.98, random residuals." The fit is good and residuals are scattered randomly around zero. Caption: "Both R² and residuals support model adequacy." Plot 2: "R² = 0.98, systematic trend in residuals." The fit looks good visually, but the residual plot shows a clear nonlinear trend — the model overestimates at low and high values and underestimates in the middle. Caption: "High R² but systematic residuals — model is structurally wrong. R² alone is misleading." Plot 3: "R² = 0.82, random residuals." The fit is imperfect visually but residuals are randomly scattered. Caption: "Lower R² but random residuals — model form may be correct; precision is the limitation." An overall caption at the bottom: "Always inspect residual plots alongside R². R² tells you about variance explained. Residual structure tells you about model form adequacy." The three plots are compact but readable, with consistent axis scales.)
Talking Point: R-squared is one of the most overused and misinterpreted statistics in engineering data analysis, and I want to spend a moment on what it actually measures. R-squared tells you what fraction of the variance in the data is explained by the model — equivalently, how much of the total scatter around the mean is captured by the fitted curve. A high R-squared does not mean the model is physically correct. R²=0.98 can coexist with systematic residuals because R² averages model performance across the full dataset — a systematic trend that is positive in one region and negative in another can average out, masking the structural failure. Look at the middle panel: R-squared is 0.98, which looks excellent, but the residuals — the pattern of errors — show a clear nonlinear trend. The model is overestimating at the extremes and underestimating in the middle. That pattern tells you the model is missing a physical effect — the form is wrong even though the overall fit looks good by the R-squared metric. The right panel shows the opposite: R-squared is 0.82, which might make you nervous, but the residuals are random scatter. The model form may be correct — the scatter is consistent with measurement noise — and the lower R-squared reflects measurement precision, not model error. The lesson is to always look at the residual plot alongside R-squared. R-squared is a summary statistic; the residual structure is the diagnostic.

slide 4: Returning to Monte Carlo — From E1 to E2 Complexity
(Image: A two-column comparison. Left column: "E1 Week 2 — Scalar Monte Carlo." Shows the P=IV example: single output (power), two input variables (V, I), simple product model, independent Gaussian inputs. A histogram of the simulated power distribution is shown. Caption: "10,000 trials, two independent inputs, one nonlinear function." Right column: "E2 Week 9 — Multi-variable Monte Carlo." Shows a more complex aerospace system: three input variables (structural stiffness k, damping c, applied force F), correlated inputs (k and c are correlated in a material sense), and a nonlinear governing equation. A histogram of the simulated peak deflection distribution is shown — slightly skewed, not perfectly Gaussian. Caption: "10,000 trials, three correlated inputs, nonlinear governing equation — output distribution is non-Gaussian." An annotation between the columns: "Same conceptual tool — different level of complexity. The core MATLAB structure is identical." The visual continuity between E1 and E2 is the central message of this slide.)
Talking Point: We first used Monte Carlo in Week 2 to propagate uncertainty through the power equation: P equals IV. We had two independent inputs — voltage and current — and we sampled them from Gaussian distributions based on the instrument specifications. The output distribution was nearly Gaussian because the function was nearly linear. In Week 9, we apply the same conceptual tool to a more complex aerospace system, and three things change. First, there are more input variables — perhaps stiffness, damping, and applied force. Second, some inputs may be correlated — for example, if both stiffness and damping come from the same material property measurement, their uncertainties are not independent. Third, the governing equation is nonlinear — the output distribution will not be Gaussian even if all the inputs are Gaussian. The MATLAB structure is almost identical to what you did in Week 2: sample each input, evaluate the model for each sample set, collect the output distribution, compute the standard deviation and percentiles. What changes is the interpretation — the non-Gaussian output requires you to report percentiles rather than assuming a symmetric uncertainty interval, and the correlated inputs require a joint sampling strategy rather than independent draws.

slide 5: Correlated Inputs — Advanced Case (Note)
(Image: A simple note card layout on the slide. Gold border around a card-shaped area with the header: "Advanced case — beyond the scope of this course." Body text: "Most groups in E2 have independent inputs and should use standard randn(N,1) sampling for each input. If two or more of your inputs come from the same source (e.g., same calibration procedure or same material batch), their uncertainties are correlated — independent sampling will underestimate worst-case outcomes. See W9 Reading 2 for the Cholesky approach and MATLAB implementation." At the bottom of the card: a small arrow icon pointing to "R2: Correlated Inputs in Monte Carlo — An Advanced Case." The rest of the slide is dark background with minimal text, emphasizing this is a brief informational note rather than lecture content.)
Talking Point: I want to flag one case that is beyond the scope of the standard E2 workflow — I am mentioning it here for completeness, not because most groups will need it. If two or more of your input uncertainties come from the same source — the same calibration procedure, the same material property measurement, the same batch test — those inputs are correlated, and sampling them independently in Monte Carlo will underestimate the worst-case output uncertainty. The standard fix uses a Cholesky decomposition to generate samples with the correct joint distribution. This is an advanced topic. The full explanation, geometric intuition, and MATLAB code are in Reading 2 this week. If you have genuinely independent inputs — which most of you do — use the standard randn approach from the previous slide and do not worry about this. If you have reason to believe two of your inputs are correlated, read R2 before implementing your Monte Carlo.

slide 6: MATLAB Implementation — Multi-Variable Monte Carlo
(Image: A MATLAB code excerpt with results, styled consistently with the previous lecture's code slides. Code: "% Multi-variable Monte Carlo for peak deflection"; "% NOTE: This example assumes independent inputs (standard case)."; "% For correlated inputs, see slide 5 and W9 Reading 2."; "N = 10000; % Number of trials"; "% Input distributions (mean, std)"; "k = 1200 + 50*randn(N,1); % Stiffness [N/m]"; "c = 0.15 + 0.01*randn(N,1); % Damping ratio [-]"; "F = 25.0 + 0.5*randn(N,1); % Applied force [N]"; "% Evaluate model"; "x_peak = F ./ k .* (1 ./ sqrt(1 - c.^2)); % Approx. peak response"; "% Statistics"; "mean_x = mean(x_peak);"; "std_x = std(x_peak);"; "CI_95 = prctile(x_peak, [2.5 97.5]); % 95% CI from percentiles" Results panel: "mean_x = 0.02087 m" | "std_x = 0.00094 m" | "CI_95 = [0.01903, 0.02271] m." A histogram of x_peak is shown to the right, slightly right-skewed. A MATLAB-style caption: "Peak deflection: 0.0209 m, 95% CI [0.0190, 0.0227] m. Non-Gaussian distribution — reported from percentiles.")
Talking Point: This is the core Monte Carlo workflow: list each uncertain input, justify a simple input model from repeat data, a calibration statement, a manufacturer coverage statement, or an explicit conservative Type B assumption; sample inputs; evaluate the model; and summarize the output with a labeled coverage interval. Gaussian is not the automatic model for a sensor specification. The percentile bounds shown here are a central 95 percent Monte Carlo coverage interval, not a confidence interval, and they are meaningful only under the stated input and dependence assumptions.

slide 7: Comparing Monte Carlo to Partial Derivative Propagation — When They Agree and When They Do Not
(Image: A comparison table with three rows. Column headers: "Method," "E1 Power example result," "E2 structural example result." Row 1 — "Partial derivative propagation": "P = 12.3 ± 0.21 W" | "x_peak = 0.0209 ± 0.00096 m (symmetric, Gaussian assumed)." Row 2 — "Monte Carlo (10,000 trials)": "P = 12.3 ± 0.19 W" | "x_peak = 0.0209, 95% CI [0.0190, 0.0227] m (non-symmetric, from percentiles)." Row 3 — "Agreement assessment": "Agree closely — power equation nearly linear, Gaussian inputs, symmetric output" | "Agree closely in mean; CI slightly asymmetric in Monte Carlo — nonlinearity causes minor departure from Gaussian." A caption: "When the model is linear and inputs are Gaussian, both methods agree. When the model is nonlinear, Monte Carlo captures asymmetric distributions that partial derivatives cannot." A note: "When the two methods give substantially different results, the Monte Carlo result should be used — it makes fewer assumptions about the model's linearity.")
Talking Point: First-order propagation linearizes the measurement model near the operating point; it does not require the true output to be Gaussian. Monte Carlo can represent nonlinear transformations and chosen input distributions, but it is not automatically more correct. If methods differ, diagnose before selecting a result: confirm common standard-uncertainty inputs, equations and units; check nonlinearity and truncation; verify distribution and percentile choices; test sample convergence; and identify any shared error sources. Report which assumption explains the difference. Covariance is awareness-level here unless the assignment provides evidence and a supported estimate.

slide 8: Residual Analysis Across a Full Dataset — Applying Regression to Validate Model Adequacy
(Image: A two-panel MATLAB figure. Top panel: Scatter data plotted against the independent variable (applied load, x-axis) with three overlaid lines: (1) the physical model prediction (dashed gold), (2) a linear regression fit to the data (solid white), and (3) the ±σ uncertainty band around the physical model (shaded region). Data points are gray circles. The physical model is slightly below the data in the middle range. Bottom panel: Residual plot (model minus data) vs. the same x-axis. Residuals are shown as scatter points with the ±σ uncertainty band shown as dashed lines. The residuals in the middle range are consistently positive, indicating systematic over-prediction. A diagonal trend line through the residuals is labeled "Systematic trend — possible model-form issue at mid-range conditions." Caption: "Regression is not the model. The physical model prediction and the regression line diverge at mid-range — the residual analysis reveals the discrepancy.")
Talking Point: Here is how regression connects to the model adequacy question. In the top panel, you see the physical model prediction, a linear regression fit to the data, and the experimental scatter. They look similar at a glance. But the bottom panel — the residual plot of the physical model versus the data — shows a systematic positive trend in the middle range: the physical model consistently over-predicts the measured deflection at intermediate loads. This is a model-form error signal: the linear regression tracks the data well because it has a free slope parameter that adjusts to fit, but the physical model is constrained by its governing equation and cannot adjust. The systematic residual tells you the physical model is missing something in the mid-range behavior — possibly a compliance effect at intermediate loads that the boundary condition assumption does not capture. This is exactly the analysis you did in Week 8 using residuals at individual points, now applied systematically across the full dataset using regression as the reference. The regression is a diagnostic reference, not the answer.

slide 10: The Improvement Proposal — Grounding in Statistical Analysis
(Image: A worked example of an improvement proposal entry, formatted as a short structured paragraph. Header: "Section 6 — Improvement Proposal, Category (c): Experimental Design." Body: "The current test matrix includes 8 repeated trials at each of 3 operating conditions. The 95% confidence interval for the mean at each condition is ±1.8 N (from standard error analysis). The model-data discrepancy at the critical operating condition (Condition 2) is 2.1 N — just outside the confidence interval at the current sample size. To resolve the model adequacy question at this condition with 95% confidence, we estimate a sample size of approximately 14 trials per condition would be required (based on the observed standard deviation of 4.2 N and the target half-CI of 1.1 N). An additional 6 trials per condition would close the gap. Alternatively, reducing the random measurement noise through improved signal conditioning (DAQ improvement category b) would achieve the same sensitivity improvement without additional trials." A gold border frames this example and a label reads: "This is the quality of improvement proposal that earns full credit — specific, grounded in data, actionable.")
Talking Point: This is what a full-quality improvement proposal looks like when it is grounded in the statistical analysis. The group identified that their confidence interval width of 1.8 newtons is just barely larger than the model-data discrepancy of 2.1 newtons — close enough that they cannot definitively classify the discrepancy as either real or within noise. They then computed the sample size that would be needed to narrow the confidence interval sufficiently to resolve the question. They arrived at a specific number of additional trials — six more per condition. And they noted an alternative path: improving measurement precision through signal conditioning would achieve the same sensitivity without requiring additional trials. Both paths are specific, quantified, and grounded in the analysis results. That is the standard. The improvement proposal is not a reflection on what went well — it is a forward-looking engineering recommendation, derived from your data, that would make the experiment more informative. That is the skill E3 is going to ask you to demonstrate independently for an experiment you design yourself.

slide 11: Closure
Regression is a diagnostic tool, not a validation verdict. R-squared describes variance explained; residual structure guides a broader diagnosis. Monte Carlo extends to nonlinear and multi-input systems, but it is only as defensible as the input and dependence assumptions. First-order propagation is a local linearization and does not inherently require Gaussian output. When methods disagree, diagnose inputs, equations, units, distributions, convergence, and dependence before choosing an interpretation. These tools support the Tier 2 adequacy argument only when connected to the intended use.
