What a Strong Technical Document Does

A useful engineering document does more than store results. It tells the reader what question was asked, how the answer was produced, how uncertain that answer is, and what decision the result supports.

That is why this course keeps pushing on captions, graph framing, result statements, and plain-language explanations. A technically correct result still fails if the reader cannot tell what it means.

Executive Summary Structure

When a full report is unnecessary, an executive summary is often the better engineering format.

Use this structure:

  1. What was the goal?
  2. How was it done?
  3. What was found?
  4. What does it mean for the system or decision?

The challenge is not making it shorter. The challenge is making it shorter without losing the argument.

Presentation Advice

Students often show a figure, a component, or a result without first locating it in the larger system. That creates the "disembodied part" problem.

Before showing any figure or component, answer:

  1. What system are we talking about?
  2. Where does this thing fit in that system?
  3. Why does it matter?

Figure Checklist

For any serious MATLAB figure or technical plot, ask:

  1. Why does this figure exist?
  2. What is on each axis, with units?
  3. Where did the data come from?
  4. What uncertainties are shown?
  5. What trend is visible?
  6. How does the trend compare to the model?
  7. What could explain the discrepancy?
  8. What does this imply about the system?
