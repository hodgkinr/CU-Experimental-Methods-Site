Your Experiment Should Outlive the Lab Session

An undocumented experiment is an experiment that cannot be checked, reproduced, or extended. In practice, engineering documentation has two jobs: it preserves what you did and why you did it, and it makes your work reviewable by someone who was not there.

GitHub is used in this course as a lightweight portfolio and process record. You are not being asked to become a software engineer. You are using a versioned public workspace so each experiment has a traceable home.

What the Repository Is For

A repository is just a folder with memory. It keeps a history of what changed, when it changed, and how your work developed over time.

In ASEN 3501, that matters because your portfolio is not just a pile of final deliverables. It is evidence of how you reasoned, revised, and documented your engineering work.

The Three-Step Workflow

The basic workflow has three steps:

  1. Stage the files you want in the next snapshot.
  2. Commit a labeled snapshot with a short note about what changed.
  3. Push that snapshot so the updated record is available online.

For this course, the key idea is the commit. Think of it as a labeled save that explains the work to a future teammate.

A Good First Use in This Course

One practical starting point is the Tier 1 Phase 1 pre-lab design work. That first entry can include:

  • your hypothesis
  • your independent and dependent variables
  • your AMVF annotation
  • a brief note about what experiment you are preparing to run

The Habit That Matters Most

GitHub is most useful when it records process, not just final products. A repository with a single end-of-semester upload tells no story. Small, clear commits create a professional record of how your thinking developed.

That record is part of the portfolio.
