Comparing Datasets — Statistical Significance & Confidence Interval Overlap

Extends the Week 2 statistical foundations to the case of comparing two or more datasets, or comparing a dataset to a model prediction across a range of conditions. Covers comparing means and standard errors, confidence interval overlap as a practical test of whether two results are distinguishable, and hypothesis testing at a conceptual level — what it means to reject a null hypothesis, and the difference between statistical significance (unlikely by chance) and engineering significance (meaningful for the intended use). Students practice interpreting cases where statistical tests give different answers depending on the comparison chosen.

    slide 1: Title Slide Title: Comparing Datasets — Statistical Significance & Confidence Interval Overlap Talking Point: Every comparison we have made in this course so far has been between a model prediction and a single experimental measurement, or between a single measurement and an accepted reference value. Week 9 extends the toolkit to a harder and more common case: comparing two or more datasets — multiple test conditions, multiple groups, multiple configurations — and making a quantitative, defensible statement about whether they are meaningfully different. This is the question that drives most real engineering decisions: is Configuration A statistically distinguishable from Configuration B? Is the treatment effect real, or is it within the noise? The statistical tools we cover today give you the vocabulary to answer those questions without relying on visual inspection.

That question requires comparing means, comparing distributions, and making a statement about whether the observed difference is statistically distinguishable. That is what today's lecture provides.

    slide 3: Standard Error of the Mean — Why More Trials Reduce Uncertainty Use standard error (σ/√n) when comparing means. Use standard deviation (σ) when characterizing individual measurement scatter. (Image: A conceptual illustration showing three sample distributions, each representing the distribution of a sample mean from an experiment with n = 5, n = 20, and n = 100 trials respectively. All three distributions are centered at the same true value. The n=5 distribution is wide and low. The n=20 distribution is narrower and taller. The n=100 distribution is very narrow and tall. Each distribution is labeled with its standard error: "σ/√5," "σ/√20," "σ/√100" respectively, where σ is the population standard deviation. An annotation reads: "The uncertainty in the mean decreases as 1/√n — this is why averaging reduces the mean's uncertainty even if each trial has the same noise." A box below the three distributions reads: "Key distinction: σ is the standard deviation of individual measurements; σ/√n is the standard error — the uncertainty in the mean of n measurements." The visual clearly shows the distributions narrowing as n increases.) Talking Point: Before we compare datasets, I need to make sure the statistical foundation from Week 2 is solid — specifically the distinction between the standard deviation of individual measurements and the standard error of the mean. The standard deviation characterizes how much scatter there is in any single measurement trial — it describes the width of the measurement distribution. The standard error of the mean is the uncertainty in the average value computed from n trials, and it is equal to the standard deviation divided by the square root of n. This is the answer to the question I posed at the start of E2 Week 6: why does the uncertainty in the mean decrease with more trials? Because the mean is a statistic computed from n measurements, and each additional measurement provides additional information that narrows the estimate of the true value. The standard error is what we use when comparing means across datasets — not the standard deviation. If you compare means using standard deviations, you are overestimating the uncertainty in the comparison by the factor of square root of n.

    slide 4: Confidence Interval Overlap — The Practical Comparison Test (Image: Four scenarios of confidence interval comparison, arranged in a two-by-two grid. Each scenario shows two means (represented as vertical bars with horizontal caps at their 95% confidence interval bounds). Scenario 1: "Clear non-overlap." The two confidence intervals are completely separated. Caption: "Statistically distinguishable at 95% confidence — the difference is real at this precision level." Scenario 2: "Partial overlap." The confidence intervals overlap by about 30% of their width. Caption: "Likely distinguishable — a formal significance test is needed to confirm." Scenario 3: "Full overlap, one inside the other." One interval is entirely contained within the other. Caption: "Not distinguishable at this sample size — results are consistent with same population mean." Scenario 4: "Confidence intervals overlap at one edge." Just touching. Caption: "Borderline case — significance depends on the specific test and its assumptions." Each scenario has the same visual style: gold bars, labeled mean values, labeled confidence bounds.) Talking Point: The most practical comparison tool for engineering data is confidence interval overlap. If you have computed a 95% confidence interval for the mean of each dataset, you can use the overlap — or lack of overlap — between those intervals as a quick test of whether the datasets are statistically distinguishable. If the two confidence intervals do not overlap at all, the datasets are statistically distinguishable at 95% confidence. If the intervals have moderate overlap, a formal significance test may be needed to confirm. If one interval is entirely contained within the other, the datasets are not distinguishable at this sample size — the precision of your measurement is insufficient to tell them apart. One important caveat on borderline cases: CI overlap is a conservative test. If 95% CIs don't overlap, the means are certainly distinguishable at 95% confidence. If they partially overlap, the means may still be statistically different at the 5% level — use a t-test for borderline cases rather than declaring "not significant" based on visual overlap alone. The confidence interval overlap test is intuitive and visually interpretable, which is why it is preferred in experimental engineering over reporting raw p-values, which require careful interpretation to avoid the common misreading of "statistically significant means practically important." I will come back to that distinction in a moment, because it is critical for the Tier 2 analysis.

    slide 5: What Statistical Significance Actually Means — And What It Does Not (Image: A conceptual diagram with two independent axes. X-axis: "Statistical significance" — a bar ranging from "Not statistically significant" to "Statistically significant" with a vertical dashed line labeled "Significance threshold (α = 0.05)." Y-axis: "Engineering significance" — a bar ranging from "Below engineering threshold" to "Above engineering threshold" with a horizontal dashed line labeled "Engineering acceptance criterion." Four quadrants: Top-right: "Statistically significant AND engineering significant — the difference matters." Top-left: "Statistically significant but NOT engineering significant — real difference, but too small to matter for the intended use." Bottom-right: "Engineering significant but NOT statistically significant — the difference would matter, but sample size is too small to detect it." Bottom-left: "Neither — no meaningful difference." Each quadrant has a brief decision note. The diagram communicates that statistical significance and engineering significance are independent dimensions.) Talking Point: Statistical significance and engineering significance are not the same thing, and confusing them leads to wrong conclusions. A result is statistically significant if the probability that the observed difference arose by chance — under the null hypothesis that the populations are identical — is below a threshold, typically five percent. A result is engineering significant if the difference is large enough to matter for the intended use. These two things are independent. Here is a concrete case where they diverge: Configuration A reads 100.0 ± 0.2 N; Configuration B reads 100.3 ± 0.2 N. The 95% CIs don't overlap — the 0.3 N difference is statistically significant. But 0.3 N is far below the 2% engineering threshold of 2.0 N. Real difference, irrelevant consequence — top-left quadrant. With a large enough sample, you can achieve statistical significance for a difference that is utterly irrelevant to the engineering decision — a load cell reads 0.05% higher under two test configurations, and with 10,000 measurements, that difference is statistically significant. But if your validation criterion is agreement within 2%, a 0.05% difference has no engineering significance. Conversely, a difference that is large enough to matter for the intended use may not achieve statistical significance if your sample size is too small to distinguish it from noise. For the Tier 2 analysis, the question is always: does this difference matter for the stated intended use? Statistical significance is the test of whether the difference is real. Engineering significance is the test of whether it matters.

Two configurations of the same experimental rig are tested: Configuration A is the baseline, Configuration B has a modification. The sample means and 95% confidence intervals are shown. Branch 2: "Both datasets from nominally identical conditions → Measurement system issue OR uncontrolled variable — investigate repeatability and setup." Branch 3: "One dataset is a model prediction, one is experimental → Model-data discrepancy — apply residual analysis from W8." Branch 2 → "Repeatability analysis — standard deviation of repeated trials at same condition." Branch 3 → "Residual plot + discrepancy classification (W8 vocabulary)." The flowchart is clean with the same visual style as previous flowcharts in the course.) Talking Point: When two datasets differ, the interpretation depends on what the two datasets represent. If the datasets are from two different test conditions — different configurations, different operating points — then the difference is a characterization of the system: it responds differently under the two conditions, and that information is useful for understanding the model. If the datasets are from nominally identical conditions and should be repeatable, then a meaningful difference indicates a measurement system problem or an uncontrolled variable. And if one of the datasets is a model prediction and the other is experimental data, you are back in the residual analysis territory from Lecture 1. The statistical comparison tools are the same across all three cases — confidence intervals, standard errors, overlap tests — but the engineering interpretation depends entirely on what the datasets represent. Before you compute, ask: what are these two datasets, and what would a difference between them mean?

    slide 8: Hypothesis Testing — Conceptual Level (Image: A conceptual diagram of hypothesis testing without any formulas. Two states are shown: "Null hypothesis H₀: the two population means are the same — any observed difference is due to sampling variability." "Alternative hypothesis H₁: the two population means are genuinely different — the observed difference is real." A sample difference distribution is shown in the center, labeled "Distribution of possible differences if H₀ is true." A vertical dashed line on the right tail of the distribution is labeled "Observed difference." A shaded region in the right tail beyond the observed difference is labeled "p-value: the probability of observing a difference this large if H₀ is true." A decision box below reads: "If p-value < α (typically 0.05), we reject H₀ — the difference is statistically significant." A caution note: "p < 0.05 does NOT mean: there is a 95% probability that H₁ is true. It means: the data is unlikely under H₀. These are different statements.") Talking Point: I want to give you a conceptual understanding of formal hypothesis testing without requiring you to implement the full statistical machinery from scratch. The null hypothesis is the assumption that any difference you observe between two datasets is due to sampling variability alone — the two populations are actually identical. The alternative hypothesis is that the populations are genuinely different. The p-value is the probability of observing a difference as large as the one you measured, assuming the null hypothesis is true. If the p-value is below the threshold — conventionally five percent — we say the result is statistically significant: the data is unlikely to have arisen by chance under the null hypothesis. Here is the caution I want you to internalize: a p-value below five percent does not mean there is a 95% probability that the difference is real. It means the data is inconsistent with the null hypothesis at the five-percent level. This distinction matters because p-values are routinely misread, and in engineering contexts, a misread p-value can lead to over-confident conclusions. The confidence interval overlap test I showed you earlier is often more interpretable in engineering contexts precisely because it is more directly connected to the physical question: are these two values close enough to be considered the same for the intended use?

    slide 9: MATLAB Implementation — Comparing Means and CI Overlap (Image: A MATLAB code excerpt with results shown alongside. Code lines (styled as MATLAB code): "% Sample means and standard deviations from two test conditions"; "n_A = 25; mean_A = 47.3; std_A = 3.2;"; "n_B = 25; mean_B = 52.1; std_B = 2.9;"; "SE_A = std_A / sqrt(n_A); % Standard error of mean A"; "SE_B = std_B / sqrt(n_B); % Standard error of mean B"; "CI_A = mean_A + [-1 1] 2 SE_A; % 95% CI (approx k=2)"; "CI_B = mean_B + [-1 1] 2 SE_B;"; "% Check overlap"; "overlap = CI_A(2) >= CI_B(1); % Logical: 1=overlap, 0=no overlap." Results panel below the code: "CI_A = [45.9, 48.6] N" | "CI_B = [50.9, 53.2] N" | "overlap = 0 (no overlap — statistically distinguishable at 95% CI)." A note below the code: "k=2 is an approximation for 95% CI. For small samples (n < 30), use the t-distribution critical value instead of 2." The code is readable and styled as a MATLAB script with comment lines.) Talking Point: Here is the MATLAB implementation you will use in the lab session today. The standard error of the mean is the sample standard deviation divided by the square root of n — that is the line that converts from the standard deviation of individual measurements to the uncertainty in the mean. The 95% confidence interval uses a coverage factor of 2 as an approximation for large samples. For small samples — fewer than about 30 trials — the t-distribution gives a larger critical value than 2, and you would use MATLAB's tinv function to get the exact value for the degrees of freedom. The overlap check is a simple logical comparison: does the upper bound of the first confidence interval exceed the lower bound of the second? In this example it does not — CI_A ends at 48.6 and CI_B begins at 50.9 — so the intervals do not overlap and the datasets are statistically distinguishable. This is the starting point for your Section 4 uncertainty analysis in the Tier 2 report: you need to compute the 95% confidence interval for your measurement means and compare them to the model prediction or to a second test condition.

    slide 11: Closure Comparing datasets requires more than visual inspection — it requires computing confidence intervals for each mean, evaluating overlap to test for statistical distinguishability, and connecting the statistical result to an engineering conclusion about whether the difference matters for the intended use. Statistical significance and engineering significance are independent: a statistically significant difference may be too small to matter, and an engineering-significant difference may not be detectable at your current sample size. The null hypothesis framework tells you whether the data is inconsistent with the assumption of identical populations — it does not tell you whether the difference is important. That judgment belongs to you and depends on the pre-stated acceptance criterion, which is why the acceptance criterion must be written before the data is collected. In Lecture 2 today, we return to Monte Carlo simulation and introduce regression — tools that extend the comparison from means at single operating points to models evaluated across full datasets. Together, these two lectures complete the E2 statistical comparison toolkit that you will apply in the lab session and in Sections 3 and 4 of your Tier 2 group report.
