    slide 1: Title Slide Title: Regression, Goodness-of-Fit & Monte Carlo for Multi-Variable Systems Talking Point: In Week 2, you ran Monte Carlo simulation for a scalar derived quantity — electrical power computed from current and voltage — and you compared the result to the partial derivative propagation method. That was an introduction to the conceptual tool. In Week 9, we return to Monte Carlo with a harder problem: a multi-variable aerospace system where several inputs have correlated uncertainty, and where the model relationship between inputs and outputs is nonlinear. We also introduce regression as a tool for assessing model adequacy across a full dataset — not at a single operating point, but across all the conditions you tested. These two tools complete the E2 statistical analysis toolkit, and they map directly to sections of your Tier 2 group report.

    slide 2: Regression — What It Is and What It Is Not (Image: A scatter plot with two regression fits overlaid. The x-axis is labeled "Applied load (N)" and the y-axis is labeled "Measured deflection (mm)." Scatter data points are plotted in gray. Two model fit lines are overlaid: a linear regression line labeled "Linear fit: R² = 0.97" in gold, and a physical model prediction curve labeled "Theory (Euler-Bernoulli): no free parameters" in a dashed white line. The linear regression line passes through the scatter well, but diverges slightly from the theory curve at the upper end of the range. An annotation on the linear fit: "High R² — but this is not the same as validating the physical model." An annotation on the theory curve: "Physical model prediction — tested against data independently." Caption: "Regression fits a curve to data. Validation tests a physical model against data. These are different activities.") Talking Point: Regression and model validation are not the same activity, and the distinction matters. Regression fits a mathematical curve — linear, polynomial, exponential — to a dataset to describe how the output varies with the inputs. A high R-squared value tells you that the curve explains most of the variance in the data. What it does not tell you is whether the curve is the physically correct model. In this example, a linear regression achieves R-squared of 0.97 — the fit is good. But the physical model for this system predicts a specific nonlinear relationship based on the governing equations. The regression line does not know about the physics — it only knows about the data in this range. If you tested outside this range, the linear regression might extrapolate poorly even though it fits well here, while the physics-based model extrapolates correctly because it captures the governing behavior. This is why regression is a diagnostic tool for model adequacy — you compare the residuals from the physical model to the residuals from a purely data-driven fit — not a validation verdict in itself.

    slide 3: Goodness-of-Fit Measures — What R² Does and Does Not Tell You (Image: Three scatter plots in a row, each showing a model fit and residual plot below it. Plot 1: "R² = 0.98, random residuals." The fit is good and residuals are scattered randomly around zero. Caption: "Both R² and residuals support model adequacy." Plot 2: "R² = 0.98, systematic trend in residuals." The fit looks good visually, but the residual plot shows a clear nonlinear trend — the model overestimates at low and high values and underestimates in the middle. Caption: "High R² but systematic residuals — model is structurally wrong. R² alone is misleading." Plot 3: "R² = 0.82, random residuals." The fit is imperfect visually but residuals are randomly scattered. Caption: "Lower R² but random residuals — model form may be correct; precision is the limitation." An overall caption at the bottom: "Always inspect residual plots alongside R². R² tells you about variance explained. Residual structure tells you about model form adequacy." The three plots are compact but readable, with consistent axis scales.) Talking Point: R-squared is one of the most overused and misinterpreted statistics in engineering data analysis, and I want to spend a moment on what it actually measures. R-squared tells you what fraction of the variance in the data is explained by the model — equivalently, how much of the total scatter around the mean is captured by the fitted curve. A high R-squared does not mean the model is physically correct. R²=0.98 can coexist with systematic residuals because R² averages model performance across the full dataset — a systematic trend that is positive in one region and negative in another can average out, masking the structural failure. Look at the middle panel: R-squared is 0.98, which looks excellent, but the residuals — the pattern of errors — show a clear nonlinear trend. The model is overestimating at the extremes and underestimating in the middle. That pattern tells you the model is missing a physical effect — the form is wrong even though the overall fit looks good by the R-squared metric. The right panel shows the opposite: R-squared is 0.82, which might make you nervous, but the residuals are random scatter. The model form may be correct — the scatter is consistent with measurement noise — and the lower R-squared reflects measurement precision, not model error. The lesson is to always look at the residual plot alongside R-squared. R-squared is a summary statistic; the residual structure is the diagnostic.

    slide 4: Returning to Monte Carlo — From E1 to E2 Complexity (Image: A two-column comparison. Left column: "E1 Week 2 — Scalar Monte Carlo." Shows the P=IV example: single output (power), two input variables (V, I), simple product model, independent Gaussian inputs. A histogram of the simulated power distribution is shown. Caption: "10,000 trials, two independent inputs, one nonlinear function." Right column: "E2 Week 9 — Multi-variable Monte Carlo." Shows a more complex aerospace system: three input variables (structural stiffness k, damping c, applied force F), correlated inputs (k and c are correlated in a material sense), and a nonlinear governing equation. A histogram of the simulated peak deflection distribution is shown — slightly skewed, not perfectly Gaussian. Caption: "10,000 trials, three correlated inputs, nonlinear governing equation — output distribution is non-Gaussian." An annotation between the columns: "Same conceptual tool — different level of complexity. The core MATLAB structure is identical." The visual continuity between E1 and E2 is the central message of this slide.) Talking Point: We first used Monte Carlo in Week 2 to propagate uncertainty through the power equation: P equals IV. We had two independent inputs — voltage and current — and we sampled them from Gaussian distributions based on the instrument specifications. The output distribution was nearly Gaussian because the function was nearly linear. In Week 9, we apply the same conceptual tool to a more complex aerospace system, and three things change. First, there are more input variables — perhaps stiffness, damping, and applied force. Second, some inputs may be correlated — for example, if both stiffness and damping come from the same material property measurement, their uncertainties are not independent. Third, the governing equation is nonlinear — the output distribution will not be Gaussian even if all the inputs are Gaussian. The MATLAB structure is almost identical to what you did in Week 2: sample each input, evaluate the model for each sample set, collect the output distribution, compute the standard deviation and percentiles. What changes is the interpretation — the non-Gaussian output requires you to report percentiles rather than assuming a symmetric uncertainty interval, and the correlated inputs require a joint sampling strategy rather than independent draws.

    slide 5: Correlated Inputs — Advanced Case (Note) (Image: A simple note card layout on the slide. Gold border around a card-shaped area with the header: "Advanced case — beyond the scope of this course." Body text: "Most groups in E2 have independent inputs and should use standard randn(N,1) sampling for each input. If two or more of your inputs come from the same source (e.g., same calibration procedure or same material batch), their uncertainties are correlated — independent sampling will underestimate worst-case outcomes. See W9 Reading 2 for the Cholesky approach and MATLAB implementation." At the bottom of the card: a small arrow icon pointing to "R2: Correlated Inputs in Monte Carlo — An Advanced Case." The rest of the slide is dark background with minimal text, emphasizing this is a brief informational note rather than lecture content.) Talking Point: I want to flag one case that is beyond the scope of the standard E2 workflow — I am mentioning it here for completeness, not because most groups will need it. If two or more of your input uncertainties come from the same source — the same calibration procedure, the same material property measurement, the same batch test — those inputs are correlated, and sampling them independently in Monte Carlo will underestimate the worst-case output uncertainty. The standard fix uses a Cholesky decomposition to generate samples with the correct joint distribution. This is an advanced topic. The full explanation, geometric intuition, and MATLAB code are in Reading 2 this week. If you have genuinely independent inputs — which most of you do — use the standard randn approach from the previous slide and do not worry about this. If you have reason to believe two of your inputs are correlated, read R2 before implementing your Monte Carlo.

    slide 6: MATLAB Implementation — Multi-Variable Monte Carlo (Image: A MATLAB code excerpt with results, styled consistently with the previous lecture's code slides. Code: "% Multi-variable Monte Carlo for peak deflection"; "% NOTE: This example assumes independent inputs (standard case)."; "% For correlated inputs, see slide 5 and W9 Reading 2."; "N = 10000; % Number of trials"; "% Input distributions (mean, std)"; "k = 1200 + 50randn(N,1); % Stiffness [N/m]"; "c = 0.15 + 0.01randn(N,1); % Damping ratio [-]"; "F = 25.0 + 0.5randn(N,1); % Applied force [N]"; "% Evaluate model"; "x_peak = F ./ k . (1 ./ sqrt(1 - c.^2)); % Approx. peak response"; "% Statistics"; "mean_x = mean(x_peak);"; "std_x = std(x_peak);"; "CI_95 = prctile(x_peak, [2.5 97.5]); % 95% CI from percentiles" Results panel: "mean_x = 0.02087 m" | "std_x = 0.00094 m" | "CI_95 = [0.01903, 0.02271] m." A histogram of x_peak is shown to the right, slightly right-skewed. A MATLAB-style caption: "Peak deflection: 0.0209 m, 95% CI [0.0190, 0.0227] m. Non-Gaussian distribution — reported from percentiles.") Talking Point: Here is the MATLAB implementation for a three-variable Monte Carlo analysis of a structural system. The inputs are stiffness, damping ratio, and applied force, each sampled from Gaussian distributions based on the uncertainty estimates from the sensor specifications and material characterization. The model is a simple approximate expression for peak dynamic response. After 10,000 evaluations, we extract the mean and standard deviation of the output distribution, but notice that we report the 95% confidence interval using the percentile function rather than by computing mean plus-or-minus two standard deviations. This is important: for a non-Gaussian output distribution, the symmetric interval computed from the standard deviation may not correspond to the correct 95% coverage. The percentile-based interval is distribution-free and always gives the correct coverage. In this example the distribution is slightly right-skewed — the upper tail is longer than the lower tail — which is physically meaningful: there is more room for the system to exceed the nominal response than to fall below it. Report both the mean and the percentile-based interval in the Tier 2 uncertainty analysis section.

    slide 7: Comparing Monte Carlo to Partial Derivative Propagation — When They Agree and When They Do Not (Image: A comparison table with three rows. A caption: "When the model is linear and inputs are Gaussian, both methods agree. When the model is nonlinear, Monte Carlo captures asymmetric distributions that partial derivatives cannot." A note: "When the two methods give substantially different results, the Monte Carlo result should be used — it makes fewer assumptions about the model's linearity.") Talking Point: This comparison table connects back to the Week 2 activity explicitly, as the blueprint asks us to do. In the power equation example, partial derivative propagation and Monte Carlo give almost identical results, because the power equation is nearly linear and the Gaussian input assumption is good. In the structural dynamics example, they agree on the mean but the Monte Carlo confidence interval is slightly asymmetric. The partial derivative method assumes a linear model and symmetric Gaussian output — it cannot capture the right-skewed distribution we saw in the previous slide. The Monte Carlo result is more accurate when the model is nonlinear because it makes fewer assumptions about the output distribution shape. When the two methods agree closely, either can be used, but for your Tier 2 report, we ask you to report both and compare them — that comparison is evidence that you understand when each method is appropriate and where they diverge. If they give substantially different results, use the Monte Carlo result and explain why the partial derivative method underestimates the uncertainty.

    slide 8: Residual Analysis Across a Full Dataset — Applying Regression to Validate Model Adequacy (Image: A two-panel MATLAB figure. Top panel: Scatter data plotted against the independent variable (applied load, x-axis) with three overlaid lines: (1) the physical model prediction (dashed gold), (2) a linear regression fit to the data (solid white), and (3) the ±σ uncertainty band around the physical model (shaded region). The physical model prediction and the regression line diverge at mid-range — the residual analysis reveals the discrepancy.") Talking Point: Here is how regression connects to the model adequacy question. In the top panel, you see the physical model prediction, a linear regression fit to the data, and the experimental scatter. They look similar at a glance. But the bottom panel — the residual plot of the physical model versus the data — shows a systematic positive trend in the middle range: the physical model consistently over-predicts the measured deflection at intermediate loads. This is a model-form error signal: the linear regression tracks the data well because it has a free slope parameter that adjusts to fit, but the physical model is constrained by its governing equation and cannot adjust. The systematic residual tells you the physical model is missing something in the mid-range behavior — possibly a compliance effect at intermediate loads that the boundary condition assumption does not capture. This is exactly the analysis you did in Week 8 using residuals at individual points, now applied systematically across the full dataset using regression as the reference. The regression is a diagnostic reference, not the answer.

Talking Point: Section 4 of the Tier 2 group report is the uncertainty analysis section, and it requires all five of these elements. You already completed the partial derivative derivation in the prediction document — reproduce it and update it with the actual measured values. The dominant error source ranking tells you which input drives the total uncertainty, and you should check whether that ranking changes across the operating conditions you tested — sometimes a different input dominates at high versus low values of the independent variable. The Monte Carlo simulation uses 10,000 samples at minimum, draws inputs from Gaussian distributions based on the actual sensor specifications, and outputs a distribution histogram with the mean and 95% CI reported from percentiles. Then you compare the partial derivative result to the Monte Carlo result and note the agreement or disagreement. And finally — the hardest part — you interpret whether the experiment is sensitive enough. If the total uncertainty is comparable to the model-data discrepancy you found, you cannot distinguish whether the discrepancy is real or within measurement noise. That is an important finding: it means a more precise measurement is needed to resolve the model adequacy question at the required confidence level. The ultimate question for Section 3B is whether the total uncertainty bound you compute here allows you to evaluate your committed Week 6 acceptance criterion. If the propagated uncertainty is comparable to the model-data discrepancy, you cannot resolve whether your acceptance criterion is met or not — and that finding belongs in your conclusion.

Talking Point: This is what a full-quality improvement proposal looks like when it is grounded in the statistical analysis. The group identified that their confidence interval width of 1.8 newtons is just barely larger than the model-data discrepancy of 2.1 newtons — close enough that they cannot definitively classify the discrepancy as either real or within noise. They then computed the sample size that would be needed to narrow the confidence interval sufficiently to resolve the question. Both paths are specific, quantified, and grounded in the analysis results. That is the standard. The improvement proposal is not a reflection on what went well — it is a forward-looking engineering recommendation, derived from your data, that would make the experiment more informative. That is the skill E3 is going to ask you to demonstrate independently for an experiment you design yourself.

    slide 11: Closure Regression is a diagnostic tool for evaluating model adequacy across a full dataset — not a substitute for a physical model, and not a validation verdict. R-squared tells you about variance explained; the residual structure tells you about model form adequacy, and you must look at both. Monte Carlo simulation, which we first applied to electrical power in Week 2, extends to multi-variable aerospace systems with correlated inputs and nonlinear models, where partial derivative propagation underestimates uncertainty due to its linearity and Gaussian output assumptions. When both methods agree, either may be reported; when they disagree, use the Monte Carlo result and explain why. These tools together — confidence interval comparison, regression, and Monte Carlo — complete the E2 statistical analysis toolkit. Section 4 of your Tier 2 group report requires all three, and the improvement proposal in Section 6 must be grounded in what the analysis revealed about measurement sensitivity. In the lab session today, you apply all of this to your own Tier 2 dataset. The tools are not abstract — they are the specific instruments for finishing your group report, and the quality of how you apply and interpret them is what distinguishes a good experimental engineer from one who can only follow a procedure.
