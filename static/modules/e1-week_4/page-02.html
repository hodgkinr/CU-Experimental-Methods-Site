Multiple Sensors, Multiple Sources — When Measurements Disagree

A short reference reading covering what to do when two sensors measuring the same quantity disagree — and, more broadly, how to think about multiple sources of uncertainty in the same measurement chain. Three cases are distinguished: (1) one sensor is biased (systematic difference — the gap is consistent across repeated trials), (2) both sensors have random noise but no systematic offset (the distributions overlap but their centers agree), (3) the two sensors are not measuring the same physical quantity at the same point in space or time (a spatial or temporal mismatch that is not a sensor error at all, but a design flaw). Each case has a different diagnostic signature and a different remedy. The reading also covers the taxonomy of error sources students encounter in the Tier 1 experiment: random noise (scatter across repeated trials), systematic bias (consistent offset from truth, bounded by the instrument spec), and structured error (a repeatable pattern that is neither random nor a constant bias — often a sign of model-form limitation or confounding factor). The reading frames how these three categories should be handled differently in a measurement report: random error is characterized statistically, systematic bias is bounded from the datasheet and propagated, and structured error is identified, named, and its physical cause investigated. Multiple Sensors, Multiple Sources — When Measurements Disagree

Three Diagnostic Cases and What Each One Tells You About Your Measurement

Two sensors measuring the same physical quantity should agree. When they don't, the disagreement isn't a problem to explain away; it is a diagnostic that tells you something about your measurement that neither sensor could reveal alone.

Using multiple sensors to measure the same quantity is standard practice in professional aerospace testing. A single sensor gives you a number; two sensors give you a check. If they agree within their specified uncertainties, you have corroboration: independent evidence that your measurement is not a sensor artifact. If they disagree, you have information. But extracting that information requires knowing which of three fundamentally different cases produced the gap.

Defaulting to "sensor error" as a catch-all explanation when sensors disagree is one of the most common errors in student lab reports, and one of the most professionally costly. Each case has a different cause, a different diagnostic signature, and a different remedy. Confusing them leads to wrong corrections and wrong conclusions.

Case 1: Systematic bias. One sensor consistently reads higher or lower than the other across all trials. The gap is approximately constant: it does not grow with the measured value, does not fluctuate randomly with time, and does not disappear when conditions change. This is a systematic error (also called a bias), and it has a specific physical source: a calibration offset, a manufacturing tolerance that shifts the sensor's output, or an installation condition that introduces a consistent reading error. A thermocouple not fully embedded in the material it is measuring, a pressure tap with a small leak, or a strain gauge bonded with an adhesive layer of inconsistent thickness can all produce a systematic offset. The diagnostic signature is a constant gap in a time-series overlay or a consistent offset in a scatter plot of one sensor's output against the other. The remedy is calibration: compare both sensors to a known reference and determine which, if either, is tracking truth.

Case 2: Statistical agreement within noise. Both sensors scatter around the same central value, but neither gives exactly the same reading on repeated measurements. The distributions overlap; the 95% confidence intervals from both sensors include a common range of values. This isn't disagreement at all; it is random variability, and the appropriate comparison is not "which sensor is right?" but "do the distributions agree within the propagated uncertainty?" If the confidence intervals overlap, the sensors are statistically consistent. The remedy is not to pick one sensor and discard the other; it is to report the comparison explicitly as evidence of consistency, and to consider averaging the two as a way to reduce the random component of the combined measurement. One important qualifier: averaging reduces the random component only when the two sensors' errors are uncorrelated. If both sensors share a common systematic bias (for example, both are mounted on a surface with the same thermal gradient), averaging will not reduce that component.

Case 3: Spatial or temporal mismatch. The two sensors appear to disagree, but they are not actually measuring the same physical quantity at the same point in space or the same moment in time. This is the most frequently misdiagnosed case. A temperature sensor in the center of a flow field and a temperature sensor at the wall will read differently because the fluid temperature genuinely varies across the cross-section; this is not sensor error, it is spatial structure in the physical quantity being measured. Similarly, two sensors sampling at different rates will produce time-series that appear mismatched because the measurement bandwidth differs. The diagnostic signature is that the disagreement has spatial or temporal structure: it is not random scatter and not a constant offset, but a pattern that maps to a physical gradient or a timing difference. The remedy is experimental redesign: co-locate the sensors or explicitly account for the spatial or temporal difference in your uncertainty analysis.

These three cases map onto a broader taxonomy of error sources that applies to any measurement chain.

Random error is the scatter you observe across repeated trials. It is a property of the measurement process, characterized statistically (standard deviation, confidence interval). It cannot be reduced by taking one more careful measurement, but it can be reduced by taking more measurements and averaging. Systematic bias is a consistent offset from truth that repeats across trials; it is bounded using the instrument's datasheet specification and propagated through partial derivative error analysis. Structured error is the most informative category: a repeatable pattern in the residuals that is neither random scatter nor a constant offset. Structured error appears as a trend: residuals that increase with the independent variable, or that switch sign at a specific condition. This is not measurement noise; it is evidence that the model underlying your measurement or data reduction is missing a physical effect.

In a lab report, these three categories are handled differently. Random error is characterized statistically and reported as a confidence interval. Systematic bias is bounded from the datasheet specification and propagated using partial derivatives. Structured error is identified, named, and its physical cause investigated; "the residuals show a systematic positive trend above 50 rpm, consistent with aerodynamic drag not included in the model" is the kind of statement that distinguishes an analytical engineer from one who simply fitted a trendline and moved on.

The three cases in this reading map directly to the discrepancy taxonomy covered later in Week 5: Case 1 (systematic bias) = Category 1 in the taxonomy; Case 2 (statistical agreement within noise) = Category 2; Case 3 (structured error in residuals) = Category 3. When you reach the Week 5 reading on discrepancy classification, you'll find the same framework applied to model-experiment comparisons; the diagnostic logic carries over exactly.

The Takeaway: When two sensors disagree, your first question should be which of the three diagnostic cases applies (systematic bias, statistical agreement, or spatial/temporal mismatch) because each has a different cause, a different remedy, and a different implication for what you can and cannot conclude from your measurement.
