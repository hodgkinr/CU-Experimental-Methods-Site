    slide 1: Title Slide Title: From Model to Experiment — Reading the Equations to Design the Test Talking Point: You have spent three weeks learning to execute the physical modeling branch of the AMVF — how to measure, how to propagate uncertainty, how to argue for agreement. This week I want to change the direction of the question. Instead of asking "given this measurement, what does it tell us?" I want to ask "given this model, what does it tell us about how to design the experiment?" Because the model is not just a thing you compare data to — it is a design document. If you can read a model's equations and extract the sensitivity structure, you will make better instrumentation choices, select more informative test conditions, and write more defensible reports. That is what this lecture is about.

    slide 2: The Model as a Design Document (Image: A simple schematic showing a mathematical model equation on the left — for example, a damped oscillation equation "x(t) = A·e^(-ζωₙt)·cos(ωd·t + φ)" — with labeled parameters: A (initial amplitude), ζ (damping ratio), ωₙ (natural frequency), ωd (damped frequency). On the right, a test article diagram showing a spring-mass-damper on a shaker platform with accelerometers attached. Bidirectional arrows labeled with three questions: "What must I measure?" pointing from equation to hardware; "How precisely?" pointing from equation to hardware; "Under what conditions?" pointing from equation to hardware. A gold banner across the bottom: "Every term in the model is an experimental design requirement — if you know how to read it.") Talking Point: The connection I want you to see is this: every term in a physical model is a measurement requirement in disguise. Look at the damped oscillation equation. It has four parameters — amplitude, damping ratio, natural frequency, and phase. Each of those parameters needs to be either measured, inferred, or held constant in the experiment. The natural frequency can be estimated from the mass and stiffness — but both mass and stiffness must be measured, and if either has uncertainty, that uncertainty appears in the natural frequency estimate. The damping ratio requires the decay envelope to be resolved — which means your time resolution must be fine enough to track the amplitude decrease over several cycles. The model tells you all of this before you touch hardware — if you know how to read it. The model is the first instrumentation document.

    slide 3: The P=IV Lesson Generalized (Image: A two-panel figure. Left panel shows the P=IV power equation example from Week 2 with the sensitivity breakdown: "P = I²R is more sensitive to current error than to resistance error because current appears squared." Annotation: "You learned this pattern in Week 2." Right panel generalizes: a generic derived quantity equation Q = f(x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ) with a table showing three columns: "Input," "Partial Derivative ∂Q/∂xᵢ," and "Sensitivity Magnitude |∂Q/∂xᵢ · δxᵢ|." The table has three filled rows with placeholder values showing one input dominating the sensitivity. A gold arrow connects the two panels with the label "Same logic — any model, any derived quantity." Caption: "The power equation was the training problem. Now you apply the same logic to your actual experiment.") Talking Point: You learned this pattern in Week 2 with the power equations. P equals I-squared times R is more sensitive to current error than to resistance error, because current appears squared and the partial derivative amplifies its contribution. That was not a special property of the power equation — it is a property of any derived quantity that depends on measured inputs. For any model Q equals f of x₁, x₂, and so on, the partial derivatives are the sensitivity coefficients, and the product of a large sensitivity coefficient and a large input uncertainty is the signature of a dominant error source. The power equation was the training example. Your Tier 1 canned experiment is the real problem. Right now, you should be taking the governing equations from your experiment — whatever physical model connects your inputs to your derived output — and computing the partial derivatives to see which measured quantity dominates the uncertainty budget. That calculation should have been in your Phase 1 report. If it was not, it will be.

    slide 4: Worked Example — An Unbalanced Wheel (Tier 1 Pool) (Image: A diagram of a rotating disk with a small eccentric mass attached at radius r from the center. The disk rotates at angular velocity ω, and the centripetal force on the eccentric mass is labeled F_c = m·r·ω². Below the diagram, a partial derivative sensitivity analysis table. Column 1: "Parameter." Column 2: "Partial Derivative of F_c." Column 3: "Value (for m = 0.05 kg, r = 0.08 m, ω = 120 rad/s, typical sensor spec uncertainties)." Rows: m — ∂F_c/∂m = r·ω²; r — ∂F_c/∂r = m·ω²; ω — ∂F_c/∂ω = 2m·r·ω. The sensitivity table shows that angular velocity dominates — the factor of 2m·r·ω, evaluated at 120 rad/s, is much larger than the others. A gold box: "Dominant uncertainty source: angular velocity measurement." This example is from the Tier 1 pool and is distinct from any experiment a student in the room is running.) Talking Point: Let me use an experiment from the Tier 1 pool — one that is not your assigned experiment — to walk through this analysis concretely. Consider a rotating disk with a small eccentric mass at radius r. The centripetal force is F_c equals m times r times omega squared. Taking partial derivatives: the sensitivity to mass is r times omega squared, to radius is m times omega squared, and to angular velocity is 2 times m times r times omega. At typical lab conditions — 120 radians per second, for example — the angular velocity term is much larger than the others. And there is an additional amplification: angular velocity appears squared in the model, so a 2% uncertainty in omega contributes 4% to the uncertainty in force. This immediately tells you three things: first, angular velocity measurement is the highest-priority sensor selection. Second, if your tachometer has poor precision, improve it before improving the mass or ruler. Third, the most informative test conditions are those where omega variation is detectable and measurable, not just those where force is conveniently large.

    slide 5: Four Questions the Model Answers (Image: A clean four-question framework formatted as a 2x2 grid of boxes. Box 1 (top-left): "Which input dominates the uncertainty budget?" — icon of a ranked bar chart. Box 2 (top-right): "What sensor spec do I need?" — icon of a sensor with a tolerance specification label. Box 3 (bottom-left): "What test conditions are most informative?" — icon of a plot with a region highlighted. Box 4 (bottom-right): "What anomalous behavior would tell me the model is wrong?" — icon of a plot with a systematic deviation from the expected curve. A gold border surrounds all four boxes with the label: "Read these from the partial derivative structure — before you touch hardware.") Talking Point: Every physical model, when you apply partial derivative sensitivity analysis, answers four questions that directly inform your experimental design. First: which input dominates the uncertainty budget? The answer tells you which sensor needs the tightest spec. Second: what sensor precision do I need? The answer comes from working backward from the acceptable output uncertainty — what maximum input uncertainty is tolerable given the sensitivity coefficients? Third: what test conditions are most informative? Models are often most sensitive to the parameter of interest — the one you are trying to estimate — at specific values of the independent variable. Testing there maximizes the information you extract. Fourth: what anomalous behavior would tell me the model is wrong? If the model assumes linearity, the anomalous signature is curvature or hysteresis. If the model ignores a coupling term, the anomalous signature is a dependence on a variable you held constant. Commit to all four answers before you run the experiment.

    slide 6: Working Backward — What Precision Do I Need? (Image: A worked example diagram. Top: "Acceptable output uncertainty goal: δF_c < 5% at 95% CI." Below: the F_c = m·r·ω² equation with partial derivative terms shown. A table computes: "Given δm from a lab scale (±0.1 g), δr from a caliper (±0.5 mm), what δω is acceptable?" Using the quadrature sum requirement and the dominant sensitivity coefficient for ω, the calculation shows that δω must be less than approximately 0.8 rad/s to meet the 5% goal. Below the table: "This means your tachometer or speed controller must have precision better than 0.8 rad/s at 120 rad/s — about 0.67% of full scale." A gold annotation: "Working backward from an output requirement to an input spec is called an accuracy requirement — it is the sensor selection criterion.") Talking Point: Here is the reverse problem, and it is the one that shows up constantly in actual test design. Suppose I have a requirement: the uncertainty in my centripetal force estimate must be less than 5% at 95% confidence. I can compute, from the partial derivative structure, what that requirement implies for each of my input sensors. If my mass scale and calipers meet their specs, then the entire remaining uncertainty budget flows through the angular velocity term. Inverting the propagation equation tells me the maximum allowable angular velocity uncertainty — say, 0.8 radians per second at 120 radians per second — which translates to a tachometer precision requirement of less than 0.67% of full scale. Now I can go to an instrument catalog with a specific requirement, not just a vague desire for a "good" tachometer. This is what sensor selection looks like when it is grounded in the model rather than in habit or convenience.

    slide 7: What Test Conditions Are Most Informative? (Image: A sensitivity versus independent variable plot. X-axis: "Angular Velocity ω (rad/s)" ranging from 0 to 200. Y-axis: "Sensitivity of F_c to mass uncertainty ∂F_c/∂m = r·ω²." The curve rises steeply with ω, labeled "Sensitivity increases as ω²." Three vertical dashed lines mark: "Low ω region — small sensitivity, hard to distinguish F_c from noise." "Mid ω region — moderate sensitivity, informative but not extreme." "High ω region — large sensitivity, best signal-to-noise for model comparison, but hardware constraints apply." A shaded green band marks the "most informative region" around mid-to-high ω. A note: "The model tells you where the comparison is most discriminating — test there.") Talking Point: The model also tells you where to run the experiment for maximum information. In the centripetal force example, the force depends on omega squared — which means the sensitivity of the comparison between model and data is much higher at large omega. At low rotation rates, the forces are small and hard to measure above the noise floor. At high rotation rates, the forces are large and the comparison between model and data is highly discriminating — small disagreements are detectable. Of course, hardware constraints — bearing loads, motor speed limits, safety margins — set the upper bound. But within those constraints, the model tells you that testing at moderate-to-high omega gives you the most informative comparison. This is not just intuition — it comes directly from the partial derivative structure. Testing at conditions where the model is most sensitive to the quantity of interest is how you design a meaningful experiment rather than one that could agree or disagree for no informative reason.

    slide 8: What Anomalous Behavior Would Tell Me the Model Is Wrong? (Image: Three side-by-side plots, each showing the centripetal force model prediction (dashed line, F_c = m·r·ω²) against possible data patterns. Plot A: "Expected — scatter around the model curve within the uncertainty band." Dots scatter around the curve within gray bounds. Label: "Consistent with measurement noise — model is adequate." Plot B: "Anomalous — nonlinear deviation at high ω." Data follows the model at low ω but falls below it at high ω. Label: "Possible aerodynamic drag on the eccentric mass — not in the model. Model is missing a velocity-dependent force." Plot C: "Anomalous — constant offset across all ω." Data runs parallel to the model but consistently below. Label: "Possible systematic bias in mass or radius measurement — calibration issue, not model-form error." Caption: "Anomalous signatures are predictable from model assumptions — commit to them before the experiment.") Talking Point: From the model, you can predict not just what the expected data looks like, but what specific anomalous patterns would tell you the model is wrong. For the centripetal force model, if the data shows scatter within the uncertainty band, the model is adequate for this experiment. But if the data shows a systematic deviation that grows with omega — falling below the model at high rotation rates — that is a signature of an additional velocity-dependent force, like aerodynamic drag on the eccentric mass, that the simple F equals m-r-omega-squared model does not include. And if the data shows a constant offset across all omega values, that is not a model-form error — it is a systematic measurement bias in mass or radius, which is a calibration issue, not a physics issue. These three patterns are distinguishable, and you can predict which ones are physically plausible before running a single trial. That predictive commitment is what I want to see in the Phase 1 report's model comparison section.

The left column is how instrumentation decisions get made when they are not connected to the model — you use what's available, measure what's accessible, and run as many trials as time allows. These are not bad choices necessarily, but they have no principled basis. You do not know if they are sufficient. The right column is what model-driven instrumentation design looks like — specific sensor choices with specific precision requirements derived from specific sensitivity coefficients, test conditions selected because the model is most discriminating there, trial count determined by the output uncertainty requirement. The model is the first instrumentation document. Read it before you write a sensor list. Read it before you design the test matrix. Read it before you finalize your lab setup. If you do, your instrumentation choices become defensible, your comparison becomes meaningful, and your report becomes an engineering argument rather than a data summary.

    slide 11: Closure The physical modeling branch of the AMVF is not a passive receiver of whatever data a measurement produces — it is an active design space that the mathematical model constrains from the beginning. The partial derivative sensitivity analysis you have practiced since Week 2 is not just a bookkeeping tool for uncertainty propagation — it is a design tool that tells you which sensors matter most, what precision they need, where to run the experiment for maximum information, and what anomalous patterns would indicate model-form failure. The Phase 1 report asks you to demonstrate this analysis explicitly. By the time you complete it, you will have walked the full Phase I pipeline — from hypothesis through sensor selection through calibration through comparison — as a single traceable workflow grounded in the model you were given. That traceable workflow is what test engineering looks like. That is what this week asks you to demonstrate.
