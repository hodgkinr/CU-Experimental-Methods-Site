Analog Signal Conditioning — Filtering, Noise & Gain

Covers the signal conditioning chain between a physical sensor and a DAQ digitizer: amplification (gain and offset), filtering (low-pass anti-aliasing filter — purpose, order, cutoff selection), and noise sources (thermal noise, electromagnetic interference, ground loops). The design rationale for anti-aliasing filter cutoff selection is emphasized: the cutoff must be below the Nyquist frequency of the chosen sampling rate, but not so aggressive that it attenuates signal content of interest. Students see that signal conditioning choices are not technical defaults — they encode assumptions about signal structure and acceptable data quality. **Analog vs. digital filtering contrast (5–7 min segment):** This lecture should include an explicit segment contrasting analog and digital filtering roles. The key message: anti-aliasing must happen in the analog domain before the ADC — digital filters applied post-digitization cannot retroactively correct aliasing because the information is already lost. Additionally, digital filters introduce phase delay, passband ripple, and potential edge-case artifacts (e.g., ringing near sharp transitions) that are non-obvious. Students do not need to know how to design analog or digital filters — this is awareness-level content. The takeaway: signal conditioning choices must be made before sampling, not after, and the choice of whether to filter in the analog or digital domain is a consequential design decision that must be documented in any credible experimental report.

    slide 1: Title Slide Title: Analog Signal Conditioning — Filtering, Noise & Gain Talking Point: In the last lecture, we established that choosing a sample rate is a modeling commitment — you are claiming that the physical signal has no meaningful content above the Nyquist frequency. But the sample rate is only part of the story. Between the physical sensor and the DAQ digitizer, the signal passes through a conditioning chain: it gets amplified, filtered, and modified in ways that can improve the measurement — or corrupt it — depending on the choices made. This lecture covers that chain: what each stage does, why it is there, and the most important design decisions you need to make and document before any data is collected.

    slide 2: The Signal Conditioning Chain — An Overview (Image: A horizontal block diagram of the complete signal conditioning chain from physical phenomenon to digital data record. Five blocks connected by arrows, left to right: "Physical Sensor / Transducer" → "Signal Amplifier (Gain + Offset)" → "Anti-Aliasing Filter (Low-pass)" → "Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC)" → "Digital Data Record." Each block has a small icon below it: the sensor has a waveform, the amplifier has a gain triangle symbol, the filter has a frequency roll-off curve, the ADC has a staircase digitization symbol, and the data record has a MATLAB plot icon. A bracket below the first three blocks is labeled "Analog domain — these stages happen before digitization." A bracket below the last two blocks is labeled "Digital domain — once here, analog information is locked in." The color of the analog domain bracket is gold; the digital domain bracket is a lighter gray.) Talking Point: Let me give you the full picture of what happens between a physical event and a number in a MATLAB variable. The sensor transduces the physical quantity into an electrical signal — typically a voltage or current proportional to what is being measured. That signal then passes through an amplifier, which increases the signal level to a range that the ADC can resolve effectively. It then passes through the anti-aliasing filter, which removes frequency content above the Nyquist frequency. Then the ADC samples the conditioned analog signal at the chosen rate and converts each sample to a digital number. Every design decision in the analog domain — how much gain, what filter cutoff, what filter order — affects the quality of the digital record. And critically: none of these effects can be undone after digitization. If you amplified the wrong band, you cannot de-amplify it. If you filtered with the wrong cutoff, you cannot unfilter. The measurement is fixed the moment it crosses into the digital domain.

    slide 3: Gain and Offset — Why Amplification Is a Design Decision (Image: A two-panel illustration. Left panel: "Under-amplified signal" — a small-amplitude sensor output waveform (about 10% of the ADC input range) plotted on a voltage axis labeled 0 to 10 V. The waveform only occupies 0 to 1 V of the range. A label reads: "Signal uses only 10% of ADC range — poor resolution, high quantization error contribution." Right panel: "Properly amplified signal" — the same waveform amplified to occupy 60–80% of the ADC input range. A label reads: "Signal occupies 70% of range — resolution optimized." Below both panels, a caption: "Gain selection directly controls how much of the ADC's resolution is used to represent the signal. An under-amplified signal wastes bits." A small inset shows the ADC quantization staircase to reinforce the concept.) Talking Point: The purpose of the amplifier stage is to match the sensor's output range to the ADC's input range — to use as much of the ADC's dynamic range as possible without clipping. An ADC converts an analog voltage to a digital integer within its input range. If your sensor output spans 0.1 volts but your ADC input range is 0 to 10 volts, you are using only one percent of the available digital counts to represent the entire signal. Every bit that you are not using is measurement precision that you are throwing away. Proper gain selection puts the signal in the operating region where the ADC provides meaningful resolution. This is directly related to quantization error — the uncertainty introduced by the discrete nature of the ADC output. Quantization error is typically estimated as plus-or-minus half of one least-significant-bit, and its contribution to the total measurement uncertainty is smaller when the signal occupies more of the ADC range.

    slide 4: Noise Sources in the Signal Chain — What You Are Fighting (Image: A layered diagram of the signal conditioning chain with noise sources identified at each stage. The sensor block has an arrow labeled "Sensor noise: thermal noise (Johnson noise), sensitivity drift." The signal cable between sensor and amplifier has a lightning bolt icon labeled "Electromagnetic interference (EMI): coupling from nearby power lines, motors, RF sources." The amplifier block has an arrow labeled "Amplifier noise: thermal noise, bias current." A ground symbol between components is labeled "Ground loops: current flowing through shared ground path produces measurable voltage offset." Each noise source has an estimated contribution in parentheses: "sensor thermal noise: typically μV level," "EMI: can be mV level," "amplifier noise: typically nV to μV," "ground loops: can be mV to hundreds of mV." The visual is a chain diagram with noise injected at each stage — cumulative noise accumulates through the chain.) Talking Point: Noise enters the signal chain at every stage, and understanding where it comes from is the first step toward controlling it. The sensor itself introduces thermal noise — a fundamental quantum-mechanical effect that is proportional to temperature and resistance. The cable between the sensor and the amplifier can pick up electromagnetic interference from nearby power lines, motors, or radio-frequency sources. The amplifier adds its own noise, and if the circuit does not have a clean, single-point ground reference, current flowing through the shared ground path can add a DC offset or a 60-hertz hum to the signal. Ground loops are one of the most common causes of unexplained noise in experimental measurements, and they are almost entirely preventable with careful grounding practice. A ground loop typically shows up as a 60 Hz peak in the FFT — a regular spike at the power line frequency that doesn't move regardless of how the physical system is loaded or excited. The takeaway is that noise is not a fixed property of your measurement system — it depends on how the system is assembled and set up, and improving signal quality sometimes requires changing the setup, not the hardware.

    slide 5: The Anti-Aliasing Filter — Design Rationale and Cutoff Selection (Image: A frequency-domain plot showing the amplitude response of a low-pass filter on the y-axis (in dB, from 0 to −60 dB) and frequency on the x-axis (from 0 to twice the sampling frequency). The filter has a flat passband extending from 0 to the cutoff frequency fc, a transition band from fc to the Nyquist frequency fs/2, and a stopband attenuating above fs/2. Three vertical dashed lines mark: fc (filter cutoff), fmax (highest frequency of signal interest), and fs/2 (Nyquist frequency). The relationships between these three frequencies are labeled: "fc must be above fmax — do not attenuate signal content," "fc must be below fs/2 — all content above Nyquist must be attenuated before ADC," "Transition band determines filter order — steeper rolloff requires higher-order filter." A note reads: "There is always a tradeoff: narrower transition band = higher filter order = more phase distortion.") Talking Point: The anti-aliasing filter's job is precise: it must attenuate all frequency content above the Nyquist frequency sufficiently that no alias can contaminate the measurement, while passing all signal content of interest without attenuation. The cutoff frequency sits in the transition zone between "signal content I want" and "content that would alias." This means the cutoff must be above the highest frequency of interest — otherwise you are filtering out signal — and it must be below the Nyquist frequency — otherwise you are not completing the anti-aliasing job. The width of the transition band is controlled by the filter order: a higher-order filter has a steeper rolloff, which gives you more separation between the passband and the stopband for a given sampling rate. But there is a tradeoff: higher-order filters introduce more phase distortion in the passband, which can affect the timing relationships in the signal. This is not a deep filter design course — but you need to be able to read a filter specification and understand whether the cutoff is in the right place relative to your signal bandwidth and sample rate.

    slide 6: Analog vs. Digital Filtering — The Critical Distinction (Image: A two-column comparison table. Column header left: "Analog filtering (pre-ADC)." Column header right: "Digital filtering (post-ADC)." Row 1: "When it happens" | "Before digitization — in the signal conditioning chain" | "After digitization — in MATLAB or DSP hardware." Row 2 [BOLD/HIGHLIGHTED]: "Can prevent aliasing?" | "YES — that is its primary purpose" | "NO — aliased content is already mixed into the digital data; it cannot be separated." Row 3: "Introduces phase delay?" | "Yes, order-dependent" | "Yes, filter-type-dependent; can be zero-phase with filtfilt in MATLAB." Row 4: "Can introduce artifacts?" | "Rarely, if properly designed" | "Yes — ringing near sharp transitions, edge effects, passband ripple." Row 5: "Primary use" | "Anti-aliasing before ADC; noise rejection" | "Post-processing: smoothing, band selection, noise removal from already-captured signal." A bold annotation at the bottom reads: "Anti-aliasing must happen in the analog domain. This is not a preference — it is a physical requirement." The 'Can prevent aliasing?' row is visually emphasized — bold text or highlighted background — to distinguish it from the other rows.) Talking Point: You do not need to design filters — you need to know which domain handles which problem. The single most important row in this table is the one about aliasing prevention. I want to be direct about the most important practical implication of this table. The anti-aliasing filter must operate in the analog domain, before the analog-to-digital converter, because once a signal has been aliased into the digital record, no digital filter can separate it from the legitimate signal content at the same frequency. Digital filters — including MATLAB's filtfilt and butter functions — are powerful post-processing tools, and they have real uses: smoothing noisy data, extracting a specific frequency band from a captured record, or removing a known noise frequency. But they are applied to data that has already been digitized, which means they cannot retroactively correct aliasing. Additionally, digital filters introduce their own artifacts: zero-phase filters like filtfilt perform the filtering twice in opposite directions, which removes phase delay but doubles the transient effects at the edges of the data record. Finite impulse response filters can introduce ringing near sharp transitions. You do not need to design filters from scratch in this course. You do need to know that the choice of whether to filter in the analog or digital domain is a consequential design decision, and it must be documented in any credible experimental report.

Not because documentation is bureaucratic, but because these choices affect whether your data is trustworthy, and a reader who wants to evaluate your validation claim needs to know that you made these choices deliberately and can explain them. The DAQ configuration section is not a settings list — it is an argument. You are arguing that the choices you made are appropriate for the signal you are trying to capture, given the bandwidth of the physical phenomenon, the Nyquist criterion, the range of the sensor output, and the noise environment of the measurement. If you cannot justify each choice, you cannot defend your data, and you cannot make a credible validation claim. This documentation habit is exactly what distinguishes a well-run experimental campaign from a black-box measurement.

    slide 8: Worked Example — Evaluating a Sensor's Signal Conditioning Setup (Image: A one-page instrument specification excerpt mockup showing four key parameters: "Sensor output range: 0 to 50 mV (full scale)," "Noise floor: 5 μV RMS," "Recommended gain: 100 (amplified output: 0 to 5 V)," "Anti-aliasing filter: 2nd-order Butterworth, −3 dB at 1 kHz (−3 dB means the filter has reduced the signal amplitude to approximately 71% of its original value at this frequency)." Below the spec excerpt, a MATLAB figure shows the frequency-domain FFT of the amplified sensor output, with a spectral peak at 240 Hz labeled "Primary signal component" and a flat noise floor. A vertical dashed line at 1000 Hz is labeled "Filter cutoff," and a vertical dashed line at 500 Hz (half the cutoff, assuming a 1000 Hz sample rate) is labeled "Nyquist frequency." An annotation reads: "Signal bandwidth (240 Hz) << Nyquist (500 Hz) — adequate margin. But: filter cutoff (1000 Hz) is above Nyquist (500 Hz) — the filter is NOT providing anti-aliasing protection at this sample rate." The problem is highlighted in red.) Talking Point: Let me walk through a real evaluation using actual instrument specifications. The sensor has a full-scale output range of 0 to 50 millivolts, a noise floor of 5 microvolts RMS, a recommended gain of 100 — which brings the output to 0 to 5 volts, matching a standard ADC input range — and a built-in second-order Butterworth anti-aliasing filter with a negative-3-dB cutoff at 1 kilohertz. If you are sampling at 1000 hertz, the Nyquist frequency is 500 hertz. The filter cutoff is at 1 kilohertz — which is above the Nyquist frequency. That means the anti-aliasing filter is not actually providing aliasing protection at this sample rate. Any content between 500 and 1000 hertz would alias into the measurement, and the filter is not attenuating it. The fix is straightforward: either increase the sample rate to at least 2 kilohertz so the Nyquist frequency is above the filter cutoff, or add a supplementary analog filter with a lower cutoff. This is exactly the kind of configuration evaluation you will do in the Week 7 lab — reading the instrument documentation, checking whether the configuration is self-consistent, and identifying where it is not.

    slide 10: The Signal Conditioning Choices Are Modeling Decisions (Image: A diagram showing the concept of signal conditioning as a modeling commitment. The center of the diagram has a bold statement: "Every signal conditioning choice encodes an assumption about the signal." Three examples branch from the center: "Sample rate choice" → "Assumes: no meaningful signal energy above fs/2." "Filter cutoff choice" → "Assumes: no signal content between fc and fs/2 that matters for the intended use." "Gain choice" → "Assumes: maximum signal amplitude will not exceed ADC input range." "Violated gain assumption → clipping — signal is permanently distorted." The visual style uses a spider diagram or concept map layout, with the central statement in a large gold circle and the branches radiating outward.) Talking Point: I want to close this lecture with the unifying frame. Every signal conditioning choice you make is a modeling decision — it encodes an assumption about the signal you are trying to capture. Choosing a sample rate of 2 kilohertz means you are claiming the signal has no meaningful energy above 1 kilohertz. Choosing an anti-aliasing filter cutoff of 800 hertz means you are claiming that any signal content between 800 hertz and 1 kilohertz can be discarded without affecting your measurement quality for the intended use. Choosing a gain of 100 means you are claiming the maximum signal amplitude will not exceed 5 volts after amplification. Each of these assumptions can be wrong, and when they are, the consequences range from reduced precision to completely corrupted data — and the corrupted data will not always be obviously wrong. The only protection is making these decisions based on analysis of the expected signal, documenting the reasoning, and being prepared to defend each choice when your measurement is scrutinized. That is what makes a measurement credible, and that is what the DAQ configuration section of your report is for.

    slide 11: Closure Signal conditioning is not a black box that happens before your MATLAB workspace — it is a sequence of design decisions that each encode an assumption about the physical signal you are trying to capture. Amplification matches sensor output to ADC dynamic range and controls quantization error contribution. The anti-aliasing filter must be placed in the analog domain before the ADC, with a cutoff below the Nyquist frequency, because aliased content cannot be removed after digitization. Noise enters the chain at every stage — from the sensor, from the cable, from the amplifier, from the ground configuration — and identifying and documenting known noise sources is a required part of your experimental report. Analog and digital filtering serve different purposes and must not be confused: digital filters cannot retroactively correct aliasing. Every one of these choices belongs in the DAQ configuration justification section of your Tier 2 group report, argued from the signal bandwidth and the intended use of the measurement. The Week 7 lab will give you the data and the documentation to perform that analysis for your own experiment.
