Variability, Error, and Uncertainty

Establishes the three-way distinction that underpins all uncertainty work in the course: variability is a property of the physical world, measurement error is a property of the instrument, and uncertainty is a quantified epistemic claim about ignorance. Covers random vs. systematic error, statistical properties (mean, standard deviation, distributions, confidence intervals), and repeatability vs. reproducibility. This distinction becomes a required element of every subsequent deliverable.

    slide 1: Title Slide Title: Variability, Error, and Uncertainty Talking Point: I want to open this lecture with a quote from Carl Richards — a financial advisor, not an engineer, but someone who understood something deeply true about working with imperfect information: "Error is what's left over after you think you've thought of everything." That sentence captures the epistemic situation of every experimentalist. You designed a careful procedure. You installed your sensors correctly. You checked your calibration. And your data still varies. What is that variation? Is it your instrument? Is it the physical system? Is it noise? Is it a problem — or is it information? This lecture gives you the vocabulary to answer those questions precisely, and the answers determine every claim you will ever make from experimental data.

    slide 2: Three Things That Are Not the Same (Image: A clean three-circle diagram arranged in a downward triangle, with no overlaps — each circle is clearly separate. Top-left circle labeled "Variability" — gold color — with a sub-note below: "A property of the physical world. Real, not an artifact." Top-right circle labeled "Measurement Error" — blue color — with sub-note: "A property of the instrument and process. Introduced by measurement, not by the phenomenon." Bottom circle labeled "Uncertainty" — gray with gold border — with sub-note: "A quantified epistemic claim. Describes the state of your knowledge, not the state of the world." Two small arrows from the top circles point down toward the bottom circle with the label "Both contribute to." A caption below the diagram: "These are distinct. Using them interchangeably produces wrong conclusions.") Talking Point: I want to establish the three-way distinction that underpins every uncertainty discussion in this course. Variability is a property of the physical world — the system itself behaves slightly differently from trial to trial, and that is real, not an artifact of your instrument. Measurement error is a property of your instrument and process — noise in the signal chain, inconsistent operator technique, thermal drift in electronics. Error is introduced by the act of measuring. Uncertainty is different from both: it is a quantified statement of how much you do not know about the true value of the quantity you are estimating. Uncertainty encompasses both variability and error, but it is an epistemic claim — it describes the state of your knowledge, not the state of the world. These three words are not interchangeable in professional engineering communication, and I will hold you to the distinction in every deliverable this semester.

    slide 3: Variability — Real Physics, Not Instrument Noise (Image: A time-series scatter plot showing repeated measurements of the same physical quantity over 30 consecutive trials, plotted as individual dots connected by a line. Values fluctuate around a mean of approximately 1.42 with a visible spread of ±0.04 units. A horizontal dashed line marks the mean. An annotation box on the right: "This scatter is not instrument noise — it is real physical variation in the system." A secondary panel below shows two overlapping Gaussian distributions side by side: one narrow one labeled "Instrument Noise Floor" and one wider one labeled "Physical Variability." The wider distribution clearly dominates. Caption: "In this case, eliminating instrument noise would not eliminate scatter.") Talking Point: Variability is the actual physical fluctuation in the thing you are measuring — and it is real even with a perfect instrument. If you measure the period of a pendulum 30 times with a precision atomic clock, you will still get slightly different values, because the pendulum itself behaves slightly differently on each swing — small differences in release amplitude, air resistance variation, thermal expansion of the bob suspension. This variability is not a problem to be eliminated; it is information about the physical system. It tells you something real about how that system behaves. The mistake I see most often in lab reports is attributing all scatter in the data to instrument noise. Sometimes the scatter is telling you about the physics, and recognizing that is actually more valuable than achieving a tight instrument spec. Know the difference before you draw conclusions.

    slide 4: Measurement Error — Systematic and Random (Image: Two side-by-side panels. Left panel: "Systematic (Bias) Error" — a histogram of measurement values shifted entirely to the right of a vertical dashed line labeled "True Value." The histogram is narrow and tight — high precision, wrong center. Annotation: "Consistent offset in one direction. Does not average away. Fixed by calibration." Right panel: "Random Error" — a histogram of measurement values symmetric around the true value dashed line, with a broad bell shape. Annotation: "Zero-mean fluctuation. Averages toward zero. Reduced by taking more measurements." Below both panels: a two-by-two summary table with rows "Reduce by averaging?" and "Reduce by calibration?" — Systematic: No / Yes. Random: Yes / No. The table is clean and easy to read.) Talking Point: Measurement error comes in two flavors that require completely different responses. Systematic error — bias — is a consistent offset in one direction. If your thermocouple always reads two degrees high because it was calibrated in a different reference environment, every measurement is shifted the same way. No amount of averaging will fix this. The mean of a thousand biased measurements is a biased mean. To remove systematic error, you need to find its source — a calibration error, an installation effect, a thermal drift — and fix it at that source. Random error, by contrast, is zero-mean fluctuation: noise that is as likely to be high as low on any given reading. Random error does average away. Take four times as many measurements and you halve the uncertainty in your mean. Recognizing which type of error dominates your measurement is the first diagnostic question you should ask when your data looks unexpected.

    slide 5: Uncertainty — The Quantified Claim (Image: A single horizontal bar showing a best estimate with symmetric uncertainty bounds. The bar is labeled "Best Estimate: 14.7 N" with a two-headed vertical arrow spanning ±0.4 N, labeled "Uncertainty Interval." To the right: a formal statement box reading "Result: 14.7 ± 0.4 N (95% CI, partial derivative method)." Below the bar: a contrast pair. Left: a bare number "14.7 N" with a red label "Not an engineering claim — cannot be used for comparison." Right: the same number with the uncertainty statement, with a green label "Engineering claim — can be compared to a model prediction." A definition box at the bottom: "Uncertainty: a quantified bound on how large the error could plausibly be, expressed as a probability interval.") Talking Point: Uncertainty is the number that converts a raw measurement into an engineering claim. Here is the key insight: you almost never know the actual error in your measurement. If you knew the error exactly, you would have corrected for it. What you can do is bound the plausible magnitude of the error, and express that bound as a probability interval. When I say the force is 14.7 plus or minus 0.4 Newtons at 95% confidence, I am saying that based on my knowledge of my instrument, my calibration procedure, and my measurement process, I am 95% confident the true value lies within 0.4 N of 14.7 N. That is an engineering claim. The bare number 14.7 N, without the interval, is just an observation — it cannot be meaningfully compared to a model prediction, because I cannot determine whether agreement is real or coincidental. Every result in this course must carry its uncertainty, stated explicitly with a confidence level and a method. ASME PTC 19.1 and professional measurement standards require this — it is not a course-specific convention but the professional standard for experimental uncertainty reporting, separate from and complementary to the modeling framework of ASME V&V 10-2006.

    slide 6: Statistical Properties — Mean, Standard Deviation, and Distributions (Image: A clean Gaussian bell curve on a white background. X-axis labeled "Measured Value." Y-axis labeled "Probability Density." Three vertical lines: solid at the mean μ, dashed at ±1σ from the mean, and dotted at ±2σ. Shaded regions: dark gold shading covering 68% of the area within ±1σ, lighter gold shading extending to cover 95% within ±2σ. Annotations: "μ = sample mean — best estimate of the true value." "σ = standard deviation — typical spread of individual measurements." A second text box: "σ/√N = standard error of the mean — uncertainty in the mean itself, decreases with more measurements." A visual comparison of σ and σ/√N shown as two differently-sized two-headed arrows below the curve.) Talking Point: When you take repeated measurements under the same conditions, the results form a distribution. For random errors, this distribution tends toward Gaussian — the classic bell curve — as you collect more data. The mean of your measurements is your best estimate of the true value. The standard deviation tells you how spread out individual measurements are — how much any single reading is likely to differ from the mean. But here is the distinction that trips up students: the standard deviation tells you about the scatter of individual readings, while the standard error of the mean — the standard deviation divided by the square root of N — tells you about the precision of your estimate of the mean itself. More measurements reduce the standard error of the mean. They do not reduce the physical scatter of individual measurements. When you report uncertainty on a derived quantity in this course, you need to be precise about which statistic you are using and why. One practical note: for small sample sizes — fewer than about 30 measurements — the t-distribution more accurately reflects the uncertainty in estimating the mean from limited data than the Gaussian approximation; MATLAB's tinv function gives you the correct coverage factor for any sample size.

    slide 7: Confidence Intervals — What They Actually Mean (Image: A vertical stack of five horizontal confidence intervals, one per row, labeled by sample size: N = 3, N = 10, N = 30, N = 100, N = 1000. Each interval is a horizontal two-headed arrow centered on the sample mean. Interval width decreases from top to bottom, illustrating convergence. A vertical dashed line marks the true value. For the N=3 case, the interval is wide and the true value falls at the edge — labeled "valid, but wide." For N=1000, the interval is very narrow and centered on the true value. A caption below: "A 95% CI means: if we repeated this experiment many times with this same N, 95% of the computed intervals would contain the true value." A cautionary annotation: "This interval either does or does not contain the true value — once computed, there is no probability.") Talking Point: A confidence interval is one of the most consistently misunderstood constructs in data analysis, and I want to get it right on Week 2. When I compute a 95% confidence interval, I am not saying there is a 95% probability that the true value lies within this interval. Once the interval is computed, the true value either is or is not inside it — there is no probability remaining. What 95% refers to is the procedure: if I repeated this entire experiment many times — same protocol, same number of measurements — 95% of the confidence intervals I computed would contain the true value. The probability refers to the long-run behavior of the procedure, not to this specific interval. This distinction matters when you use confidence intervals to argue for or against agreement between a measurement and a model prediction, which is exactly what you will do in the Week 3 comparative analysis memo. If you have encountered the Bayesian alternative — where a credible interval really does express a probability that the true value lies within a range — that is a different framework; both are valid, but this course uses the frequentist confidence interval because it is the standard in engineering reporting under ASME and ISO measurement standards.

    slide 8: Repeatability vs. Reproducibility (Image: A two-column comparison table with four rows. Column headers: "Repeatability" (blue) and "Reproducibility" (gold). Row 1 "Conditions": "Same operator, same instrument, same lab, short time interval" vs. "Different operators, different instruments, different labs, longer intervals." Row 2 "What it tests": "Instrument precision and procedural consistency" vs. "Whether the result generalizes beyond this specific setup." Row 3 "Typical metric": "Standard deviation of repeated runs within one session" vs. "Standard deviation across operators, labs, or sessions." Row 4 "Professional standard": "ASME — within-laboratory repeatability" vs. "ISO/ASME — inter-laboratory reproducibility." A note at the bottom: "Always report which you computed. They are not the same claim.") Talking Point: Repeatability and reproducibility are both consistency measures, but they answer different questions. Repeatability is about whether you — same person, same instrument, same session — get the same answer when you repeat a measurement. It tests your instrument's precision and your own procedural discipline. Reproducibility asks whether different people, with different instruments, in different facilities, get the same answer. Reproducibility is a claim about generalizability. In professional laboratory practice — and in ASME and ISO measurement standards — both are formally defined and both matter for establishing the credibility of an experimental result. In this course, most of your work will assess repeatability, because we are working in a single lab environment. But I want you to know that reproducibility is the higher standard — it is the one that matters when someone who was not in this room needs to trust your result.

    slide 9: The Three-Way Distinction in Practice (Image: A three-row decision flowchart. Row 1: "I observe scatter in my repeated measurements." Three labeled branches emerge. Branch A: "Does the scatter scale with N (reduce when averaging)?" → "Random error dominates — take more measurements." Branch B: "Is the scatter consistent with my instrument spec?" → "Within spec — characterize and report it as uncertainty." Branch C: "Is there a consistent offset from the expected value regardless of N?" → "Systematic error — find the source, correct or bound it." All three branches converge on a final node: "State your result as: Estimate ± Uncertainty (confidence level, method)." Caption: "The distinction is diagnostic — it tells you what action to take.") Talking Point: Let me pull this all together with a decision framework you can use when examining your own data. When you observe scatter in repeated measurements, the first question is what kind of variation it is. If the scatter reduces as you take more measurements, you have a random-error-dominated system — averaging helps. If the scatter is consistent with your instrument's datasheet specification, it is within expectation — characterize it and propagate it as uncertainty. If there is a consistent offset from the expected value that does not go away with more measurements, you have a systematic error — stop taking data and find the source. All three scenarios end in the same place: you quantify your uncertainty and express your result as an engineering claim. But the path to that destination depends on correctly diagnosing what you are seeing. This is the skill we build in the lab work over the next several weeks.

    slide 12: Closure Error is what's left over after you think you've thought of everything. Carl Richards meant that as a caution about overconfidence — about the gap between what you planned for and what actually happened. In experimental engineering, that gap has a name: uncertainty. And the discipline of this course is learning how to quantify that gap honestly, propagate it through your analysis rigorously, and use it to make defensible engineering claims. Today you have the vocabulary — variability, random error, systematic error, uncertainty, confidence interval, repeatability, reproducibility.
