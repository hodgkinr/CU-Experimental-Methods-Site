Engineering Standards, Handbooks & Prior Art

Introduces the professional ecosystem of engineering standards, measurement handbooks, and prior literature that experimental aerospace engineers draw on. ASME V&V 10-2006 is named explicitly as the primary required reference alongside domain-specific standards — AIAA measurement standards, ASME PTC measurement uncertainty standards, ASTM measurement standards, and relevant MIL-HDBK documents. This lecture establishes ASME V&V 10-2006 as the formal standards anchor that has been cited since Week 1 and is now a required citation in E3 design work. Students also practice locating a standard relevant to a sample measurement problem during the lecture session. Prior literature is covered: what a standard specifies and what it does not, how handbooks provide measurement method recommendations and uncertainty estimates, and how prior publications establish what is already known.

Learning Objectives

  • Apply formal experimental design vocabulary — independent and dependent variables, replication, randomization, blocking, control, and confounding factors — to an aerospace measurement problem — CLO 1
  • State a formal hypothesis that defines what a proposed experiment is expected to show, what data would confirm it, and what data would challenge it — CLO 1, CLO 3
  • Identify and cite ASME V&V 10-2006 and at least one additional relevant engineering standard, handbook, or prior publication applicable to their E3 design challenge — CLO 1, CLO 6
  • Describe what is already established in prior literature about their measurement domain and articulate what gap or question their experiment addresses — CLO 6

    slide 1: Title Slide Title: Engineering Standards, Handbooks & Prior Art Talking Point: In the last lecture we talked about how to structure an experimental design. Now we add the professional layer: standards, handbooks, and prior work. Engineering design is not supposed to start from a blank page, and experimental design is no different.

    slide 2: Why Standards Matter (Image: A technical design desk with three stacked reference sources: a standards document, a measurement handbook, and a journal article. A student design sketch sits beside them with arrows pulling requirements and assumptions from each source.) Talking Point: Standards are one way the profession remembers what it has already learned. They do not design your experiment for you, but they tell you what vocabulary, assumptions, tolerances, and documentation expectations other engineers will recognize. When you cite a standard, you are saying that your design is not just personally plausible. You are anchoring it in a shared professional framework.

    slide 3: Choose the Standard That Fits the Claim (Image: A V&V plan connected to three candidate sources: ASME PTC 19.1/GUM for measurement uncertainty, ASME V&V 10 for computational solid mechanics, and ASME V&V 20 for CFD and heat transfer. Each arrow is labeled "relevance must be explained.") Talking Point: No single standard is the automatic anchor for every E3 project. PTC 19.1 and the GUM support measurement-uncertainty practice. ASME V&V 10-2006 is the legacy computational solid-mechanics guide; ASME V&V 20 applies to computational fluid dynamics and heat transfer. Choose the document that fits your predictive model, measurement domain, and intended use. Annotate what it contributes and what it does not establish.

    slide 4: Domain-Specific Standards (Image: Four labeled shelves in a technical library: AIAA for aerospace practices, ASME PTC for performance testing and uncertainty, ASTM for measurement procedures and material tests, and MIL-HDBK for defense/aerospace handbook guidance. Each shelf has a callout showing an example measurement domain.) Talking Point: The right standard depends on the measurement domain. A structural test, a thermal test, a flow measurement, and a vibration measurement may all need different references. AIAA, ASME PTC, ASTM, and MIL-HDBK documents can each matter depending on the experiment. Your task is not to cite every possible standard. Your task is to find the few references that actually constrain or inform your measurement problem.

    slide 5: What a Standard Does and Does Not Tell You (Image: A two-column diagram. Left column labeled "Standards often specify" with bullets shown visually as icons: definitions, reporting requirements, uncertainty practices, calibration expectations. Right column labeled "Standards usually do not specify" with icons for your exact test fixture, your exact sensor choice, and your exact acceptance criterion.) Talking Point: A standard can tell you how a quantity should be defined, how uncertainty should be reported, or what calibration documentation is expected. It usually will not tell you exactly how to build your undergraduate test setup. That gap is where engineering judgment lives. The standard gives boundaries and precedent, but you still have to connect it to your model, constraints, and intended use.

    slide 6: Handbooks as Design Companions (Image: An open engineering handbook with annotated tables for sensor accuracy, uncertainty propagation, and recommended test practice. A magnifying glass highlights a table row, and a margin note says "use as rationale, not as decoration.") Talking Point: Handbooks are often more directly useful than standards when you are making design choices. They can provide typical uncertainty values, recommended measurement practices, or examples of how engineers handle a common test problem. Coleman and Steele, for example, are useful for uncertainty analysis practice because they connect measurement decisions to uncertainty propagation. A good handbook citation can justify why your method is reasonable and what limitations you should expect.

    slide 7: Prior Art (Image: A citation map with several prior papers clustered around a proposed student experiment. Some links are labeled known method, known limitation, typical sensor, and unresolved question. The student's proposed design sits at the center as a new node.) Talking Point: Prior art means the existing body of work around your measurement problem. It might be peer-reviewed papers, technical reports, manufacturer application notes, or prior course examples if they are technically relevant. Prior art helps you answer three questions: what is already known, what method has worked before, and what gap remains. If your proposal ignores prior art, it risks rediscovering a known problem or repeating a weak method.

    slide 8: Locating a Relevant Reference (Image: A search workflow diagram showing "measurement quantity" plus "system domain" plus "uncertainty/calibration/standard" feeding into databases, standards libraries, and manufacturer datasheets. Example query chips include "pressure transducer calibration uncertainty" and "strain gauge mounting effects.") Talking Point: Start with the physical quantity you need to measure and the context in which it is measured. Then search for standards, handbooks, and papers that connect that quantity to calibration, uncertainty, or test practice. A vague search like "best sensor for vibration" will produce shopping advice. A focused search like "accelerometer mounting transverse sensitivity vibration measurement standard" is much more likely to produce engineering evidence.

    slide 9: Annotated Citations (Image: A sample annotated citation card with fields for source, what it establishes, how it applies to the proposed experiment, and what it does not answer. The "how it applies" field is highlighted.) Talking Point: For E3, citation alone is not enough. I want annotated citations because annotation forces you to explain how the reference applies to your design. "This standard discusses validation" is weak. "This standard motivates our use of an intended-use statement and validation metric before data collection" is useful. The annotation is where you show that you understand the source rather than merely found it.

    slide 10: In-Class Practice Reference Hunt (Image: A classroom exercise board with a sample problem: "Measure wing-tip deflection under load to validate a beam model." Around it are blank boxes for standard, handbook, prior paper, sensor datasheet, and design implication.) Talking Point: We will practice with a sample measurement problem before you work on your own. The goal is to locate one standards-type reference, one handbook or technical guide, and one prior-art source that changes the design conversation. I am less interested in perfect citations today than in seeing the habit form. A professional experimentalist asks what the field already knows before proposing a measurement plan.

    slide 11: Closure Standards, handbooks, and prior art are not ornamental references at the end of a report. They shape the design by defining terms, identifying accepted practices, warning about known failure modes, and supporting your uncertainty and validation logic. In your preliminary E3 proposal, at least one relevant standard or professional guide and one additional technical source must appear for a stated reason. Next week we turn those design claims into instrumentation choices, where the same rule applies: every choice needs a technical rationale.
