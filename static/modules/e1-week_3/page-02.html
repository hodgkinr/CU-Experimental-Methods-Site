    slide 1: Title Slide Title: Calibration vs. Validation — A Distinction That Matters Talking Point: I want to discuss two words that get conflated constantly in engineering practice — calibration and validation. Both sound like they are about making sure something is right. Both involve comparing a result to some reference. But they are asking completely different questions about completely different things, and conflating them can produce conclusions that are not just imprecise but genuinely wrong. By the end of this lecture I want these two words to mean something precise and distinct to you. And in the second half, I will show you something that sounds simple but has real professional consequences: before you touch hardware, you should commit not just to what the right result looks like, but to what the wrong result looks like — and why.

    ================================================================================

    slide 2: Two Words — Two Different Questions (Image: A split-panel diagram with a bold vertical divider. Left panel header: "Calibration" — wrench icon. Large centered text: "Is the sensor reporting the right value?" Sub-items: "Activity: adjust sensor output to match a known reference." "Domain: the measurement instrument." "Output: a corrected sensor reading." Right panel header: "Validation" — target-and-arrow icon. Large centered text: "Is the model making accurate predictions?" Sub-items: "Activity: compare model predictions to independent experimental outcomes." "Domain: the mathematical model." "Output: a defensible claim about model fidelity." Below the divider: bold text "Calibration is a measurement activity. Validation is a modeling activity. Neither is a substitute for the other.") Talking Point: Here is the cleanest statement of the distinction. Calibration asks whether the sensor is reporting the right value — it is a question about the instrument. Validation asks whether the model is making accurate predictions — it is a question about the model. Calibration involves comparing sensor output to a known reference standard and correcting for any systematic offset. Validation involves comparing model predictions to experimental measurements that were taken independently — not used to fit the model. The two activities are structurally similar: both involve comparing two quantities. But the things being evaluated are completely different. Calibrating your sensor improves your measurement. It tells you nothing about whether your model of the physical phenomenon is correct. Calibration is a prerequisite for credible validation — you need to trust your measurement before you use it to test a model — but it is not a substitute for validation.

    slide 3: Calibration — What It Is (Image: A laboratory setup diagram. A known standard mass is hanging from a load cell on a rigid test stand. The mass is labeled "Reference Force: 9.81 N (traceable to NIST)." A digital readout shows the sensor voltage. To the right, a calibration curve plot: X-axis "Reference Force (N)," Y-axis "Sensor Voltage (V)." Data points from multiple known loads are shown, with a best-fit line and slight scatter. The correction equation is displayed: "Force (N) = 8.42 × Voltage + 0.23." Residuals are shown in a small inset below the calibration curve. Caption: "Calibration adjusts the sensor output to match a known standard. The calibration curve is itself a model with its own uncertainty.") Talking Point: Calibration is the process of characterizing a sensor's response to known inputs and generating a correction equation. To calibrate a load cell, you apply known reference forces traceable to a national standard — NIST-traceable weights, for example — record the sensor's voltage output at each force level, and fit a calibration curve to map voltage to force. After calibration, you apply that equation to every future measurement. The calibration residuals — the differences between your fitted curve and the actual data points — characterize the sensor's nonlinearity and the quality of your calibration fit. An important nuance: the calibration curve is itself a model. It is an approximation of the sensor's true response. It has uncertainty — both from scatter in the reference measurements and from the adequacy of the functional form you chose. That calibration uncertainty propagates into every subsequent measurement you make, and it is part of your total uncertainty budget.

    slide 4: Validation — What It Is (Image: The AMVF flowchart with the two-branch structure shown clearly. Left branch labeled "Mathematical Modeling Branch — drag coefficient prediction from aerodynamic model: Cd = f(α, Re)." Right branch labeled "Physical Modeling Branch — drag coefficient measured independently in wind tunnel at multiple angles of attack." At the Quantitative Comparison node, both branches meet: a graph shows the model prediction curve (dashed line) and experimental data points with uncertainty bars, overlaid. The comparison is annotated: "Validation: does the model predict Cd correctly within uncertainty bounds, across the tested range of α?" A gold annotation arrow points to "independent" with emphasis — "measurements not used to tune model parameters." A red X through a separate small diagram showing "model parameters tuned to match these same data — NOT validation.") Talking Point: Validation asks whether your mathematical model correctly predicts the behavior of the physical system across the domain of interest. The key structural requirement is independence: the experimental measurements used for validation must not have been used to fit the model's parameters. If you tuned your model to match your data, and then compare the model to that same data and report agreement, you have not validated — you have fit. Fitting a model to data and then checking how well the model fits that same data is circular, not validating. Validation requires a hold-out: data that the model has never seen, from conditions the model was not tuned to reproduce. Only then does agreement between prediction and measurement constitute evidence of model predictive validity. This is not a technicality — it is the logical structure that makes validation meaningful.

    slide 5: The Error Pattern — Treating Calibration as Validation (Image: A two-step scenario diagram. Step 1: "Engineer calibrates load cell against reference conditions. Calibrated measurements now agree with model predictions at those conditions." A checkmark: "Sensor is more accurate." Step 2: "Engineer reports: 'Model is validated — calibrated measurements agree with model predictions.'" A large red X with the annotation: "This is not validation. The model was never tested against independent data. The calibration and model tuning may both be internally consistent with each other — this is not evidence that either correctly represents the physics outside the calibrated range." A final note: "The pattern appears constantly. Name it when you see it.") Talking Point: Here is the error pattern I want you to recognize. An engineer calibrates their sensor against a set of reference conditions. The calibrated measurements agree with the model predictions at those conditions. The engineer concludes: the model is validated. This is wrong. The calibration adjusted the sensor to match the reference values. The model predictions may have been used to select or tune the calibration conditions. Whether intentionally or not, the calibration and the model tuning are not independent — they may both be consistent with each other without either one correctly representing the physics at conditions that were not tested. When you see the phrase "our calibrated results validate our model" in a report, ask: were the measurements used for calibration the same measurements used to fit the model? If yes, nothing has been validated.

    slide 6: Predicting What Right Looks Like (Image: A simple force-displacement graph for a spring-mass system. X-axis: "Displacement (mm)." Y-axis: "Force (N)." A solid straight diagonal line labeled "Expected model response: F = kx (linear)." Two uncertainty bands flank the line — one for measurement uncertainty (gray shading) and one for model parameter uncertainty (lighter outer shading). Annotations: "Slope = k (spring constant)" and "Expected scatter: within these bounds for any individual measurement." A caption: "Before touching hardware: commit to what the expected data signature looks like — the slope, the linearity, the magnitude, and the noise floor.") Talking Point: One of the habits I want you to develop before the end of this course is this: before you run an experiment, commit to what the right result looks like — specifically. Not "force increases with displacement," but "force increases linearly with displacement at a slope equal to k, with scatter bounded by the propagated uncertainty from my sensor spec." The sketch on this slide is what that commitment looks like. You have drawn the expected data signature, including the expected noise floor, before any hardware has been touched. This sketch is worth something — it is a commitment that can be evaluated after the fact. And it has a corollary that is just as important: if you can describe what right looks like, you should also be able to describe what wrong looks like.

    slide 7: Predicting What Wrong Looks Like — Anomalous Data Signatures (Image: Three side-by-side force-displacement plots for the same spring-mass system. Plot A (left): "Expected — scatter around a straight line. Scatter is symmetric, consistent with measurement noise." Dots scattered around the model line within the uncertainty band. Plot B (center): "Anomalous — hysteresis loop. Loading and unloading traces follow different paths, forming a closed loop." Labeled: "This is not noise — it is a systematic pattern that the linear model cannot explain. Evidence of energy dissipation (damping, friction) not in the model." Plot C (right): "Anomalous — nonlinearity at large displacement. Slope increases at large displacement." Labeled: "Model assumes constant k. Data shows k is displacement-dependent at large deflection — geometric nonlinearity or material yielding." Caption: "You can predict which anomalous signatures are possible — if you understand your model's assumptions.") Talking Point: Here is the key insight of this lecture. If you understand your model's assumptions, you can predict not just what the expected data looks like, but what specific anomalous patterns would indicate model-form failure — failure that is not measurement noise, but a sign that the model is fundamentally wrong for these conditions. For a linear spring model, the expected signature is scatter around a straight line within the propagated uncertainty. But there are specific signatures that would tell you the model is wrong. Hysteresis — different loading and unloading paths — is evidence of energy dissipation: the model does not include damping or friction, so it cannot produce a loop. Nonlinearity at large displacement is evidence of material or geometric effects the model treats as negligible. These anomalous signatures are predictable in advance if you understand what assumptions your model is making. Commit to them before you run the experiment. Then, if you see them in your data, you will know exactly what physical effect they represent — and you will not mistake them for measurement noise.

    slide 8: Scatter vs. Anomaly — The Diagnostic Distinction (Image: Two plots shown side by side, both using the same force-displacement setup. Left plot: "Measurement Noise — scatter." Multiple data points scatter around the model line, some above and some below, with no systematic pattern. The scatter is bounded by the propagated uncertainty band (gray shading). Label: "This is consistent with random measurement error. No model revision required." Right plot: "Model-Form Error — hysteresis." The data forms a distinct loading and unloading loop that is clearly outside the uncertainty band and clearly systematic. Label: "This is NOT measurement noise. The uncertainty band does not explain this pattern. The model is missing an energy dissipation term." A central annotation: "The distinguishing feature is structure — systematic patterns are model-form errors. Random scatter within the uncertainty band is measurement noise.") Talking Point: The distinction between scatter and anomaly is the practical form of the calibration-versus-validation distinction. Scatter that is bounded by your propagated uncertainty band is consistent with measurement noise — random, symmetric, not systematic. It tells you your measurement process is working as expected. A pattern that is systematic — like hysteresis, or a trending residual, or data that falls consistently above or below the model across a range of conditions — is not measurement noise. It cannot be explained by your sensor spec. It is evidence that the model is missing a physical effect. The uncertainty band is your null hypothesis: data within it is consistent with your model being adequate. Data outside it, in a systematic way, is evidence that the model form is wrong. Commit to this diagnostic before the experiment, and you will recognize a model-form failure immediately when you see it in your data.

    slide 11: Closure Calibration and validation are both essential, and they are not the same. Calibration improves the accuracy of your measurement by correcting for sensor bias — it is a measurement activity. Validation tests the predictive fidelity of your model by comparing independent measurements to model predictions — it is a modeling activity. Conflating them produces false confidence in models that have not actually been tested. And the extension of this idea — committing before an experiment to what both the expected and the anomalous results look like — is the discipline that transforms experiment design from observation to hypothesis testing. When you know what wrong looks like before you start, you will recognize it when you see it, and you will be able to name what it means. That is the skill you need for the Phase 2 Prediction Memo. That is the skill you need for every validation activity in the rest of this course.
